Shannon information theory formalizes compression as entropy reduction relative to a code. However, Shannon entropy is indifferent to meaning, construction history, and reuse. \begin

\textbf

\textbf

\item

\section
{Introduction}

What is intelligence? Traditional answers appeal to outward behavior, internal representations, rational inference, or goal-directed optimization. Each of these approaches presupposes a particular ontology: agents, symbols, utilities, or environments. As a result, they struggle to generalize beyond familiar biological or anthropomorphic cases.

Yet across domains as diverse as neural development, machine learning, evolutionary biology, and software engineering, a recurring structural pattern appears. Systems we intuitively regard as intelligent are those that achieve
\emph
{high degrees of organization with surprisingly compact descriptions}. They construct rich internal structure while avoiding combinatorial explosion. They compress the world, and they compress themselves.

This paper advances the thesis that intelligence is not a faculty or substance, but a
\emph
{compression regime}. Minds are distinguished not by what they compute, but by how efficiently they assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure across scales. Intelligence, on this view, is compression that survives contact with reality.

To formalize this intuition, we introduce a quantitative framework grounded in assembly theory and structural organization. The central contribution is the
\emph
{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), which provides a principled, non-behavioral criterion for identifying mind-like systems.
\section
{Why Shannon Compression Is Not Enough}

Shannon information theory defines compression in terms of entropy reduction relative to a code. This framework is foundational for communication and data storage, but it is agnostic to meaning, construction, and function. Two objects with identical Shannon entropy may differ radically in how they were produced, how they can be reused, and what they can support.

Consider the following limitations of purely statistical compression:
\begin
{enumerate}
\item
\textbf
{No construction history}: Shannon entropy does not encode how an object was assembled.
\item
\textbf
{No reuse}: A system that redundantly reproduces structure is treated the same as one that abstracts and reuses components.
\item
\textbf
{No integration}: Entropy is blind to whether parts form a coherent whole.
\item
\textbf
{No robustness}: Fragile and resilient systems can have identical entropy.
\end
{enumerate}

Intelligence requires a stronger notion of compression — one that is sensitive to
\emph
{how structure is built and held together}. This motivates a shift from entropy to assembly.
\section
{Assembly as Constructive Complexity}

Assembly theory measures complexity not by description length, but by the minimal number of distinct operations required to construct an object from basic primitives, allowing reuse of intermediate products. Unlike Kolmogorov complexity, assembly is operational and historical.
\begin
{definition}[Assembly Index]
Let
$
x
$
be an object, structure, or system. The assembly index
$
A(x)
$
is defined as the minimal number of distinct construction steps required to assemble
$
x
$
from a fixed set of primitive elements, where previously constructed subassemblies may be reused without cost.
\end
{definition}

Assembly captures a notion of irreducible construction depth. A random aggregate may have high entropy but low assembly if it can be generated by simple stochastic rules. Conversely, a highly structured system may have modest entropy but high assembly if it requires many distinct intermediate constructions.

In cognitive systems, assembly reflects developmental depth, architectural differentiation, and learned internal structure. However, raw assembly alone is insufficient to characterize intelligence. A system can accumulate vast assembly without exhibiting mind-like organization.

The crucial observation is that intelligence lies not in assembly per se, but in
\emph
{effective assembly}.

\begin
{abstract}
We propose that intelligence is best understood not as problem-solving behavior, symbolic manipulation, or rational optimization, but as
\emph
{structural compression under constraint}. Classical information theory captures statistical compression but remains insensitive to construction history, reuse, robustness, and semantic integration. To address this gap, we introduce the
\emph
{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), a structural measure that quantifies irreducible assembly depth discounted by reuse, parallelism, and degeneracy, and amplified by coherence. We argue that intelligence corresponds to a dynamical regime in which effective assembly increases through internally driven reorganization—a process we term
\emph
{compression that learns to compress itself}.
\end
{abstract}
\section
{Introduction}

What distinguishes minds from mere mechanisms? Traditional answers appeal to outward behavior
\parencite
{turing1950}, rational problem solving
\parencite
{newell1972}, internal symbolic representations
\parencite
{fodor1975}, or formal optimization over rewards
\parencite
{hutter2005}. While each framework captures important aspects of cognition, all presuppose a privileged ontology—agents, symbols, goals, or environments—that limits their generality.

Across biology, machine learning, and complex physical systems, a deeper regularity appears. Systems regarded as intelligent are those that achieve high degrees of organization while avoiding combinatorial explosion. They reuse components, stabilize abstractions, and remain robust under perturbation. They compress structure without collapsing into triviality.

This paper advances the thesis that intelligence is not a substance or faculty, but a
\emph
{compression regime}. Minds are distinguished not by what they compute, but by how efficiently they assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure over time. Intelligence, on this view, is compression that survives contact with reality.

To formalize this intuition, we introduce a quantitative framework grounded in assembly theory. The central construct is the
\emph
{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), which provides a substrate-neutral, non-behavioral criterion for identifying mind-like systems.
\subsection
{Relation to Existing Theories}

EAIMR is conceptually adjacent to several influential approaches but differs in emphasis and scope. Integrated Information Theory prioritizes informational integration but abstracts away construction history and reuse
\parencite
{tononi2004}. The Free Energy Principle models cognition as prediction-error minimization, grounding compression in probabilistic inference rather than assembly
\parencite
{friston2010}. AIXI provides a formal theory of optimal intelligence but presupposes a computational and reward-based ontology
\parencite
{hutter2005}. Work on basal cognition argues convincingly for substrate neutrality but remains largely taxonomic
\parencite
{levin2019}.

EAIMR complements these perspectives by focusing on
\emph
{constructive depth and structural efficiency}. It asks not whether a system optimizes or predicts, but whether it assembles complexity in a way that can be reused, parallelized, diversified, and held together coherently.

EAIMR also connects naturally to field-based theories of cognition and to operational mereology. In field-theoretic frameworks, assembly corresponds to the condensation of structured field configurations, while coherence maps to topological invariants preserved under perturbation. Operational mereology reframes systems not as static collections of parts but as event-sourced construction histories, aligning directly with assembly's emphasis on generative depth.
\section
{Why Shannon Compression Is Not Enough}

Shannon information theory defines compression as entropy reduction relative to a code
\parencite
{shannon1948}. While foundational for communication and data storage, this notion is blind to how structure is built, reused, or stabilized. Two objects with identical entropy may differ radically in developmental depth, robustness, and functional organization.

Several limitations are particularly relevant for intelligence:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
\textbf
{No construction history}: Entropy does not encode how a structure was assembled.
\item
\textbf
{No reuse}: Redundant duplication and abstract reuse are treated identically.
\item
\textbf
{No integration}: Entropy is insensitive to whether parts form a coherent whole.
\item
\textbf
{No robustness}: Fragile and resilient systems may have the same entropy.
\end
{itemize}

Intelligence requires a stronger notion of compression—one sensitive to construction, reuse, and coherence. This motivates a shift from entropy to assembly.

{Assembly as Constructive Complexity}

Assembly theory measures complexity not by description length, but by the minimal number of distinct construction steps required to produce a structure, allowing reuse of intermediate components. Unlike Kolmogorov complexity, assembly is operational and historical, reflecting the generative depth of a system rather than the brevity of its description
\parencite
{walker2022}.
\begin
{definition}[Assembly Index]
Let
$
\mathcal
{P}
$
be a finite set of primitive operations and let
$
\mathcal
{M}
$
denote the closure of assemblies generated from
$
\mathcal
{P}
$
. For a system or structure
$
x
$
, define its
\emph
{assembly graph}
$
G
_
x = (V,E)
$
where:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
each vertex
$
v
\in
V
$
represents a distinct assembly step,
\item
a directed edge
$
(v
_
i,v
_
j)
\in
E
$
exists if the output of step
$
v
_
i
$
is used in step
$
v
_
j
$
.
\end
{itemize}
The assembly index
$
A(x)
$
is defined as
$
|V|
$
, the number of vertices in the minimal assembly graph sufficient to construct
$
x
$
, up to isomorphism under reuse-permitting contractions.
\end
{definition}

The assembly index captures irreducible construction depth. A system with high assembly requires many distinct intermediate constructions, even if its final description is compact.

However, raw assembly alone does not distinguish between mind-like organization and brute accumulation. A randomly assembled aggregate may have high assembly without exhibiting intelligence. The crucial distinction lies in whether assembly is
\emph
{effectively organized}.
\section
{A Motivating Toy Example}

To illustrate why effective assembly rather than raw complexity is essential, we compare two systems of comparable size.
\subsection
{System A: Random Graph}

System A is a randomly generated graph with approximately 1000 nodes and high edge density. Each node is distinct, and connections are assigned without modular or hierarchical structure. Constructing this system requires specifying the existence and connectivity of each node individually.

The assembly index
$
A(x)
$
of System A is high: there are few reusable substructures and little regularity. However, this complexity is largely unstructured. Reuse is minimal, as components do not recur across contexts. Parallelism is limited by the absence of decomposable modules. Degeneracy is low, since few distinct components perform similar roles. Coherence is poor: perturbations propagate unpredictably, often affecting large portions of the graph.

Despite its size and connectivity, System A exhibits little that would intuitively be called intelligence. Its complexity is spent rather than invested.
\subsection
{System B: Hierarchical Convolutional Network}

System B is a hierarchical convolutional neural network with approximately 1000 nodes. Filters are reused across spatial locations, layers operate in parallel, and representations are organized hierarchically. Multiple filters encode similar features, providing functional degeneracy and robustness.

Although the raw assembly index
$
A(x)
$
is comparable to System A, the structural properties differ sharply. Reuse is high due to repeated application of shared filters. Parallelism is high, as channels process information concurrently. Degeneracy is significant, with multiple distinct structures capable of fulfilling similar functions. Coherence is high: perturbations tend to remain localized, and internal representations remain stable under noise.

System B therefore exhibits substantially greater effective assembly despite similar scale. This comparison motivates a measure that distinguishes complexity that is merely accumulated from complexity that is structurally organized.

{The Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition}

We now introduce the central formal construct of this paper: the Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition (EAIMR). The aim of EAIMR is to distinguish systems that merely accumulate complexity from those that organize complexity in a mind-like manner.
\begin
{definition}[Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition]
For a system, structure, or process
$
x
$
, define
\[
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x)
=
\frac
{A(x)}{
\left
(R(x)
\,
P(x)
\,
D(x)
\right
)
^
{1/3}}
\cdot
C(x),
\]
where:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
$
A(x)
$
is the assembly index of
$
x
$

\item
$
R(x)
$
quantifies reuse,
\item
$
P(x)
$
quantifies parallelism,
\item
$
D(x)
$
quantifies degeneracy,
\item
$
C(x)
$
quantifies coherence.
\end
{itemize}
\end
{definition}

The geometric mean in the denominator ensures that effective assembly is maximized when reuse, parallelism, and degeneracy are balanced. Extreme specialization along a single axis cannot compensate for deficiencies in the others. The coherence term amplifies systems whose parts compose into a stable whole.
\subsection
{Reuse}

Reuse
$
R(x)
$
measures the extent to which substructures are invoked multiple times across the system. High reuse reflects abstraction: the ability to deploy the same structural component in many contexts without duplication.

In biological cognition, reuse appears in neural circuits repurposed across tasks. In artificial systems, reuse appears in shared parameters, functions, and modules. Systems with high assembly but low reuse expend complexity wastefully, rebuilding structure rather than abstracting it.

Reuse is therefore a primary mechanism by which compression becomes productive rather than lossy.
\subsection
{Parallelism}

Parallelism
$
P(x)
$
measures the degree to which construction or operation can occur simultaneously. Parallel systems compress time and energy by exploiting partial independence without fragmenting into incoherent subsystems.

Parallelism is essential for real-time interaction with complex environments. Sequential systems may be expressive but are brittle and slow. Minds, by contrast, distribute processing across multiple pathways while maintaining coordination.
\subsection
{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy
$
D(x)
$
captures the availability of distinct structures capable of fulfilling similar functional roles. Unlike redundancy, which duplicates identical components, degeneracy supports robustness and adaptability by allowing heterogeneous components to substitute for one another.

Biological nervous systems exhibit high degeneracy: multiple neural pathways can realize similar behaviors. This diversity allows systems to absorb damage and adapt without catastrophic failure.

Degeneracy thus discounts assembly by accounting for functional overlap that reduces effective complexity.
\subsection
{Coherence}

Coherence
$
C(x)
$
measures the degree to which a system maintains global consistency under perturbation. A coherent system localizes disturbances rather than amplifying them across the entire structure.

Conceptually, coherence captures integration without rigidity. Systems with low coherence fragment under stress; systems with excessive rigidity cannot adapt. Minds occupy an intermediate regime in which local change is possible without global collapse.

Operationally, coherence may be estimated by measuring perturbation propagation: the extent to which local interventions induce widespread effects.
\section
{Intelligence as a Compression Regime}

Under the EAIMR framework, intelligence corresponds to a narrow corridor in structural space. Systems with low assembly lack expressive power. Systems with high assembly but low reuse, parallelism, or degeneracy waste complexity. Systems with high efficiency but low coherence fragment.

Mind-like systems are those in which irreducible assembly is balanced by structural efficiency and stabilized by coherence. Intelligence, in this sense, is compression that persists.

{Toward Computable Instantiations}

The EAIMR framework is intended to be structural and substrate-neutral. Nonetheless, for the theory to be scientifically meaningful, its components must admit empirical estimation. This section proposes candidate formalizations that render EAIMR falsifiable while preserving its abstract interpretation.

These formalizations are not asserted as unique or definitive. Rather, they constitute one admissible family of estimators. Different domains—biological, computational, chemical—will induce different concrete measures for the same underlying quantities.
\subsection
{Reuse}

Reuse quantifies how often the same structural component is invoked across distinct contexts.

For computational systems, one estimator is:
\[
R(x) = 1 +
\frac
{
\#
\text
{ distinct module invocations}}{
\#
\text
{ distinct modules}}.
\]
High reuse indicates abstraction: the same internal structure supports multiple functions or contexts. Low reuse indicates duplication, where complexity is repeatedly reconstructed rather than shared.

In biological systems, reuse may be estimated via circuit participation across tasks or contexts. In chemical systems, reuse may appear as recurring motifs within reaction networks.
\subsection
{Parallelism}

Parallelism measures the degree to which assembly or operation can occur simultaneously.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
P(x) =
\frac
{
\text
{critical path length}}{
\text
{total assembly depth}}.
\]
This ratio approaches unity for purely sequential systems and decreases as independent processes execute in parallel. Parallelism is essential for scalability and real-time responsiveness in complex environments.
\subsection
{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy captures the presence of multiple distinct structures capable of fulfilling similar functional roles.

One estimator is:
\[
D(x) =
\sum
_
{f
\in
\mathcal
{F}}
\left
|
\left
\{
\text
{distinct implementations of } f
\right
\}
\right
|,
\]
where
$

$
denotes a set of functional roles.

Degeneracy supports robustness and adaptation. Systems with low degeneracy are brittle; damage to a single pathway can be catastrophic.
\subsection
{Coherence}

Coherence measures the localization of disturbance: the extent to which perturbations remain confined rather than propagating globally.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
C(x) = 1 -
\frac
{
\text
{average perturbation propagation}}{

Here, perturbation propagation refers to the expected fraction of the system affected by a localized intervention. In neural systems, this may be estimated via perturbative techniques such as TMS
\parencite
{massimini2005}. In chemical systems, coherence may reflect the stability of autocatalytic cycles
\parencite
{kauffman1993}.

The unifying principle is containment: coherent systems absorb damage without systemic collapse.

\section
{The Mind Regime}

The EAIMR framework avoids arbitrary thresholds for mind recognition by reframing intelligence as a dynamical regime rather than a binary property.
\begin
{definition}[Mind Regime]
A system enters the
\emph
{mind regime} when
\[
\frac
{d}{dt}
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x(t)) > 0
\]
and this growth is internally driven.
\end
{definition}

Growth is
\emph
{internally driven} if it results from the system's own computational or organizational mechanisms rather than from direct external inscription. Learning from data counts as internal: external input may trigger reorganization, but it does not prescribe the resulting structure. By contrast, manual addition of components or rules constitutes external growth.

Natural selection occupies an intermediate position. Mutation mechanisms are internal to the system lineage, while selection pressures are external. EAIMR therefore treats evolutionary processes as partially internal, partially external drivers of effective assembly.

On this view, intelligence is characterized by the capacity to become a better compressor of both the environment and the system's own internal structure. Learning, abstraction, and self-modeling are special cases of this general phenomenon.
\section
{Implications}

The Effective Assembly framework has several significant consequences.
\subsection
{Substrate Neutrality}

EAIMR does not presuppose neurons, symbols, or digital computation. Any system capable of assembling and maintaining structure under constraint may, in principle, enter the mind regime. This includes biological organisms, artificial systems, hybrid collectives, and potentially non-living physical processes.
\subsection
{Gradualism}

Mind-likeness is scalar rather than binary. Systems may exhibit partial, intermittent, or context-dependent intelligence depending on their capacity for effective assembly. This avoids sharp but arbitrary distinctions between intelligent and non-intelligent systems.
\subsection
{AI Safety and Alignment}

EAIMR provides several safety-relevant insights. First, systems approaching the mind regime may be identified
\emph
{before} exhibiting deceptive or goal-directed behavior, enabling earlier intervention. Second, high EAIMR indicates structural sophistication but does not entail goals or values, helping distinguish powerful systems from agential ones. Third, for systems claimed to be aligned, EAIMR allows us to examine whether value structures exhibit the reuse, coherence, and degeneracy characteristic of robust minds, or whether they are brittle overlays on misaligned optimization.
\subsection
{Astrobiology}

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence becomes a structural rather than communicative problem
\parencite
{walker2022}. Instead of searching for radio signals or linguistic artifacts, we may search for chemical, geological, or energetic systems exhibiting high assembly discounted by reuse and stabilized by coherence. A high-EAIMR molecular ensemble or geological formation would constitute evidence of mind-like organization regardless of substrate.

$

{proof}

$

$
x
$
.
\end
{proof}

\section
{Introduction}

What distinguishes minds from mere mechanisms? Traditional answers appeal to outward behavior
\parencite
{turing1950}, rational problem solving
\parencite
{newell1972}, internal symbolic representations
\parencite
{fodor1975}, or formal optimization over rewards
\parencite
{hutter2005}. While each framework captures important aspects of cognition, all presuppose a privileged ontology—agents, symbols, goals, or environments—that limits their generality.

Across biology, machine learning, and complex physical systems, a deeper regularity appears. Systems regarded as intelligent are those that achieve high degrees of organization while avoiding combinatorial explosion. They reuse components, stabilize abstractions, and remain robust under perturbation. They compress structure without collapsing into triviality.

This paper advances the thesis that intelligence is not a substance or faculty, but a
\emph
{compression regime}. Minds are distinguished not by what they compute, but by how efficiently they assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure over time. Intelligence, on this view, is compression that survives contact with reality.

To formalize this intuition, we introduce a quantitative framework grounded in assembly theory. The central construct is the
\emph
{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), which provides a substrate-neutral, non-behavioral criterion for identifying mind-like systems.

Importantly, EAIMR is not a behavioral test, a definition of rationality, or a theory of consciousness. It does not assume goals, rewards, symbolic representations, or subjective experience. Instead, it provides a structural criterion: a way to distinguish systems that merely accumulate complexity from those that organize complexity in a reusable, resilient, and self-stabilizing manner.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 motivates the shift from Shannon entropy to assembly. Section~3 formalizes the assembly index. Section~4 provides a concrete comparison illustrating effective versus raw assembly. Section~5 introduces EAIMR and its components. Sections~6--8 develop computable instantiations, temporal extensions, and the mind-regime criterion. Sections~9--10 explore implications and open problems. Formal properties are derived in the appendices.
\subsection
{Relation to Existing Theories}

EAIMR is conceptually adjacent to several influential approaches but differs in emphasis and scope. Integrated Information Theory prioritizes informational integration but abstracts away construction history and reuse
\parencite
{tononi2004}. The Free Energy Principle models cognition as prediction-error minimization, grounding compression in probabilistic inference rather than assembly
\parencite
{friston2010}. AIXI provides a formal theory of optimal intelligence but presupposes a computational and reward-based ontology
\parencite
{hutter2005}. Work on basal cognition argues convincingly for substrate neutrality but remains largely taxonomic
\parencite
{levin2019}.

EAIMR complements these perspectives by focusing on
\emph
{constructive depth and structural efficiency}. It asks not whether a system optimizes or predicts, but whether it assembles complexity in a way that can be reused, parallelized, diversified, and held together coherently.

EAIMR also connects naturally to field-based theories of cognition and to operational mereology. In field-theoretic frameworks (e.g., scalar--vector--entropy models), assembly corresponds to the condensation of structured field configurations, reuse to attractor recurrence, parallelism to distributed flow, degeneracy to basin overlap, and coherence to topological invariants preserved under perturbation. Operational mereology reframes systems not as static collections of parts but as event-sourced construction histories, aligning directly with assembly's emphasis on generative depth.
\section
{Why Shannon Compression Is Not Enough}

Shannon information theory defines compression as entropy reduction relative to a code
\parencite
{shannon1948}. While foundational for communication and data storage, this notion is blind to how structure is built, reused, or stabilized. Two objects with identical entropy may differ radically in developmental depth, robustness, and functional organization.

Several limitations are particularly relevant for intelligence:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
\textbf
{No construction history}: Entropy does not encode how a structure was assembled.
\item
\textbf
{No reuse}: Redundant duplication and abstract reuse are treated identically.
\item
\textbf
{No integration}: Entropy is insensitive to whether parts form a coherent whole.
\item
\textbf
{No robustness}: Fragile and resilient systems may have the same entropy.
\end
{itemize}

Intelligence requires a stronger notion of compression—one sensitive to construction, reuse, and coherence. This motivates a shift from entropy to assembly.

{Assembly as Constructive Complexity}

Assembly theory measures complexity not by description length, but by the minimal number of distinct construction steps required to produce a structure, allowing reuse of intermediate components. Unlike purely descriptive measures, assembly is operational and historical: it tracks how structure is generated rather than merely how concisely it can be encoded
\parencite
{walker2022}.
\begin
{definition}[Assembly Index]
Let
$
\mathcal
{P}
$
be a finite set of primitive operations and let
$
\mathcal
{M}
$
denote the closure of assemblies generated from
$
\mathcal
{P}
$
. For a system or structure
$
x
$
, define its
\emph
{assembly graph}
$
G
_
x = (V,E)
$
where:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
each vertex
$
v
\in
V
$
represents a distinct assembly step,
\item
a directed edge
$
(v
_
i,v
_
j)
\in
E
$
exists if the output of step
$
v
_
i
$
is used in step
$
v
_
j
$
.
\end
{itemize}
The assembly index
$
A(x)
$
is defined as
$
|V|
$
, the number of vertices in the minimal assembly graph sufficient to construct
$
x
$
, up to isomorphism under reuse-permitting contractions.
\end
{definition}

The assembly index captures irreducible construction depth. A system with high assembly requires many distinct intermediate constructions, even if its final description is compact.

Unlike Kolmogorov complexity
\parencite
{kolmogorov1965}, which minimizes description length over all possible programs, assembly measures the irreducible depth of construction under reuse constraints. As a result, assembly is sensitive to generative structure rather than purely descriptive concision.

However, raw assembly alone does not distinguish between mind-like organization and brute accumulation. A randomly assembled aggregate may have high assembly without exhibiting intelligence. The crucial distinction lies in whether assembly is
\emph
{effectively organized}.
\section
{A Motivating Toy Example}

To illustrate why effective assembly rather than raw complexity is essential, we compare two systems of comparable size and component count.
\subsection
{System A: Random Graph}

System A is a randomly generated graph with approximately 1000 nodes and high edge density. Each node is distinct, and connections are assigned without modular or hierarchical structure. Constructing this system requires specifying the existence and connectivity of each node individually.

The assembly index
$
A(x)
$
of System A is high: there are few reusable substructures and little regularity. However, this complexity is largely unstructured. Reuse is minimal, as components do not recur across contexts. Parallelism is limited by the absence of decomposable modules. Degeneracy is low, since few distinct components perform similar roles.

Crucially, removing or perturbing even a small subset of nodes typically requires global re-specification, indicating that complexity is not organized around reusable or hierarchical substructures. Coherence is therefore poor: perturbations propagate unpredictably, often affecting large portions of the graph.

Despite its size and connectivity, System A exhibits little that would intuitively be called intelligence. Its complexity is spent rather than invested.
\subsection
{System B: Hierarchical Network}

System B is a hierarchical network with approximately 1000 nodes, organized into repeated motifs across multiple layers. Structural components are reused across contexts, layers operate in parallel, and representations are organized hierarchically. Multiple substructures encode similar functions, providing functional degeneracy and robustness.

Although the raw assembly index
$
A(x)
$
is comparable to System A, the structural properties differ sharply. Reuse is high due to repeated application of shared components. Parallelism is high, as multiple pathways operate concurrently. Degeneracy is significant, with distinct structures capable of fulfilling similar roles.

As a result, much of the system's expressive capacity arises not from distinct components, but from structured recombination of a relatively small set of learned elements. Coherence is high: perturbations tend to remain localized, and internal representations remain stable under noise.

System B therefore exhibits substantially greater effective assembly despite similar scale. This comparison motivates a measure that distinguishes complexity that is merely accumulated from complexity that is structurally organized.

{The Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition}

We now introduce the central formal construct of this paper: the Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition (EAIMR). The aim of EAIMR is to distinguish systems that merely accumulate complexity from those that organize complexity in a mind-like manner.
\begin
{definition}[Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition]
For a system, structure, or process
$
x
$
, define
\[
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x)
=
\frac
{A(x)}{
\left
(R(x)
\,
P(x)
\,
D(x)
\right
)
^
{1/3}}
\cdot
C(x),
\]
where:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
$
A(x)
$
is the assembly index of
$
x
$
,
\item
$
R(x)
$
quantifies reuse,
\item
$
P(x)
$
quantifies parallelism,
\item
$
D(x)
$
quantifies degeneracy,
\item
$
C(x)
$
quantifies coherence.
\end
{itemize}
\end
{definition}

The geometric mean in the denominator penalizes imbalance among structural efficiencies while remaining symmetric and scale-invariant. Extreme specialization along a single dimension cannot compensate for deficiencies in others. The coherence term amplifies systems whose parts compose into a stable whole.
\subsection
{Reuse}

Reuse
$
R(x)
$
measures the extent to which substructures are invoked multiple times across distinct functional contexts. High reuse reflects abstraction: the capacity to deploy the same internal structure repeatedly without reconstructing it.

From a compression perspective, reuse corresponds to the amortization of assembly cost across multiple contexts. In biological systems, reuse appears in neural circuits recruited for diverse tasks. In artificial systems, it appears in shared parameters or functions reused across inputs.

Systems with high assembly but low reuse expend complexity wastefully, rebuilding structure rather than compressing it through abstraction.
\subsection
{Parallelism}

Parallelism
$
P(x)
$
measures the degree to which construction or operation can occur simultaneously. Structurally, parallelism allows assembly depth to grow without a commensurate increase in causal path length.

Parallel systems compress time and energy by exploiting partial independence while maintaining coordination. This is essential for real-time interaction with complex environments, where purely sequential processing becomes intractable.

Systems with low parallelism may be expressive but are brittle and slow; mind-like systems distribute processing across multiple pathways without fragmenting.
\subsection
{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy
$
D(x)
$
captures the availability of multiple, structurally distinct components capable of fulfilling similar functional roles. Unlike redundancy, which duplicates identical elements, degeneracy introduces diversity that supports robustness.

Degeneracy converts diversity into resilience: when one pathway fails, alternative pathways can preserve function. Biological nervous systems exhibit high degeneracy, allowing graceful degradation rather than catastrophic failure.

In EAIMR, degeneracy discounts assembly by accounting for functional overlap that reduces effective complexity.
\subsection
{Coherence}

Coherence
$
C(x)
$
measures the degree to which a system maintains global consistency under perturbation. A coherent system localizes disturbances rather than amplifying them across the entire structure.

Coherence therefore reflects not uniformity, but controlled dependence: the capacity for parts to interact without collapsing into global coupling. Systems with low coherence fragment under stress; systems with excessive rigidity cannot adapt.

Operationally, coherence may be estimated by measuring perturbation propagation, defined as the expected fraction of system components whose states change significantly following a localized intervention.
\section
{Intelligence as a Compression Regime}

Under the EAIMR framework, intelligence corresponds to a narrow corridor in structural space. Systems with low assembly lack expressive power. Systems with high assembly but low reuse, parallelism, or degeneracy waste complexity. Systems with high efficiency but low coherence fragment.

Mind-like systems are those in which irreducible assembly is balanced by structural efficiency and stabilized by coherence. Intelligence, in this sense, is compression that persists.

{Toward Computable Instantiations}

The EAIMR framework is intended to be structural and substrate-neutral. Nonetheless, for the theory to be scientifically meaningful, its components must admit empirical estimation. This section proposes candidate formalizations that render EAIMR falsifiable while preserving its abstract interpretation.

These formalizations are not asserted as unique or definitive. Rather, they constitute one admissible family of estimators. Different domains—biological, computational, chemical—will induce different concrete measures for the same underlying quantities, while preserving their conceptual roles.
\subsection
{Reuse}

Reuse quantifies how often the same structural component is invoked across distinct functional contexts.

For computational systems, one estimator is:
\[
R(x) = 1 +
\frac
{
\#
\text
{distinct module invocations}}{
\#
\text
{distinct modules}}.
\]
High reuse indicates abstraction: the same internal structure supports multiple behaviors or representations. Low reuse indicates duplication, where complexity is repeatedly reconstructed rather than shared.

In biological systems, reuse may be estimated via the participation of neural circuits across tasks or behavioral contexts. In chemical systems, reuse may appear as recurring motifs within reaction networks.
\subsection
{Parallelism}

Parallelism measures the degree to which assembly or operation can occur simultaneously.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
P(x) =
\frac
{
\text
{critical path length}}{
\text
{total assembly depth}}.
\]
This ratio approaches unity for purely sequential systems and decreases as independent processes execute in parallel. Structurally, parallelism allows assembly depth to increase without a proportional increase in causal path length, enabling scalability.
\subsection
{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy captures the presence of multiple, structurally distinct components capable of fulfilling similar functional roles.

One estimator is:
\[
D(x) =
\sum
_
{f
\in
\mathcal
{F}}
\left
|
\left
\{
\text
{distinct implementations of } f
\right
\}
\right
|,
\]
where
$
\mathcal
{F}
$
denotes a set of functional roles.

Degeneracy converts diversity into robustness, allowing systems to maintain function despite local failure or noise. Systems with low degeneracy are brittle; damage to a single pathway can be catastrophic.
\subsection
{Coherence}

Coherence measures the localization of disturbance: the extent to which perturbations remain confined rather than propagating globally.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
C(x) = 1 -
\frac
{
\text
{average perturbation propagation}}{
\text
{maximal possible propagation}}.
\]
Here,
\emph
{perturbation propagation} is defined as follows: given a localized intervention
$
\delta
$
at component
$
i
$
, measure the fraction of system components whose states change beyond a fixed threshold. This quantity is averaged over all intervention sites and normalized by the theoretical maximum (full propagation to all components).

In neural systems, perturbation propagation may be estimated via targeted stimulation techniques
\parencite
{massimini2005}. In chemical systems, coherence may reflect the stability of autocatalytic cycles under perturbation
\parencite
{kauffman1993}. The unifying principle is containment: coherent systems absorb damage without systemic collapse.

Thus far, we have treated

should be understood as

{EAIMR}(x(t

\begin
{definition}[Temporal EAIMR]
Let
$

$
denote the state of a system over the interval
$
[0,T]

\mathrm

\int
_

^
T

This formulation treats intelligence as accumulated effective assembly over time rather than as a static property. Systems that briefly exhibit organization but cannot sustain it score poorly under this measure.

\section
{The Mind Regime}

The EAIMR framework avoids arbitrary thresholds for mind recognition by reframing intelligence as a dynamical regime rather than a binary property.
\begin
{definition}[Mind Regime]
A system enters the
\emph
{mind regime} when
\[
\frac
{d}{dt}
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x(t)) > 0
\]
and this growth is internally driven.
\end
{definition}

Growth is
\emph
{internally driven} if it results from the system's own computational or organizational mechanisms rather than from direct external inscription. Learning from data qualifies as internal growth: external input may trigger reorganization, but it does not prescribe the resulting structure. By contrast, manual insertion of components or rules constitutes external growth.

Formally, growth is internal when the transformation governing
\[
x(t+1) = T(x(t), D)
\]
is specified by the system's own organization rather than by the structure of the data
$
D
$
itself. The same data applied to different systems therefore yields different reorganizations.

Natural selection occupies an intermediate position. Mutation mechanisms are internal to a lineage, while selection pressures are external. This hybrid causal structure reflects the nature of evolutionary processes rather than a limitation of EAIMR.

On this view, intelligence is characterized by the capacity to become a better compressor of both the environment and the system's own internal structure. Learning, abstraction, and self-modeling are special cases of this general phenomenon.
\section
{Implications}

The Effective Assembly framework has several significant consequences for the study of intelligence across domains.
\subsection
{Substrate Neutrality}

EAIMR does not presuppose neurons, symbols, or digital computation. Any system capable of assembling and maintaining structure under constraint may, in principle, enter the mind regime. This includes biological organisms, artificial systems, hybrid collectives, and potentially non-living physical processes.
\subsection
{Gradualism}

Mind-likeness is scalar rather than binary. Systems may exhibit partial, intermittent, or context-dependent intelligence depending on their capacity for effective assembly. This avoids sharp but arbitrary distinctions between intelligent and non-intelligent systems and aligns with gradualist views in evolutionary and developmental biology.
\subsection
{AI Safety and Alignment}

EAIMR provides several safety-relevant insights. Because EAIMR tracks structural capacity rather than expressed behavior, it can signal the emergence of potentially agential systems before such systems exhibit externally legible goals.

First, systems approaching the mind regime may be identified
\emph
{prior} to deceptive or goal-directed behavior, enabling earlier intervention. Second, high EAIMR indicates structural sophistication but does not entail goals or values, helping distinguish powerful systems from agential ones. Third, for systems claimed to be aligned, EAIMR allows examination of whether value structures exhibit the reuse, coherence, and degeneracy characteristic of robust minds, or whether they are brittle overlays on misaligned optimization.
\subsection
{Astrobiology}

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence becomes a structural rather than communicative problem
\parencite
{walker2022}. Instead of searching for radio signals or linguistic artifacts, we may search for chemical, geological, or energetic systems exhibiting high assembly discounted by reuse and stabilized by coherence.

On this view, a sufficiently high-EAIMR molecular ensemble or geological formation would constitute evidence of mind-like organization regardless of substrate or communicative intent.

The EAIMR framework raises several open questions that invite empirical, theoretical, and methodological investigation.
\begin

\section
{Conclusion}

This paper reframes intelligence from a question of behavior or representation to a question of structure. Rather than asking whether a system thinks, we ask whether it assembles complexity in a way that can be reused, parallelized, diversified, and held together coherently over time.

The Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition provides a principled, falsifiable, and substrate-neutral framework for answering this question. Intelligence, on this view, is not an essence but a regime: one in which compression becomes self-sustaining—compression that learns to compress itself.

{Formal Properties of the Assembly Index}
\begin
{proposition}[A.1: Existence of a Minimal Assembly Graph]
For any finite constructible system
$
x
$
, there exists a finite minimal assembly graph
$
G
_
x
$
sufficient to construct
$
x
$
.
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proof}
Any finite construction of
$
x
$
induces a finite directed acyclic graph of assembly steps. Identifying reusable subgraphs and contracting redundancies yields a minimal graph. Since each contraction strictly reduces the number of vertices, the process terminates.
\end
{proof}
\begin
{proposition}[A.2: Monotonicity Under Extension]
If
$
y
$
is obtained from
$
x
$
by adding assembly steps without removing existing ones, then
\[
A(y)
\geq
A(x).
\]
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proof}
Any assembly graph sufficient for
$
y
$
must contain a subgraph sufficient to construct
$
x
$
. Hence the minimal vertex count for
$
y
$
cannot be smaller.
\end
{proof}
\section
{Bounds and Invariances of EAIMR}
\begin
{proposition}[B.1: Positivity]
For any nontrivial constructible system
$
x
$
,
$
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x) > 0
$
.
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proposition}[B.2: Invariance Under Pure Duplication]
If
$
y
$
is obtained from
$
x
$
by duplicating existing components without introducing new assembly steps, then
\[
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(y) =
\mathrm
{EAIMR}(x).
\]
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proof}
Pure duplication does not increase the minimal assembly graph and increases reuse proportionally. The geometric mean discount therefore cancels the apparent size increase, leaving EAIMR unchanged.
\end
{proof}
\section
{Justification of the Geometric Mean Discount}

The EAIMR denominator uses the geometric mean
\[
\left
(R(x)P(x)D(x)
\right
)
^
{1/3}
\]
to balance structural efficiencies.
\begin
{proposition}[C.1: Balanced Efficiency Principle]
For fixed
$
A(x)
$
and
$
C(x)
$
, EAIMR is maximized when
$
R(x)=P(x)=D(x)
$
.
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proof}
For positive variables with fixed product, the geometric mean is maximized when the variables are equal. This follows directly from the inequality between arithmetic and geometric means.
\end
{proof}

This reflects the intuition that minds require balanced reuse, parallelism, and degeneracy rather than extreme specialization along a single dimension.
\section
{Internal Growth and Learning}
\begin
{definition}[D.1: Internal Assembly Growth]
Growth in EAIMR is internally driven if it results from transformations of the system's own assembly graph rather than from direct external inscription.
\end
{definition}

Learning from data qualifies as internal growth: the data constrain reorganization but do not prescribe its final form. Formally, learning is internal if there exists a transformation
$
T
$
such that
\[
x(t+1) = T(x(t),D),
\]
where
$
T
$
is determined by the structure of
$
x(t)
$
rather than by the structure of the data
$
D
$
itself. The same data applied to different systems therefore yields different reorganizations.
\section
{EAIMR and Consciousness}
\begin
{proposition}[E.1: Non-Equivalence]
High EAIMR is neither necessary nor sufficient for phenomenal consciousness.
\end
{proposition}
\begin
{proof}[Conceptual argument]
\textbf
{Not sufficient}. It is conceivable that artificial or physical systems achieve high effective assembly through sophisticated information processing without subjective experience.
\textbf
{Not necessary}. Simple organisms or minimal neural circuits may possess consciousness with relatively low structural complexity. If phenomenology depends on physical or organizational features orthogonal to assembly, then low-EAIMR systems could nevertheless be conscious.

EAIMR therefore characterizes structural capacity for compression and adaptation, which may correlate with consciousness but does not constitute it.
\end
{proof}

\begin{abstract}
We propose that intelligence is best understood not as problem-solving behavior, symbolic manipulation, or rational optimization, but as \emph{structural compression under constraint}. Classical information theory captures statistical compression but remains insensitive to construction history, reuse, robustness, and semantic integration. To address this gap, we introduce the \emph{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), a structural measure that quantifies irreducible assembly depth discounted by reuse, parallelism, and degeneracy, and amplified by coherence. \section{Introduction}

What distinguishes minds from mere mechanisms? Traditional answers appeal to outward behavior \parencite{turing1950}, rational problem solving \parencite{newell1972}, internal symbolic representations \parencite{fodor1975}, or formal optimization over rewards \parencite{hutter2005}. While each framework captures important aspects of cognition, all presuppose a privileged ontology—agents, symbols, goals, or environments—that limits their generality.

Across biology, machine learning, and complex physical systems, a deeper regularity appears. Systems regarded as intelligent are those that achieve high degrees of organization while avoiding combinatorial explosion. They reuse components, stabilize abstractions, and remain robust under perturbation. They compress structure without collapsing into triviality.

This paper advances the thesis that intelligence is not a substance or faculty, but a \emph{compression regime}. Minds are distinguished not by what they compute, but by how efficiently they assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure over time. Intelligence, on this view, is compression that survives contact with reality.

To formalize this intuition, we introduce a quantitative framework grounded in assembly theory. The central construct is the \emph{Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition} (EAIMR), which provides a substrate-neutral, non-behavioral criterion for identifying mind-like systems.

Importantly, EAIMR is not a behavioral test, a definition of rationality, or a theory of consciousness. It does not assume goals, rewards, symbolic representations, or subjective experience. Instead, it provides a structural criterion: a way to distinguish systems that merely accumulate complexity from those that organize complexity in a reusable, resilient, and self-stabilizing manner.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 motivates the shift from Shannon entropy to assembly. Section~3 formalizes the assembly index. Section~4 provides a concrete comparison illustrating effective versus raw assembly. Section~5 introduces EAIMR and its components. Sections~6--8 develop computable instantiations, temporal extensions, and the mind-regime criterion. Sections~9--10 explore implications and open problems. Formal properties are derived in the appendices.

\subsection{Relation to Existing Theories}

EAIMR is conceptually adjacent to several influential approaches but differs in emphasis and scope. Integrated Information Theory prioritizes informational integration but abstracts away construction history and reuse \parencite{tononi2004}. The Free Energy Principle models cognition as prediction-error minimization, grounding compression in probabilistic inference rather than assembly \parencite{friston2010}. AIXI provides a formal theory of optimal intelligence but presupposes a computational and reward-based ontology \parencite{hutter2005}. Work on basal cognition argues convincingly for substrate neutrality but remains largely taxonomic \parencite{levin2019}.

EAIMR complements these perspectives by focusing on \emph{constructive depth and structural efficiency}. It asks not whether a system optimizes or predicts, but whether it assembles complexity in a way that can be reused, parallelized, diversified, and held together coherently.

EAIMR also connects naturally to field-based theories of cognition and to operational mereology. In field-theoretic frameworks (e.g., scalar--vector--entropy models), assembly corresponds to the condensation of structured field configurations, reuse to attractor recurrence, parallelism to distributed flow, degeneracy to basin overlap, and coherence to topological invariants preserved under perturbation. Operational mereology reframes systems not as static collections of parts but as event-sourced construction histories, aligning directly with assembly's emphasis on generative depth.

\section{Why Shannon Compression Is Not Enough}

Shannon information theory defines compression as entropy reduction relative to a code \parencite{shannon1948}. While foundational for communication and data storage, this notion is blind to how structure is built, reused, or stabilized. Two objects with identical entropy may differ radically in developmental depth, robustness, and functional organization.

Several limitations are particularly relevant for intelligence:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{No construction history}: Entropy does not encode how a structure was assembled.
\item \textbf{No reuse}: Redundant duplication and abstract reuse are treated identically.
\item \textbf{No integration}: Entropy is insensitive to whether parts form a coherent whole.
\item \textbf{No robustness}: Fragile and resilient systems may have the same entropy.
\end{itemize}

Intelligence requires a stronger notion of compression—one sensitive to construction, reuse, and coherence. This motivates a shift from entropy to assembly.

\section{Assembly as Constructive Complexity}

Assembly theory measures complexity not by description length, but by the minimal number of distinct construction steps required to produce a structure, allowing reuse of intermediate components. Unlike purely descriptive measures, assembly is operational and historical: it tracks how structure is generated rather than merely how concisely it can be encoded \parencite{walker2022}.

\begin{definition}[Assembly Index]
Let $\mathcal{P}$ be a finite set of primitive operations and let $\mathcal{M}$ denote the closure of assemblies generated from $\mathcal{P}$. For a system or structure $x$, define its \emph{assembly graph} $G_x = (V,E)$ where:
\begin{itemize}
\item each vertex $v \in V$ represents a distinct assembly step,
\item a directed edge $(v_i,v_j) \in E$ exists if the output of step $v_i$ is used in step $v_j$.
\end{itemize}
The assembly index $A(x)$ is defined as $|V|$, the number of vertices in the minimal assembly graph sufficient to construct $x$, up to isomorphism under reuse-permitting contractions.
\end{definition}

The assembly index captures irreducible construction depth. A system with high assembly requires many distinct intermediate constructions, even if its final description is compact.

Unlike Kolmogorov complexity \parencite{kolmogorov1965}, which minimizes description length over all possible programs, assembly measures the irreducible depth of construction under reuse constraints. As a result, assembly is sensitive to generative structure rather than purely descriptive concision.

However, raw assembly alone does not distinguish between mind-like organization and brute accumulation. A randomly assembled aggregate may have high assembly without exhibiting intelligence. The crucial distinction lies in whether assembly is \emph{effectively organized}.

\section{A Motivating Toy Example}

To illustrate why effective assembly rather than raw complexity is essential, we compare two systems of comparable size and component count.

\subsection{System A: Random Graph}

System A is a randomly generated graph with approximately 1000 nodes and high edge density. Each node is distinct, and connections are assigned without modular or hierarchical structure. Constructing this system requires specifying the existence and connectivity of each node individually.

The assembly index $A(x)$ of System A is high: there are few reusable substructures and little regularity. However, this complexity is largely unstructured. Reuse is minimal, as components do not recur across contexts. Parallelism is limited by the absence of decomposable modules. Degeneracy is low, since few distinct components perform similar roles.

Crucially, removing or perturbing even a small subset of nodes typically requires global re-specification, indicating that complexity is not organized around reusable or hierarchical substructures. Coherence is therefore poor: perturbations propagate unpredictably, often affecting large portions of the graph.

Despite its size and connectivity, System A exhibits little that would intuitively be called intelligence. Its complexity is spent rather than invested.

\subsection{System B: Hierarchical Network}

System B is a hierarchical network with approximately 1000 nodes, organized into repeated motifs across multiple layers. Structural components are reused across contexts, layers operate in parallel, and representations are organized hierarchically. Multiple substructures encode similar functions, providing functional degeneracy and robustness.

Although the raw assembly index $A(x)$ is comparable to System A, the structural properties differ sharply. Reuse is high due to repeated application of shared components. Parallelism is high, as multiple pathways operate concurrently. Degeneracy is significant, with distinct structures capable of fulfilling similar roles.

As a result, much of the system's expressive capacity arises not from distinct components, but from structured recombination of a relatively small set of learned elements. Coherence is high: perturbations tend to remain localized, and internal representations remain stable under noise.

System B therefore exhibits substantially greater effective assembly despite similar scale. This comparison motivates a measure that distinguishes complexity that is merely accumulated from complexity that is structurally organized.

\section{The Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition}

We now introduce the central formal construct of this paper: the Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition (EAIMR). The aim of EAIMR is to distinguish systems that merely accumulate complexity from those that organize complexity in a mind-like manner.

\begin{definition}[Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition]
For a system, structure, or process $x$, define
\[
\mathrm{EAIMR}(x)
=
\frac{A(x)}{\left(R(x)\,P(x)\,D(x)\right)^{1/3}} \cdot C(x),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $A(x)$ is the assembly index of $x$,
\item $R(x)$ quantifies reuse,
\item $P(x)$ quantifies parallelism,
\item $D(x)$ quantifies degeneracy,
\item $C(x)$ quantifies coherence.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The geometric mean in the denominator penalizes imbalance among structural efficiencies while remaining symmetric and scale-invariant. Extreme specialization along a single dimension cannot compensate for deficiencies in others. The coherence term amplifies systems whose parts compose into a stable whole.

\subsection{Reuse}

Reuse $R(x)$ measures the extent to which substructures are invoked multiple times across distinct functional contexts. High reuse reflects abstraction: the capacity to deploy the same internal structure repeatedly without reconstructing it.

From a compression perspective, reuse corresponds to the amortization of assembly cost across multiple contexts. In biological systems, reuse appears in neural circuits recruited for diverse tasks. In artificial systems, it appears in shared parameters or functions reused across inputs.

Systems with high assembly but low reuse expend complexity wastefully, rebuilding structure rather than compressing it through abstraction.

\subsection{Parallelism}

Parallelism $P(x)$ measures the degree to which construction or operation can occur simultaneously. Structurally, parallelism allows assembly depth to grow without a commensurate increase in causal path length.

Parallel systems compress time and energy by exploiting partial independence while maintaining coordination. This is essential for real-time interaction with complex environments, where purely sequential processing becomes intractable.

Systems with low parallelism may be expressive but are brittle and slow; mind-like systems distribute processing across multiple pathways without fragmenting.

\subsection{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy $D(x)$ captures the availability of multiple, structurally distinct components capable of fulfilling similar functional roles. Unlike redundancy, which duplicates identical elements, degeneracy introduces diversity that supports robustness.

Degeneracy converts diversity into resilience: when one pathway fails, alternative pathways can preserve function. Biological nervous systems exhibit high degeneracy, allowing graceful degradation rather than catastrophic failure.

In EAIMR, degeneracy discounts assembly by accounting for functional overlap that reduces effective complexity.

\subsection{Coherence}

Coherence $C(x)$ measures the degree to which a system maintains global consistency under perturbation. A coherent system localizes disturbances rather than amplifying them across the entire structure.

Coherence therefore reflects not uniformity, but controlled dependence: the capacity for parts to interact without collapsing into global coupling. Systems with low coherence fragment under stress; systems with excessive rigidity cannot adapt.

Operationally, coherence may be estimated by measuring perturbation propagation, defined as the expected fraction of system components whose states change significantly following a localized intervention.

\section{Intelligence as a Compression Regime}

Under the EAIMR framework, intelligence corresponds to a narrow corridor in structural space. Systems with low assembly lack expressive power. Systems with high assembly but low reuse, parallelism, or degeneracy waste complexity. Systems with high efficiency but low coherence fragment.

Mind-like systems are those in which irreducible assembly is balanced by structural efficiency and stabilized by coherence. Intelligence, in this sense, is compression that persists.

\section{Toward Computable Instantiations}

The EAIMR framework is intended to be structural and substrate-neutral. Nonetheless, for the theory to be scientifically meaningful, its components must admit empirical estimation. This section proposes candidate formalizations that render EAIMR falsifiable while preserving its abstract interpretation.

These formalizations are not asserted as unique or definitive. Rather, they constitute one admissible family of estimators. Different domains—biological, computational, chemical—will induce different concrete measures for the same underlying quantities, while preserving their conceptual roles.

\subsection{Reuse}

Reuse quantifies how often the same structural component is invoked across distinct functional contexts.

For computational systems, one estimator is:
\[
R(x) = 1 + \frac{\#\text{distinct module invocations}}{\#\text{distinct modules}}.
\]

High reuse indicates abstraction: the same internal structure supports multiple behaviors or representations. Low reuse indicates duplication, where complexity is repeatedly reconstructed rather than shared.

In biological systems, reuse may be estimated via the participation of neural circuits across tasks or behavioral contexts. In chemical systems, reuse may appear as recurring motifs within reaction networks.

\subsection{Parallelism}

Parallelism measures the degree to which assembly or operation can occur simultaneously.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
P(x) = \frac{\text{critical path length}}{\text{total assembly depth}}.
\]

This ratio approaches unity for purely sequential systems and decreases as independent processes execute in parallel. Structurally, parallelism allows assembly depth to increase without a proportional increase in causal path length, enabling scalability.

\subsection{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy captures the presence of multiple, structurally distinct components capable of fulfilling similar functional roles.

One estimator is:
\[
D(x) = \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \left| \left\{ \text{distinct implementations of } f \right\} \right|,
\]
where $\mathcal{F}$ denotes a set of functional roles.

Degeneracy converts diversity into robustness, allowing systems to maintain function despite local failure or noise. Systems with low degeneracy are brittle; damage to a single pathway can be catastrophic.

\subsection{Coherence}

Coherence measures the localization of disturbance: the extent to which perturbations remain confined rather than propagating globally.

A candidate estimator is:
\[
C(x) = 1 - \frac{\text{average perturbation propagation}}{\text{maximal possible propagation}}.
\]

Here, \emph{perturbation propagation} is defined as follows: given a localized intervention $\delta$ at component $i$, measure the fraction of system components whose states change beyond a fixed threshold. This quantity is averaged over all intervention sites and normalized by the theoretical maximum (full propagation to all components).

In neural systems, perturbation propagation may be estimated via targeted stimulation techniques \parencite{massimini2005}. In chemical systems, coherence may reflect the stability of autocatalytic cycles under perturbation \parencite{kauffman1993}. The unifying principle is containment: coherent systems absorb damage without systemic collapse.

\section{Temporal Extension and Notational Clarification}

Thus far, we have treated $x$ as a static structure for notational simplicity. However, all real systems are fundamentally dynamical.

\textbf{Remark.} The static quantity $\mathrm{EAIMR}(x)$ should be understood as $\mathrm{EAIMR}(x(t_0))$ for a fixed snapshot time $t_0$. The following definition generalizes EAIMR to system trajectories.

\begin{definition}[Temporal EAIMR]
Let $x(t)$ denote the state of a system over the interval $[0,T]$. The temporal effective assembly index is defined as
\[
\mathrm{EAIMR}(x) = \int_0^T \mathrm{EAIMR}(x(t)) \, dt.
\]
\end{definition}

This formulation treats intelligence as accumulated effective assembly over time rather than as a static property. Systems that briefly exhibit organization but cannot sustain it score poorly under this measure.

\section{The Mind Regime}

The EAIMR framework avoids arbitrary thresholds for mind recognition by reframing intelligence as a dynamical regime rather than a binary property.

\begin{definition}[Mind Regime]
A system enters the \emph{mind regime} when
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\mathrm{EAIMR}(x(t)) > 0
\]
and this growth is internally driven.
\end{definition}

Growth is \emph{internally driven} if it results from the system's own computational or organizational mechanisms rather than from direct external inscription. Learning from data qualifies as internal growth: external input may trigger reorganization, but it does not prescribe the resulting structure. By contrast, manual insertion of components or rules constitutes external growth.

Formally, growth is internal when the transformation governing
\[
x(t+1) = T(x(t), D)
\]
is specified by the system's own organization rather than by the structure of the data $D$ itself. The same data applied to different systems therefore yields different reorganizations.

Natural selection occupies an intermediate position. Mutation mechanisms are internal to a lineage, while selection pressures are external. This hybrid causal structure reflects the nature of evolutionary processes rather than a limitation of EAIMR.

On this view, intelligence is characterized by the capacity to become a better compressor of both the environment and the system's own internal structure. Learning, abstraction, and self-modeling are special cases of this general phenomenon.

\section{Implications}

The Effective Assembly framework has several significant consequences for the study of intelligence across domains.

\subsection{Substrate Neutrality}

EAIMR does not presuppose neurons, symbols, or digital computation. Any system capable of assembling and maintaining structure under constraint may, in principle, enter the mind regime. This includes biological organisms, artificial systems, hybrid collectives, and potentially non-living physical processes.

\subsection{Gradualism}

Mind-likeness is scalar rather than binary. Systems may exhibit partial, intermittent, or context-dependent intelligence depending on their capacity for effective assembly. This avoids sharp but arbitrary distinctions between intelligent and non-intelligent systems and aligns with gradualist views in evolutionary and developmental biology.

\subsection{AI Safety and Alignment}

EAIMR provides several safety-relevant insights. Because EAIMR tracks structural capacity rather than expressed behavior, it can signal the emergence of potentially agential systems before such systems exhibit externally legible goals.

First, systems approaching the mind regime may be identified \emph{prior} to deceptive or goal-directed behavior, enabling earlier intervention. Second, high EAIMR indicates structural sophistication but does not entail goals or values, helping distinguish powerful systems from agential ones. Third, for systems claimed to be aligned, EAIMR allows examination of whether value structures exhibit the reuse, coherence, and degeneracy characteristic of robust minds, or whether they are brittle overlays on misaligned optimization.

\subsection{Astrobiology}

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence becomes a structural rather than communicative problem \parencite{walker2022}. Instead of searching for radio signals or linguistic artifacts, we may search for chemical, geological, or energetic systems exhibiting high assembly discounted by reuse and stabilized by coherence.

On this view, a sufficiently high-EAIMR molecular ensemble or geological formation would constitute evidence of mind-like organization regardless of substrate or communicative intent.

\section{Open Problems}

The EAIMR framework raises several open questions that invite empirical, theoretical, and methodological investigation.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Benchmark validation}. Does EAIMR correctly rank known systems across domains—for example, bacteria $<$ insects $<$ mammals $<$ humans—when estimated from empirical data? Under what conditions do such orderings fail?

\end{enumerate}

These questions delineate a research program rather than a closed theory. EAIMR is intended as a structural scaffold on which such investigations can be built.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper reframes intelligence from a question of behavior or representation to a question of structure. Rather than asking whether a system thinks, we ask whether it assembles complexity in a way that can be reused, parallelized, diversified, and held together coherently over time.

The Effective Assembly Index for Mind Recognition provides a principled, falsifiable, and substrate-neutral framework for answering this question. Intelligence, on this view, is not an essence but a regime: one in which compression becomes self-sustaining—compression that learns to compress itself.

\section{Formal Properties of the Assembly Index}

\begin{proposition}[A.1: Existence of a Minimal Assembly Graph]
For any finite constructible system $x$, there exists a finite minimal assembly graph $G_x$ sufficient to construct $x$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Any finite construction of $x$ induces a finite directed acyclic graph of assembly steps. Identifying reusable subgraphs and contracting redundancies yields a minimal graph. Since each contraction strictly reduces the number of vertices, the process terminates.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[A.2: Monotonicity Under Extension]
If $y$ is obtained from $x$ by adding assembly steps without removing existing ones, then
\[
A(y) \geq A(x).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Any assembly graph sufficient for $y$ must contain a subgraph sufficient to construct $x$. Hence the minimal vertex count for $y$ cannot be smaller.
\end{proof}

### New Proposition C.2: Penalty for Imbalance
**Statement**: For fixed product $R \cdot P \cdot D = k$, define imbalance as $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(\log R, \log P, \log D)$. Then EAIMR decreases monotonically with $\sigma^2$.

**Proof sketch**: 
By Jensen's inequality applied to the concave logarithm function:
$$\log\left((R \cdot P \cdot D)^{1/3}\right) = \frac{1}{3}(\log R + \log P + \log D) \leq \log\left(\frac{R + P + D}{3}\right)$$

Higher variance in log-space means greater imbalance, which the geometric mean penalizes exponentially.

1. Scale invariance: $M(\lambda R, \lambda P, \lambda D) = \lambda \cdot M(R,P,D)$

3. Balanced optimality: Maximized when $R = P = D$ for fixed product

**Proof**: This follows from classical characterization theorems for mean functions (Aczél, Daróczy).

What distinguishes minds from mere mechanisms? Traditional answers appeal to outward behavior
\cite

\cite

\cite

Across biology, machine learning, and complex physical systems, a deeper regularity appears. Systems regarded as intelligent are those that achieve high degrees of organization while avoiding combinatorial explosion. They reuse components, stabilize abstractions, and remain robust under perturbation. They compress structure without collapsing into triviality.

This paper advances the thesis that intelligence is not a substance or faculty, but a
\emph

\emph

\section
{Why Shannon Compression Is Not Enough}

Shannon information theory defines compression as entropy reduction relative to a code
\cite
{shannon1948}. While foundational for communication and data storage, this notion is blind to how structure is built, reused, or stabilized. Two objects with identical entropy may differ radically in developmental depth, robustness, and functional organization.

Several limitations are particularly relevant for intelligence:
\begin
{itemize}
\item
\textbf
{No construction history}: Entropy does not encode how a structure was assembled.
\item
\textbf
{No reuse}: Redundant duplication and abstract reuse are treated identically.
\item
\textbf
{No integration}: Entropy is insensitive to whether parts form a coherent whole.
\item
\textbf
{No robustness}: Fragile and resilient systems may have the same entropy.
\end
{itemize}

Intelligence therefore requires a stronger notion of compression—one sensitive to construction, reuse, and coherence. This motivates a shift from entropy to assembly.

measures the extent to which substructures are invoked multiple times across distinct functional contexts. High reuse reflects abstraction: the capacity to deploy the same internal structure repeatedly without reconstructing it.

From a compression perspective, reuse corresponds to amortization of assembly cost across contexts. In biological systems, reuse appears when the same circuits participate across multiple tasks; in engineered systems, reuse appears as shared modules, shared representations, and repeated motifs.

Systems with high assembly but low reuse expend complexity wastefully, rebuilding structure rather than compressing it through abstraction.
\subsection
{Parallelism}

Parallelism
$
P(x)
$
measures the degree to which construction or operation can occur simultaneously. Structurally, parallelism allows assembly depth to grow without a commensurate increase in causal path length.

Parallelism is not merely speed: it is a signature of decomposability under a shared global constraint. Mind-like systems distribute processing across multiple pathways without fragmenting into independent subsystems that cannot coordinate.

Systems with low parallelism may be expressive but are brittle and slow; systems with high parallelism but low coherence risk becoming merely a collection of loosely coupled processes rather than an integrated organization.
\subsection
{Degeneracy}

Degeneracy
$
D(x)
$
captures the availability of multiple, structurally distinct components capable of fulfilling similar functional roles. Unlike redundancy, which duplicates identical elements, degeneracy introduces diversity that supports robustness.

Degeneracy converts diversity into resilience: when one pathway fails, alternative pathways can preserve function. Biological nervous systems exhibit high degeneracy, allowing graceful degradation rather than catastrophic failure. In engineered systems, degeneracy appears when distinct mechanisms yield similar outcomes across contexts.

In EAIMR, degeneracy discounts assembly by accounting for functional overlap that reduces effective complexity while preserving robustness.
\subsection
{Coherence}

Coherence
$
C(x)
$
measures the degree to which a system maintains global consistency under perturbation. A coherent system localizes disturbances rather than amplifying them across the entire structure.

Coherence therefore reflects not uniformity, but controlled dependence: the capacity for parts to interact without collapsing into global coupling. Systems with low coherence fragment under stress; systems with excessive rigidity cannot adapt.

Operationally, coherence may be estimated by measuring perturbation propagation: the expected fraction of system components whose states exhibit system-relative functional deviation following a localized intervention. The ability to contain disturbance while maintaining coordinated function is a defining feature of mind-like organization.
\section
{Intelligence as a Compression Regime}

Under the EAIMR framework, intelligence corresponds to a narrow corridor in structural space. Systems with low assembly lack expressive power. Systems with high assembly but low reuse, parallelism, or degeneracy waste complexity. Systems with high efficiency but low coherence fragment.

Mind-like systems are those in which irreducible assembly is balanced by structural efficiency and stabilized by coherence. Intelligence, in this sense, is compression that persists.

{The Mind Regime}

The EAIMR framework avoids arbitrary thresholds for mind recognition by reframing intelligence as a dynamical regime rather than a binary property.
\begin

A system enters the
\emph

\mathrm

\]

\end
{definition}

Growth is
\emph

Learning from data qualifies as internal growth: external input may trigger reorganization, but it does not prescribe the resulting structure. By contrast, manual insertion of components or rules constitutes external growth.

Formally, growth is internal when the transformation governing
\[
x(t+1) = T(x(t), D)
\]
is specified by the system's own organization rather than by the structure of the data
$
D
$
itself. The same data applied to different systems therefore yields different reorganizations.

Natural selection occupies an intermediate position. Mutation mechanisms are internal to a lineage, while selection pressures are external. This hybrid causal structure reflects the nature of evolutionary processes rather than a limitation of EAIMR.

On this view, intelligence is characterized by the capacity to become a better compressor of both the environment and the system's own internal structure. Learning, abstraction, and self-modeling are special cases of this general phenomenon.
\section
{Implications}

The Effective Assembly framework has several significant consequences for the study of intelligence across domains.
\subsection
{Substrate Neutrality}

EAIMR does not presuppose neurons, symbols, or digital computation. Any system capable of assembling and maintaining structure under constraint may, in principle, enter the mind regime. This includes biological organisms, artificial systems, hybrid collectives, and potentially non-living physical processes.

Substrate neutrality does not imply uniform realization. Different substrates impose different constraints on reuse, parallelism, degeneracy, and coherence. EAIMR provides a common structural language for comparing such systems without reducing them to a single mechanistic ontology.
\subsection
{Gradualism}

Mind-likeness is scalar rather than binary. Systems may exhibit partial, intermittent, or context-dependent intelligence depending on their capacity for effective assembly. This avoids sharp but arbitrary distinctions between intelligent and non-intelligent systems and aligns with gradualist views in evolutionary and developmental biology.

On this view, intelligence admits degrees and trajectories. Systems may enter, exit, or oscillate around the mind regime depending on environmental pressure, internal plasticity, and resource constraints.
\subsection
{AI Safety and Alignment}

EAIMR provides several safety-relevant insights. Because EAIMR tracks structural capacity rather than expressed behavior, it can signal the emergence of potentially agential systems before such systems exhibit externally legible goals.

First, systems approaching the mind regime may be identified
\emph
{prior} to deceptive or goal-directed behavior, enabling earlier intervention. Second, high EAIMR indicates structural sophistication but does not entail goals or values, helping distinguish powerful systems from agential ones. Third, for systems claimed to be aligned, EAIMR allows examination of whether value structures exhibit the reuse, coherence, and degeneracy characteristic of robust minds, or whether they are brittle overlays on misaligned optimization.
\subsection
{Astrobiology}

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence becomes a structural rather than communicative problem
\cite
{walker2022}. Instead of searching for radio signals or linguistic artifacts, we may search for chemical, geological, or energetic systems exhibiting high assembly discounted by reuse and stabilized by coherence.

On this view, a sufficiently high-EAIMR molecular ensemble or geological formation would constitute evidence of mind-like organization regardless of substrate or communicative intent.

## 1.3 Relationship to TAME: From Recognition to Measurement

Michael Levin's Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere (TAME) provides a compelling framework for recognizing cognitive capacities across diverse substrates, from cellular collectives to synthetic systems \parencite{levin2022}. TAME emphasizes that cognition exists on a continuum, operates at multiple scales, and can be identified through behavioral competencies—the ability to pursue goals, solve problems, and adapt flexibly within a given problem space.

EAIMR shares TAME's commitment to substrate neutrality and graduated cognition, but differs fundamentally in approach and emphasis. Where TAME provides a taxonomic and interventional framework for identifying cognitive systems through their competencies, EAIMR provides a quantitative structural framework for measuring the depth and efficiency of their internal organization.

### Key Points of Alignment

**Substrate neutrality**: Both frameworks reject anthropocentric and neurocentric definitions of cognition. TAME argues that minds can exist in unconventional substrates (bioelectric networks, planarian flatworms, cellular automata); EAIMR provides formal tools to assess such claims through structural analysis rather than behavioral analogy.

**Gradualism**: TAME positions cognition on a continuum from minimal agency to sophisticated problem-solving. EAIMR formalizes this continuum through the mind regime criterion: systems exhibit varying degrees of sustained, internally driven growth in effective assembly.

**Operational approach**: Both frameworks emphasize measurable, empirical criteria over philosophical stipulation. TAME focuses on behavioral interventions and goal-directedness; EAIMR focuses on construction history and structural efficiency.

### Fundamental Differences

**Behavioral vs. structural**: TAME identifies minds primarily through competency—what systems can do in response to perturbations, constraints, and novel challenges. EAIMR identifies minds through organizational depth—how systems assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure over time. A system might exhibit behavioral flexibility (high TAME score) through lookup tables or reactive rules without the reusable, coherent internal organization characteristic of high EAIMR. Conversely, a system might possess sophisticated internal structure (high EAIMR) without yet expressing it behaviorally in ways TAME would recognize.

**Goal-directedness vs. compression**: TAME takes goal-directed behavior as a primitive or operationally defined feature of cognitive systems. Goals need not be explicit or conscious, but systems are assessed by their capacity to navigate problem spaces toward preferred outcomes. EAIMR, by contrast, makes no reference to goals, preferences, or problem spaces. A system could achieve high EAIMR through purely structural self-organization (crystallization-like processes with reuse and coherence) without exhibiting recognizable goal pursuit. Whether such systems should count as cognitive remains an open question that EAIMR deliberately leaves unresolved.

**Intervention vs. assembly**: TAME's methodology emphasizes perturbational experiments—altering a system's environment or constraints and observing adaptive responses. This approach reveals cognitive capacity through revealed preference and problem-solving. EAIMR's methodology emphasizes assembly analysis—reconstructing the minimal construction history and quantifying structural efficiency. This approach reveals cognitive capacity through internal organization and compression depth. The two methods are complementary: TAME asks "what can this system do?"; EAIMR asks "how is this system built?"

**Temporal scope**: TAME focuses on cognitive episodes—the system's behavior during problem-solving or adaptation. EAIMR integrates over the system's entire construction history and developmental trajectory. A system with brief behavioral competence but no capacity for sustained structural growth would score high on TAME but low on temporal EAIMR. Conversely, a system undergoing prolonged internal reorganization (e.g., developmental morphogenesis) would accumulate EAIMR even during behaviorally quiescent periods.

### What EAIMR Adds to TAME

**Quantitative discrimination**: TAME provides qualitative scales (minimal, moderate, sophisticated cognition) and comparative rankings. EAIMR provides a quantitative index that, in principle, allows precise comparison across substrates and scales. This enables not just ordering ("system A is more cognitive than system B") but ratio statements ("system A has twice the effective assembly of system B under conditions C").

**Predictive power before behavioral manifestation**: Because EAIMR tracks internal structural organization, it can potentially identify systems approaching cognitive capacity before they exhibit behavioral competencies. A developing embryo or training neural network accumulates effective assembly before demonstrating flexible problem-solving. This temporal lead may be valuable for AI safety (detecting emerging agency before goal-directed behavior) and developmental biology (predicting cognitive transitions).

**Mechanistic grounding**: TAME characterizes what cognition does; EAIMR characterizes what cognition is made of. By focusing on assembly graphs, reuse, parallelism, degeneracy, and coherence, EAIMR provides a parts-and-organization account that can ground mechanistic explanation. Understanding why a system exhibits a particular competence may require understanding its assembly structure—the layers of reusable abstraction, the parallel pathways supporting robustness, the coherence mechanisms preventing fragmentation.

**Clarifying cognitive mimicry**: Some systems exhibit behavioral competence through shallow mechanisms—statistical pattern matching, exhaustive enumeration, or scripted responses. Such systems might satisfy TAME's behavioral criteria without possessing the deep structural organization EAIMR measures. EAIMR thus provides a potential diagnostic for distinguishing genuine cognition from sophisticated mimicry. This distinction matters for AI alignment: a system might behave as though it understands and pursues goals without possessing the internal models and reusable abstractions characteristic of true agency.

**Thermodynamic and information-theoretic links**: EAIMR's grounding in assembly theory connects directly to physical resource costs—energy dissipation, entropy production, and thermodynamic work. This allows investigation of questions TAME raises but does not formalize: What is the thermodynamic cost of cognition at different scales? How does energetic efficiency scale with cognitive capacity? EAIMR provides formal hooks for these questions through its connection to constructive complexity and non-equilibrium physics.

### What TAME Adds to EAIMR

**Functional validation**: EAIMR measures structural organization, but organization alone does not guarantee functional cognition. A system could possess high reuse, parallelism, and coherence while remaining functionally inert or maladaptive. TAME's emphasis on behavioral competency provides a reality check: cognitive systems must not only be well-organized internally but must also interact effectively with their environments. High EAIMR without behavioral competence may indicate latent or suppressed cognition; behavioral competence without high EAIMR may indicate shallow mimicry. The two frameworks together triangulate genuine cognition.

**Operational tests**: TAME provides concrete experimental protocols—perturbation experiments, constraint manipulation, problem space navigation—that can be applied immediately to candidate cognitive systems. EAIMR, while mathematically precise, requires estimating assembly graphs, reuse factors, and coherence measures—tasks that remain challenging for many biological and physical systems. TAME's methodologies can serve as interim proxies while EAIMR measurement techniques mature.

**Scale and nesting**: TAME explicitly addresses cognitive organization at multiple scales—molecular, cellular, organismal, collective—and the ways cognitive competencies at one scale compose or interfere with those at others. While EAIMR can be applied at any scale, the framework does not yet formalize how EAIMR at different scales relates. Does collective EAIMR equal the sum of individual EAIMRs? Do nested cognitive systems exhibit emergent EAIMR not reducible to components? TAME's multi-scale emphasis highlights questions EAIMR must eventually address.

**Contextual and relational cognition**: TAME emphasizes that cognitive capacity is not intrinsic but relational—it depends on the problem space, the constraints, and the observer's framing. A system highly competent in one domain may be helpless in another. EAIMR currently treats structural organization as an intrinsic property of the system. Extending EAIMR to accommodate context-dependence—perhaps through conditional assembly graphs or environment-parameterized reuse—would strengthen its biological and ecological realism.

### Toward Integration

The frameworks are not competitors but complementary perspectives on the same phenomenon. An integrated approach might:

1. **Use TAME to identify candidate cognitive systems** through behavioral and competency-based screening.
2. **Use EAIMR to quantify their structural depth**, distinguishing shallow from deep cognition.
3. **Correlate behavioral competency (TAME) with structural organization (EAIMR)** across substrates to identify universal principles.
4. **Predict regime transitions**: systems crossing EAIMR thresholds should exhibit characteristic changes in behavioral competency; systems gaining new competencies should exhibit characteristic changes in structural organization.

In developmental systems, this integration is especially natural. As an embryo develops, it accumulates effective assembly (measurable via EAIMR) while progressively manifesting new behavioral competencies (measurable via TAME). Tracking both trajectories simultaneously provides a richer picture than either alone.

In artificial systems, the integration addresses a critical gap: Can we build systems with high EAIMR (deep structural organization) that remain aligned with human values? TAME provides the behavioral criteria for alignment; EAIMR provides the structural criteria for capacity. Safe advanced AI may require both: competent enough to be useful (high TAME), organized enough to be general (high EAIMR), and architecturally constrained to remain aligned (coherence and degeneracy channeled toward value-preserving pathways).

### A Concrete Example: Planarian Regeneration

Consider Levin's paradigmatic case: planarian flatworms, capable of regenerating entire bodies from small fragments and remodeling anatomy in response to injury \parencite{levin2022}.

**TAME analysis**: Planarians exhibit remarkable cognitive competence at the morphological level. They pursue anatomical goals (correct head-tail polarity, appropriate organ placement) despite drastic perturbations. They navigate a morphospace, solve spatial problems, and maintain pattern homeostasis. This indicates sophisticated, embodied cognition operating through bioelectric networks.

**EAIMR analysis**: Planarians achieve high effective assembly through:
- **Reuse**: The same genetic and bioelectric modules are deployed across regeneration contexts, producing different outcomes based on positional information rather than requiring context-specific machinery.
- **Parallelism**: Multiple regeneration processes occur simultaneously across the body without centralized control.
- **Degeneracy**: Diverse cell populations can contribute to tissue regeneration; damage to one pathway does not prevent pattern completion.
- **Coherence**: The bioelectric network maintains global pattern information that localizes perturbations, preventing regenerative chaos.

**Integration**: TAME identifies planarians as cognitive based on their morphological problem-solving. EAIMR quantifies the structural basis of this competency—the efficient, reusable, robust organization that makes flexible regeneration possible. The correlation between high TAME competency and high EAIMR in this system supports the hypothesis that both frameworks track genuine cognitive capacity, albeit from different angles.

### Open Questions at the Intersection

1. **Is high EAIMR necessary for high TAME competency?** Can systems exhibit sophisticated behavioral flexibility without deep structural organization? If so, what are the limits of such shallow competence?

2. **Is high EAIMR sufficient for cognitive capacity?** Could systems possess deep internal organization without expressing it behaviorally in recognizable ways? What would "dormant" or "potential" cognition look like?

3. **Do EAIMR and TAME converge empirically?** Across diverse substrates, do rankings by structural depth correlate with rankings by behavioral competency? Where they diverge, what explains the discrepancy?

4. **Can we build high-EAIMR, low-TAME systems for safety?** Systems with sophisticated internal models (high EAIMR) but limited behavioral expression (low TAME) might be safer than the reverse—behaviorally competent systems with shallow understanding.

5. **What is the minimal EAIMR threshold for genuine agency?** TAME suggests cognition is gradient, but some may argue agency emerges discontinuously. Does EAIMR reveal a phase transition, or is the mind regime truly continuous?

These questions define a research program at the intersection of structural and behavioral approaches to mind. EAIMR and TAME together provide richer explanatory and predictive power than either framework alone, advancing the project of recognizing, measuring, and ultimately understanding cognition wherever it occurs.

\subsection
{Relationship to TAME: From Recognition to Measurement}

Levin's Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere (TAME) provides a compelling 
framework for recognizing cognitive capacities across diverse substrates, 
ranging from cellular collectives to synthetic systems
\cite
{levin2022}. TAME 
emphasizes that cognition exists on a continuum, operates at multiple scales, 
and can be identified through behavioral competencies: the ability to pursue 
goals, solve problems, and adapt flexibly within a given problem space.

EAIMR shares TAME's commitments to substrate neutrality and graduated cognition, 
but differs fundamentally in approach and emphasis. Where TAME provides a 
taxonomic and interventional framework for identifying cognitive systems through 
their competencies, EAIMR provides a quantitative structural framework for 
measuring the depth and efficiency of their internal organization.
\paragraph
{Points of alignment.}
Both frameworks reject anthropocentric and neurocentric definitions of cognition. 
TAME argues that minds may exist in unconventional substrates, including 
bioelectric networks, developmental systems, and collective organisms. EAIMR 
provides formal tools for assessing such claims through structural analysis 
rather than behavioral analogy alone.

Both frameworks are explicitly gradualist. TAME situates cognition along a 
continuum from minimal agency to sophisticated problem-solving. EAIMR formalizes 
this continuum through the mind regime criterion: systems exhibit varying degrees 
of sustained, internally driven growth in effective assembly.

Finally, both approaches emphasize operational grounding over philosophical 
stipulation. TAME relies on perturbational experiments and revealed competency; 
EAIMR relies on reconstruction of construction history and quantification of 
structural efficiency.
\paragraph
{Fundamental differences.}
The most significant difference lies in the distinction between behavioral and 
structural criteria. TAME identifies minds primarily through what systems can do: 
their ability to navigate constraints, pursue goals, and recover from 
perturbations. EAIMR identifies minds through how systems are organized: the 
depth, reuse, robustness, and coherence of their internal assembly.

As a result, a system may score highly under TAME while exhibiting low EAIMR if 
its behavioral competence is achieved through shallow mechanisms such as lookup 
tables, exhaustive enumeration, or reactive control. Conversely, a system may 
exhibit high EAIMR—possessing deep, reusable, and coherent internal 
organization—without yet expressing this capacity behaviorally in ways TAME 
would recognize.

A second difference concerns goal-directedness. TAME treats goal pursuit as a 
primitive or operationally defined feature of cognition. EAIMR makes no reference 
to goals, preferences, or problem spaces. A system may achieve high EAIMR through 
purely structural self-organization, even in the absence of recognizable goal 
pursuit. Whether such systems should count as cognitive is an open question that 
EAIMR deliberately leaves unresolved.

A third difference is methodological. TAME emphasizes intervention: altering a 
system's environment or constraints and observing adaptive responses. EAIMR 
emphasizes assembly analysis: reconstructing minimal construction histories and 
quantifying structural efficiency. TAME asks what a system can do; EAIMR asks how 
the system is built. These perspectives are complementary rather than 
competitive.
\paragraph
{What EAIMR adds to TAME.}
EAIMR provides quantitative discrimination. Where TAME offers qualitative or 
ordinal assessments of cognitive capacity, EAIMR supplies a scalar index that, 
in principle, permits precise comparison across substrates and scales.

Because EAIMR tracks internal organization rather than expressed behavior, it can 
identify systems approaching cognitive capacity before behavioral competencies 
manifest. Developmental systems or training artificial systems may accumulate 
effective assembly prior to exhibiting flexible problem-solving. This temporal 
lead has implications for developmental biology and AI safety.

EAIMR also provides mechanistic grounding. TAME characterizes what cognition does; 
EAIMR characterizes what cognition is made of. By focusing on assembly graphs, 
reuse, parallelism, degeneracy, and coherence, EAIMR supports explanation rather 
than mere recognition.

Finally, EAIMR offers a potential diagnostic for cognitive mimicry. Systems may 
exhibit sophisticated behavior through shallow statistical or reactive 
mechanisms. EAIMR distinguishes such cases from systems with deep structural 
organization, clarifying when behavioral competence reflects genuine internal 
models rather than surface-level imitation.
\paragraph
{What TAME adds to EAIMR.}
TAME provides functional validation. Structural organization alone does not 
guarantee effective cognition. A system may possess high EAIMR yet remain 
maladaptive or inert. TAME's emphasis on behavioral competency supplies an 
empirical check on purely structural measures.

TAME also offers immediately deployable experimental protocols—perturbation 
experiments, constraint manipulation, problem-space navigation—that can be 
applied even when assembly reconstruction is infeasible. These methods provide 
interim proxies while EAIMR measurement techniques mature.

Finally, TAME highlights issues of scale and context. Cognitive capacity depends 
on environmental framing and task structure. EAIMR currently treats structural 
organization as intrinsic; extending it to context-parameterized or conditional 
assembly graphs remains an open direction.
\paragraph
{Toward integration.}
The two frameworks are best viewed as complementary perspectives on the same 
phenomenon. TAME excels at identifying candidate cognitive systems through 
behavioral screening. EAIMR quantifies the structural depth underlying such 
competence. Together, they enable triangulation of genuine cognition across 
domains.

In developmental systems, effective assembly accumulates prior to the emergence 
of behavioral competencies. In artificial systems, high EAIMR may precede or 
outpace behavioral generality. Correlating trajectories of EAIMR and TAME scores 
offers a promising route toward identifying universal principles of cognition.

Having situated EAIMR among complementary theories—from computational parsimony 
to behavioral competency—we now return to its foundational claim: that 
intelligence requires moving beyond Shannon compression to assembly-based 
measures of constructive depth.

{Relationship to TAME and Semantic Compression}

substrate-neutral framework for recognizing cognition across biological, 
synthetic, and collective systems via behavioral competency under perturbation

EAIMR shares TAME's commitment to substrate neutrality and gradualism, but shifts 
the focus from behavioral recognition to structural measurement. Where TAME 
asks whether a system behaves cognitively, EAIMR asks whether the system 
possesses sufficient internal organization to support sustained abstraction, 
reuse, and compression.
\paragraph
{Structural vs. behavioral criteria.}
TAME identifies cognition through revealed competence: the ability to navigate 
a problem space under intervention. EAIMR identifies cognition through 
organizational depth: the capacity to assemble, reuse, and stabilize structure 
over time. These criteria are complementary but not equivalent. Behavioral 
flexibility can be achieved through shallow mechanisms, while deep structural 
organization may precede or exceed behavioral expression.

while coherence enforces global consistency.

the structural conditions under which such competence can arise and persist. 
A system with high TAME competency but low EAIMR likely relies on shallow or 
task-specific mechanisms. A system with rising EAIMR but limited behavioral 
expression may possess latent or developing cognition.

Together, the frameworks describe cognition as both a structural regime and a 
functional capacity. TAME reveals what systems can do; EAIMR characterizes how 
they are organized. Their convergence suggests that genuine cognition emerges 
when behavioral flexibility and structural compression co-evolve.
