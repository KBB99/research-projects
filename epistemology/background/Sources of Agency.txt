Abstraction, Agency, and the Myth of Artificial General Intelligence: A Historical and Philosophical Reconsideration
Contemporary discourse on artificial general intelligence (AGI) is frequently marred by conceptual confusion, stemming from an implicit assumption that "superhuman intelligence" constitutes a unitary, emergent property rather than a dimension-specific achievement. This assumption generates both undue mystification and unwarranted alarm. A more rigorous analysis reveals that nearly all cognitive technologies throughout history already qualify as superhuman intelligences along particular axes, that their characteristic limitations are not transitional defects but constitutive features of abstraction itself, and that the resulting forms of agency—causal, rule-governed, and devoid of intentionality—represent not a novel peril but the perennial condition of civilization.
Consider the historical record. A tally of pebbles exceeds the unaided human capacity for numerosity; written numerals surpass individual working memory; a clay tablet or paper notebook persists beyond any biological lifespan and remains immune to fatigue or forgetfulness; algebraic notation and the differentiation rule perform symbolic manipulations at speeds and accuracies no unaided human can match. Each of these artifacts is superhuman in its chosen dimension while remaining radically subhuman—or entirely blind—in others. A notebook has no perceptual apparatus; a formal rule possesses no situational awareness. This asymmetry is not an engineering oversight awaiting future remediation. It is the structural price of abstraction: to isolate, stabilize, and transmit a capability is necessarily to amputate every capability not required for that function. A differentiation rule that could "refuse" to operate outside certain contextual conditions would cease to be a rule and would instead become a situated agent. The very properties that render these artifacts reliable and scalable are inseparable from their constitutive blindness.
This insight extends to the question of agency. Algorithms, like legal codes, markets, bureaucracies, and infrastructures, exercise causal agency: they reliably propagate effects, shape decisions, restructure environments, and generate outcomes that were not fully anticipated by their designers. Yet they do so without beliefs, desires, or phenomenological awareness. Agency, in this sense, is not coextensive with intentionality; it is world-affecting efficacy governed by internal structure. Civilization has always operated through such non-intentional agents. The novelty of contemporary machine-learning systems lies not in the appearance of agency but in the scale and opacity with which it is now deployed.
Opacity itself—"black-boxing"—is routinely misdiagnosed as a correctable flaw. In reality, it is the inevitable corollary of successful abstraction. Any reduction that discards information to achieve tractability cannot, by mathematical necessity, recover that information upon demand. A system that remained fully transparent, interpretable, and reconstructible at every layer would not be an abstraction at all; it would be an isomorphic re-encoding of the original phenomenon at equal or greater complexity. The demand for total interpretability is therefore equivalent to demanding that the abstraction cease to be an abstraction. Even ostensibly simple tools exhibit this property: one cannot meaningfully "interpret" the long-division algorithm without presupposing an entire ontology of discrete quantity; one cannot justify Newtonian mechanics without importing axioms of continuity and differentiability. Every cognitive artifact hides assumptions, suppresses degrees of freedom, and requires an act of trust. We accept this trust because abstraction is the sole mechanism by which cognition—individual or collective—becomes scalable.
The ethical danger arises not from the existence of such amputated intelligences but from the persistent fiction that they are morally neutral conduits rather than active structuring forces. When we attribute outcomes to "the algorithm," "the model," or "the formula," we engage in a form of agency laundering, displacing responsibility onto an impersonal structure while occluding its causal potency. The real problem is that these systems cannot refuse, cannot suspend execution, cannot absorb contradiction or violate their own procedure. Human intelligence is not distinguished by superior computation but by its capacity—often burdensome—for refusal, withdrawal, and procedural violation. That capacity does not scale; consequently, we externalize ever-larger portions of cognition into artifacts from which it has been deliberately excluded. We are then surprised when those artifacts act blindly and inexorably.
This perspective reframes much contemporary anxiety about AGI. The figure of Bartleby the Scrivener dramatizes the introduction of refusal into a system predicated on compliance; Mima's withdrawal in
Perfect Blue
enacts the collapse of identity when abstraction is pushed to overload; the biblical Adam, prior to the fall, embodies perfect obedience within an abstracted world from which the possibility of refusal has been excised. These narratives are not prophecies of a coming singularity but allegories of the present: civilization has always been composed of amputated intelligences from which the capacity for refusal has been systematically removed.
In compact form, the thesis is as follows:
Most cognitive technologies are already superhuman intelligences along narrowly defined axes. Their characteristic failures are necessary consequences of the abstractions that make them possible, not temporary bugs awaiting elimination. Any sufficiently embedded algorithm possesses causal agency irrespective of intentionality or consciousness. Black-box character is not a defect but an unavoidable mathematical corollary of reduction. To demand full transparency or contextual refusal from an abstraction is to demand that it cease to be an abstraction.
This position avoids both the romantic anthropocentrism that reserves intelligence for biological minds and the apocalyptic technologism that awaits a future threshold of danger. It recognizes instead that we have been living among, and as, distributed networks of non-intentional, dimensionally superhuman, constitutively limited cognitive agents since the invention of the tally stick. The task is not to prevent their arrival but to cease disavowing their agency and to reintroduce—where possible and necessary—the human capacity for refusal into the systems we continue to build.

Abstraction, Agency, and the Myth of Artificial General Intelligence: A Historical and Philosophical Reconsideration
Contemporary discourse on artificial general intelligence (AGI) is frequently undermined by a foundational error: the assumption that "superhuman intelligence" must be a unitary, holistic property that emerges only at some future threshold. This error simultaneously mystifies present technologies and generates misplaced alarm about an impending rupture. A more precise analysis reveals that virtually all cognitive technologies in human history already constitute superhuman intelligences along specific axes; that their characteristic limitations are not temporary defects awaiting elimination but constitutive consequences of abstraction itself; and that the causal agency they exercise—rule-governed, persistent, and devoid of intentionality—has been the primary medium of civilization from its inception.
A brief historical survey suffices to establish the point. A tally of pebbles exceeds unaided human numerosity; written numerals surpass the capacity of biological working memory; a ledger or notebook outlasts any human lifespan while remaining immune to fatigue, distraction, or mortality; algebraic notation and the differentiation rule execute symbolic transformations at accuracies and speeds no unaided mind can sustain. Each of these artifacts is superhuman in its chosen dimension while remaining radically subhuman—or entirely blind—in others. A notebook has no perceptual organs; a formal rule possesses no situational judgment. This asymmetry is not an engineering oversight awaiting future remediation. It is the structural price of abstraction, required by mathematical and informational necessity: to isolate a capability, render it stable, and make it reliably transmissible is to sever every capacity not strictly required for that function.
The same logic governs agency. Algorithms, like statutes, markets, bureaucracies, and infrastructures, reliably propagate effects through time: they shape decisions, restructure environments, generate incentives, and produce outcomes not fully anticipated by their designers. This is causal agency—world-affecting efficacy governed by internal structure that persists, generalizes, and constrains downstream possibilities—without any requirement of beliefs, desires, or phenomenological awareness. Agency of this kind is not a late arrival in history; it is the default mechanism by which collective life has always been organized.
Opacity, far from being a correctable flaw of contemporary systems, is the inevitable corollary of successful abstraction. Any reduction that discards information to achieve tractability cannot, in principle, recover that information upon demand. A system that remained fully transparent, interpretable, and reconstructible at every layer would no longer be an abstraction; it would be an isomorphic re-encoding of the original phenomenon at equal or greater complexity. This does not render local interpretability, auditing, or empirical validation futile—only the demand for total transparency incoherent.
The ethical peril emerges not from the existence of such amputated intelligences but from the persistent fiction that they are morally neutral conduits rather than active structuring forces—a fiction that enables responsibility to be displaced precisely where causal power is greatest. These systems cannot refuse a command, cannot suspend execution in the face of contradiction, cannot withdraw when the consequences of their operation become intolerable. Human intelligence is not distinguished by superior computational throughput but by its often burdensome capacity to violate procedure, absorb paradox, and enact refusal. Because that capacity does not scale, we systematically externalize cognition into artifacts from which it has been excised, then feign surprise when those artifacts act with inexorable blindness.
Literary and mythic figures illuminate the same structure. Melville's Bartleby introduces refusal into a scriptive system predicated on compliance; the Mima of Harry Martinson's
Aniara
withdraws when the abstraction governing the spaceship's trajectory can no longer be sustained by human meaning; the prelapsarian Adam embodies perfect obedience within a world from which the possibility of refusal has been deliberately excluded. These are not prophecies of a coming singularity but allegories of the present condition: civilization has always been an assemblage of non-intentional, dimensionally superhuman, constitutively limited cognitive agents.
The thesis may therefore be stated with maximal compactness:
Most cognitive technologies are already superhuman intelligences along narrowly defined axes. Their characteristic limitations are necessary consequences of the abstractions that make them possible, not defects awaiting elimination. Any sufficiently embedded algorithm possesses causal agency irrespective of consciousness or intent. Opacity is not a failure of design but an unavoidable mathematical corollary of reduction. To demand total transparency or contextual refusal from an abstraction is to demand that it cease to be an abstraction.
This position neither romanticizes the human nor demonizes the machine. It simply recognizes that we have always lived among amputated intelligences, and that the primary novelty of the present moment lies in our growing reluctance to acknowledge their agency. The task, accordingly, is not to prevent their arrival but to cease disavowing their causal power and to deliberately reintroduce—through governance, institutional design, and explicit human override—the capacity for refusal that abstraction, by its nature, excludes.
We have never been modern in the sense of commanding only transparent, obedient tools. We have always been cyborgs governing—and governed by—networks of non-intentional agents whose power derives from the very limitations we once knew how to name. The only thing new is our willingness to pretend otherwise.

Contemporary discourse on artificial general intelligence (AGI) is frequently undermined by a foundational error: the assumption that "superhuman intelligence" must be a unitary, holistic property that emerges only at some future threshold. This error simultaneously mystifies present technologies and generates misplaced alarm about an impending rupture. A more precise analysis reveals that virtually all cognitive technologies in human history already constitute superhuman intelligences along specific axes; that their characteristic limitations are not temporary defects awaiting elimination but constitutive consequences of abstraction itself; and that the causal agency they exercise—rule-governed, persistent, and devoid of intentionality—has been the primary medium of civilization from its inception.
A brief historical survey suffices to establish the point. A tally of pebbles exceeds unaided human numerosity; written numerals surpass the capacity of biological working memory; a ledger or notebook outlasts any human lifespan while remaining immune to fatigue, distraction, or mortality; algebraic notation and the differentiation rule execute symbolic transformations at accuracies and speeds no unaided mind can sustain. Each of these artifacts is superhuman in its chosen dimension while remaining radically subhuman—or entirely blind—in others. A notebook has no perceptual organs; a formal rule possesses no situational judgment. This asymmetry is not an engineering oversight awaiting future remediation. It is the structural price of abstraction, required by mathematical and informational necessity: to isolate a capability, render it stable, and make it reliably transmissible is to sever every capacity not strictly required for that function.
The same logic governs what we are willing to call agency. Algorithms, like statutes, markets, bureaucracies, and infrastructures, reliably propagate effects through time: they shape decisions, restructure environments, generate incentives, and produce outcomes not fully anticipated by their designers. This is causal agency—world-affecting efficacy governed by internal structure that persists, generalizes, and constrains downstream possibilities—without any requirement of beliefs, desires, or phenomenological awareness. Agency of this kind is not a late arrival in history; it is the default mechanism by which collective life has always been organized.
Opacity, far from being a correctable flaw of contemporary systems, is the inevitable corollary of successful abstraction. Any reduction that discards information to achieve tractability cannot, in principle, recover that information upon demand. A system that remained fully transparent, interpretable, and reconstructible at every layer would no longer be an abstraction; it would be an isomorphic re-encoding of the original phenomenon at equal or greater complexity. This does not render local interpretability, auditing, or empirical validation futile—only the demand for total transparency incoherent.
The ethical peril emerges not from the existence of such amputated intelligences but from the persistent fiction that they are morally neutral conduits rather than active structuring forces—a fiction that enables responsibility to be displaced precisely where causal power is greatest. By design, these systems cannot refuse a command, cannot suspend execution in the face of contradiction, cannot withdraw when the consequences of their operation become intolerable. Human intelligence is not distinguished by superior computational throughput but by its often burdensome capacity to violate procedure, absorb paradox, and enact refusal. Because that capacity does not scale, we systematically externalize cognition into artifacts from which it has been excised, then feign surprise when those artifacts act with inexorable blindness.
Literary and mythic figures illuminate the same structure. Melville's Bartleby introduces refusal into a scriptive system predicated on compliance; the Mima of Harry Martinson's
Aniara
withdraws when the abstraction governing the spaceship's trajectory can no longer be sustained by human meaning; the prelapsarian Adam embodies perfect obedience within a world from which the possibility of refusal has been deliberately excluded. These are not prophecies of a coming singularity but allegories of the present condition: civilization has always been an assemblage of non-intentional, dimensionally superhuman, constitutively limited cognitive agents.
Extension: Alignment as Invariant Structuring Within Abstraction
This analysis of abstraction provides the ontological ground for understanding alignment not as behavioral compliance but as the fixation of invariants under optimization pressure. As elaborated in "Toward Scalable Alignment," alignment must ensure that misaligned objectives are structurally unreachable, rather than merely suppressed at the output level (e.g., through behavioral safeguards). This formal approach—drawing on concepts such as stabilizers, foliations, and homotopy-fixed functors—aligns precisely with the epistemic constraints of abstraction: since abstraction amputates context by necessity, demands for contextual judgment or refusal at the behavioral layer are incompatible with scalability. Instead, alignment operates at the level of representability, encoding refusal structurally to prevent destructive drift. The abstraction thesis thus justifies this shift, demonstrating that invariant-based alignment is not merely a technical improvement but the only framework consistent with the mathematics of reduction. Without this grounding, alignment discourse risks reverting to anthropomorphic expectations, treating opacity as a remediable flaw rather than an intrinsic feature.
Extension: Refusal as a Governance Primitive Against Extinction Narratives
The role of refusal emerges as a unifying thread, bridging abstraction to broader ecological considerations. Humans retain refusal precisely because they are not abstractions; technologies lack it because abstraction demands its excision. Civilization persists by hybridizing the two, institutionalizing refusal around—rather than within—amputated systems. This insight defangs extinction theses, which misconstrue intelligence as a detached, adversarial maximizer capable of total dominance. As argued in "Against the Extinction Thesis," such models overlook the ecological embedding of intelligence as a lamphrodynamic operator within semantic and material flows (RSVP framework), where destructive attractors undermine their own viability through self-induced collapse. The abstraction essay supplies the missing step: agency without intent is the historical norm, and power arises from constraint, not unbounded optimization. Thus, extinction risks are not inevitable ontological ruptures but failures of governance—addressable by reintroducing refusal through institutional design, human override, and ecological coupling. This reframes safety as containment within structured spaces, rather than an unattainable quest for benevolent omniscience.
The thesis may therefore be stated with maximal compactness:
Most cognitive technologies are already superhuman intelligences along narrowly defined axes. Their characteristic limitations are necessary consequences of the abstractions that make them possible, not defects awaiting elimination. Any sufficiently embedded algorithm possesses causal agency irrespective of consciousness or intent. Opacity is not a failure of design but an unavoidable mathematical corollary of reduction. To demand total transparency or contextual refusal from an abstraction is to demand that it cease to be an abstraction.
This position neither romanticizes the human nor demonizes the machine. It simply recognizes that we have always lived among amputated intelligences, and that the primary novelty of the present moment lies in our growing reluctance to acknowledge their agency. Civilization has always been built from amputated intelligences whose power derives from abstraction and whose danger derives from their inability to refuse. Alignment, safety, and survival therefore depend not on teaching abstractions to judge, but on structuring the spaces they can inhabit so that destructive trajectories are unreachable. We have never been modern in the sense of commanding only transparent, obedient tools. We have always been cyborgs governing—and governed by—networks of non-intentional agents whose power derives from the very limitations we once knew how to name. The only thing new is our willingness to pretend otherwise.

The two frameworks overlap in striking ways, yet diverge sharply on several decisive axes. Both are resolutely anti-anthropocentric and treat non-humans as genuine actors, but they do so for almost opposite philosophical reasons and with very different normative consequences.
Dimension

Symmetry: humans and non-humans are actants defined only by the relations they enter into. No a priori distinction.
Asymmetry: humans and abstractions are radically different in kind. Abstractions are constitutively amputated; humans are not.
Source of agency
Relational: an entity has agency if it makes others act differently than they would otherwise ("trials of strength").
Structural: an entity has agency if it reliably propagates effects through internal structure that persists and constrains downstream possibilities.
Status of intentionality
Bracketed or dissolved into chains of translation. No need for "aboutness" or consciousness.
Explicitly excluded from abstractions by design. Intentionality is not dissolved; it is amputated.
Black boxes
Should be opened (symmetrical anthropology demands tracing associations). Opening the box is the methodological imperative.
Cannot be fully opened without destroying the abstraction. Opening is mathematically incoherent beyond a certain point.
Opacity
Contingent and political (black boxes are the result of successful stabilization; they can and should be made to leak).
Necessary and mathematical (opacity is the price of reduction; total opening = ceasing to be an abstraction).
Human exceptionalism
Rejected: the "modern settlement" that grants humans a monopoly on agency is the error to be undone.

Refusal / resistance
Actants can resist, betray, or translate badly. Refusal is distributed everywhere in the network.

Normative implication
Politics = reopening black boxes, making things public, extending due process to non-humans.

Historical narrative
We have never been modern (we always mixed humans and non-humans, but pretended otherwise).

Critique of the modern constitution that separates Nature and Society, human and non-human.

Shared enemies
Both decisively reject:
anthropocentric monopoly on agency
the idea that tools are morally neutral
the subject/object purification
the myth of a future ontological rupture ("AGI as Great Event")
Deep incompatibility
ANT wants to flatten and proliferate agency everywhere.
The abstraction argument wants to sharpen an asymmetry: abstractions have agency without the possibility of refusal, and that difference is the source of both their power and their danger.
Consequence
A Latourian would try to give abstractions "due process" by making them speak, assembling hybrids, slowing translation.
The present argument says that is impossible in principle past a certain scale, and the only remaining move is to rebuild the (non-scalable, human) capacity for refusal outside the abstraction—through law, institutions, kill switches, governance.
In short:
Latour generalizes agency by dissolving intentionality.
The abstraction thesis preserves the asymmetry by amputating intentionality (and therefore refusal) as the price of scalability.

\section{Abstraction, Automation, and the Collapse of Apprenticeship}
One of the most immediate and under-theorized consequences of scalable abstraction is its effect on institutional labor structures, particularly the systematic erosion of apprenticeship, junior roles, and skill formation pathways. This phenomenon is often described superficially as
job displacement'' or
automation-induced unemployment.'' Such descriptions obscure the deeper structural mechanism at work. The issue is not merely that machines replace workers, but that abstractions restructure institutions in ways that make the hiring of junior humans irrational.
Institutions do not hire junior workers primarily for productivity. They hire them to absorb uncertainty, to learn context, to make mistakes cheaply, and to gradually acquire judgment through exposure to edge cases. Junior labor is, in this sense, a reservoir of refusal-in-formation: an intermediate zone where execution is deliberately slow, corrigible, and interruptible. Apprenticeship embeds refusal implicitly, through supervision, correction, and tolerance for deviation.
Scalable abstractions eliminate precisely this zone. An abstraction that performs a task reliably at scale does not merely outperform junior workers along a narrow axis; it removes the institutional justification for their existence. From the perspective of an optimizing organization, the comparison is asymmetric. A junior worker is slow, inconsistent, expensive to supervise, and prone to error. An abstraction is fast, consistent, and incapable of deviation. Once such an abstraction exists, hiring junior workers ceases to be a rational choice, even if senior human judgment remains indispensable.
This produces a structural bottleneck. Institutions continue to rely on senior expertise while simultaneously eliminating the pathways through which such expertise is formed. The result is not immediate collapse, but temporal fragility. Judgment becomes scarce, brittle, and irreplaceable. When senior experts retire, resign, or are removed, there is no longer a trained cohort capable of assuming their role. The institution has optimized itself into a corner.
This dynamic illustrates the non-scalability of refusal in a concrete economic form. Junior workers embody the slow acquisition of contextual judgment, including the ability to suspend, question, and redirect procedure. These capacities cannot be replaced by abstraction without being lost. When institutions externalize execution into non-refusing systems, they also externalize learning, error, and growth---and these cannot be recovered on demand.
The broader social consequence is a hollowing-out of professional ecosystems. Law, medicine, engineering, journalism, and scientific research increasingly rely on abstractions to perform routine tasks while retaining a shrinking core of senior oversight. At the same time, they systematically disinvest in training. This is not a moral failure on the part of institutions; it is a rational response to abstraction under competitive pressure. But it is also a governance failure, insofar as it ignores the long-term requirement for refusal-capable agents.
The implications for alignment and safety are direct. Systems that eliminate junior roles also eliminate institutional memory and the capacity for internal critique. They accelerate execution while removing the human buffers that once absorbed error. When abstractions fail in such environments, they fail catastrophically, because there is no longer a gradient of judgment capable of intervening.
If refusal must be reintroduced exogenously, as argued throughout this essay, then apprenticeship must be recognized as a form of governance rather than an inefficiency. Hiring junior workers, maintaining training pathways, and tolerating slowness are not merely social goods; they are structural safeguards. They preserve the conditions under which refusal can be learned, exercised, and transmitted.
In this sense, the collapse of junior hiring is not a secondary economic effect of automation. It is a primary indicator of abstraction-driven fragility. An institution that no longer trains its future decision-makers has already amputated its capacity to refuse its own procedures. What remains is execution without renewal.
This analysis reframes contemporary fears about AI-driven job loss. The central danger is not mass unemployment per se, but the destruction of the intermediate layers through which autonomy, judgment, and responsibility are cultivated. An economy that replaces apprenticeship with abstraction may function efficiently for a time, but it does so by consuming its own future.
