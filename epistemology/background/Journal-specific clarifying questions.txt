4. Information / Entropy / Complexity

(Entropy, Complex Systems, Information Theory audiences)

Unifying question:

> How can semantic structure arise and persist in entropy-driven systems without collapsing into mere compression?



Working title (indicative)

Semantic Stability as Entropy-Constrained Replay


---

Outline

1. Introduction: Intelligence Beyond Compression

This section motivates the insufficiency of classical information-theoretic compression as an account of intelligence or meaning. It frames the problem: entropy reduction alone cannot explain why certain structures persist, generalize, and support counterfactual reasoning. The core thesis is introduced: semantic organization arises from entropy-constrained replay, not from compression alone.

2. Events, Irreversibility, and Semantic Cost

Here the paper introduces events as irreversible commitments that carry thermodynamic and informational cost. Memory is framed as an entropy-bearing structure rather than a passive store. This establishes the physical asymmetry between construction and observation.

3. Replay as Information Preservation

This section develops deterministic replay as a mechanism for preserving semantic information across time. Replay is contrasted with lossy compression: while compression reduces description length, replay preserves causal and semantic structure under reconstruction.

4. Semantic Equivalence and Entropy Reduction

Abstraction is introduced as the stabilization of equivalence classes over replayable histories. The key distinction is made explicit: compression reduces entropy by discarding detail, whereas abstraction reduces entropy by identifying invariants. This section shows how equivalence collapse can reduce entropy without information loss at the semantic level.

5. Stability, Perturbation, and Semantic Basins

This section formalizes stability as resistance to perturbations in construction history. Semantic basins are introduced as regions of low effective entropy where meaning remains invariant under replay variations. Analogies to attractors and dissipative structures are developed carefully.

6. Phase Transitions in Semantic Organization

The paper argues that abstraction emerges via phase transitions rather than gradual compression. Below a threshold, semantic equivalence is unstable; above it, abstraction locks in. This is framed using concepts from statistical mechanics and complexity theory.

7. Relation to Biological and Artificial Systems

Without mentioning specific AI systems, this section explains why purely statistical learners plateau: they compress signals but do not generate stable semantic basins. Biological systems are framed as entropy-exporting structures that maintain semantic invariants.

8. Discussion: Entropy, Meaning, and Irreversibility

This section synthesizes the argument, emphasizing that meaning is not free: it requires entropy expenditure and structural stabilization. Intelligence is reframed as the ability to pay entropy costs to preserve semantic invariants.

9. Conclusion

The paper concludes by reframing intelligence as an entropy-regulated phenomenon, where abstraction is the maintenance of low-entropy semantic structure under replay.

This section introduces semantic objects and relations as first-class kernel entities. Equivalence collapse is presented as a semantic operation, not a garbage-collection or optimization step.

2. Events as Generators of Structure

Events are introduced as generators in a structured space. Rather than states, the focus is on morphisms and constructions. Time enters as an ordering constraint, not a dimension.

3. Replay as a Structural Constraint

Replay is formalized as a requirement that constructions be reconstructible. This section draws analogies to flat connections, holonomy, and path-independence without overcommitting to any single formalism.

This section develops an informal geometry of semantic space, where distance corresponds to divergence under replay and curvature corresponds to sensitivity to perturbation.

6. Stability as Topological Invariance

Stability is reframed as topological robustness: small perturbations do not change equivalence class. Connections to homotopy, stratification, and fiber bundles are discussed at a high level.

7. Phase Transitions and Structural Change

The emergence of abstraction is framed as a change in the topology of semantic space. This section discusses bifurcations, symmetry breaking, and regime shifts.

8. Relation to Physical and Computational Systems

The paper argues that semantic invariance appears in physical theories, biological systems, and computation when replay-like constraints exist.

9. Discussion: Structure Before Interpretation

This section emphasizes that semantics arises from structural constraints, not interpretation layered atop structure.

10. Conclusion

Meaning is reframed as a structural invariant. Replay is identified as the minimal condition for semantic geometry.
