Title: Abstraction as Stabilized Replay: Memory, Equivalence, and Semantic Depth

Authors: Flyxion (Author not explicitly mentioned)

Date: December 14, 2025

This paper presents a novel perspective on abstraction in artificial intelligence (AI) and cognitive theory, arguing against the common conflation of abstraction with compression or representation. The authors propose that true understanding emerges from stabilized semantic effects under variation rather than just data reduction or memorization.

  1. Conflations in AI and Cognitive Theory: The paper critiques existing views in AI and cognitive theory, which often equate abstraction with compression or representational economy. These conflations, the authors argue, obscure a deeper structural distinction between these concepts. Compression reduces construction costs by minimizing necessary events to build structures, while abstraction reduces distinct representatives needed for specific semantic outcomes under variation.

  2. Memory-First Framework: The authors propose a memory-first ontology where meaning is grounded in causally ordered events and abstraction emerges through equivalence under deterministic replay. This view contrasts with traditional symbol manipulation or expressive output models of reasoning, framing intelligence as organized construction rather than symbolic inference.

    • Events: An event is an irreversible commitment to semantic structure occurring at a unique position in causal order. It’s not merely observations or descriptions; it’s a constructive act.
    • Replay: This is the deterministic reconstruction of semantic state from initial conditions and ordered sequences of events. Replay is exact, not probabilistic, enabling tracing of semantic consequences to their origins.

  3. Equivalence and Abstraction: Abstraction arises when multiple distinct construction paths are treated as semantically interchangeable through equivalence relations induced by construction history. Semantically equivalent entities yield identical effects under all admissible continuations of the event history, constraining future interpretations without erasing historical information.

  4. Compression and Its Limits: While compression reduces description length or construction cost, it does not guarantee abstraction. A system can compress its history aggressively without introducing new equivalences, leading to efficient but brittle structures. Conversely, retaining long construction histories while stabilizing high-level equivalences enables robust generalization.

  5. Stability Under Variation: Abstraction corresponds to identifying stable semantic effects under replay – those preserved under a class of perturbations in event history. This stability is essential for abstraction, distinguishing it from mere compression.

  6. Memory, Explanation, and Non-Interference: In this memory-first framework, explanations are derived projections from memory rather than inputs to it. This separation allows understanding to remain grounded without corrupting reasoning through confabulation or self-deception.

  7. Why Representation is Insufficient: Representational systems manipulate compressed artifacts lacking intrinsic access to conditions maintaining their abstractions’ validity. Replay-based systems, however, can test the stability of abstractions by directly varying construction histories, revealing a qualitative difference between recognizing patterns and understanding structure.

  8. Semantic Depth and Generalization: Abstractions vary in depth – shallow ones capture surface regularities while deep ones reflect governing constraints across broad constructions. Semantic depth is revealed through replay: the more variations an abstraction survives, the deeper it becomes.

  9. Implications for Intelligence: This framework posits intelligence as the capacity to construct, stabilize, and reuse abstractions grounded in memory, not as accumulations of representations or optimized predictions. Systems lacking these properties may perform well but remain fragile, opaque, or ungrounded.

In conclusion, this paper distinguishes abstraction from compression and representational systems, proposing that true understanding emerges from stabilized semantic effects under replay in a memory-first ontology of events. This framework aims to explain both the successes and limitations of current intelligent systems while offering a foundation for future AI systems reasoning by construction rather than description.

  2. Parallelism (P): This principle measures how well a system can perform multiple tasks or processes simultaneously. High parallelism allows for complex computations and information processing to occur concurrently, enhancing the system’s overall capabilities.
