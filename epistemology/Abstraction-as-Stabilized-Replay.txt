Abstraction as Stabilized Replay:
Memory, Equivalence, and Semantic Depth
Flyxion
December 14, 2025
Abstract
Much contemporary work in artificial intelligence and cognitive theory conflates abstraction
with compression, and understanding with representation. This paper argues that such conflations
obscure a deeper structural distinction: compression reduces construction cost, while abstraction
stabilizes semantic effects under variation. We develop a memory-first framework in which
meaning is grounded in causally ordered events and abstraction emerges through equivalence
under deterministic replay. On this view, reasoning is not symbolic manipulation but controlled
reconstruction, and understanding corresponds to the stabilization of semantic outcomes across
counterfactual histories. The framework provides a substrate-neutral account of abstraction that
operates below language, explains the limits of purely representational systems, and clarifies the
structural conditions under which intelligence-like behavior becomes possible.

Introduction
The history of intelligence research is marked by a recurring ambiguity concerning the nature of
abstraction. At various times, abstraction has been identified with compression, with generalization,
with symbol manipulation, or with representational economy. Each of these notions captures an
important aspect of intelligent behavior, yet none adequately distinguishes abstraction from mere
data reduction or memorization.
This ambiguity has practical consequences. Systems that excel at compressing large corpora
of experience often fail to exhibit understanding, while systems that manipulate abstract symbols
frequently lack grounding. These failures suggest that abstraction is neither reducible to compression
nor fully captured by representation.
This paper advances a structural account of abstraction grounded in memory and replay. The
central claim is that abstraction consists in the stabilization of semantic effects under variation,
achieved through equivalence relations induced by construction history.
Compression reduces
the number of events required to build a structure; abstraction reduces the number of distinct
representatives required to produce a given semantic outcome. The two operations are orthogonal.
To articulate this distinction, we adopt a memory-first ontology in which events precede
representations, and reasoning consists in deterministic reconstruction rather than symbolic inference.
This perspective reframes intelligence as a property of organized construction rather than expressive
output.

Events, Time, and the Ontology of Memory
Any theory of abstraction presupposes an ontology of structure. In representational accounts, this
ontology is typically static: symbols, vectors, or propositions are taken as given, and abstraction is
defined over them. By contrast, a memory-first ontology treats structure as something that comes
into being through time.
Definition 2.1 (Event). An event is an irreversible commitment to semantic structure occurring at
a unique position in a causal order.
Events are not observations or descriptions; they are constructive acts. The semantic state of a
system at any moment is therefore not a snapshot in isolation, but the cumulative result of all prior
events. Memory, on this view, is not a storage medium but a causal artifact.
Time plays a constitutive role. Because events are ordered, semantic state is inherently temporal.
Any account of meaning that ignores construction history necessarily discards information relevant
to abstraction.
3
Replay as a Model of Reasoning
If semantic state is defined by event history, then reasoning cannot consist merely in forward
execution. Instead, reasoning must involve the ability to reconstruct, inspect, and vary construction
histories.
Definition 3.1 (Replay). Replay is the deterministic reconstruction of semantic state from an
initial condition and an ordered sequence of events.
Replay is not simulation in the probabilistic sense; it is exact reconstruction. Its determinism
guarantees that identical histories yield identical states, while its inspectability allows semantic
consequences to be traced to their origins.
This conception of reasoning differs fundamentally from inference over representations. Rather
than manipulating symbols according to syntactic rules, the system re-instantiates the very processes
that produced meaning. Counterfactual reasoning becomes possible by replaying modified histories,
not by hypothesizing over abstract models.

Equivalence and the Emergence of Abstraction
Abstraction arises when multiple distinct construction paths are treated as semantically inter-
changeable. This interchangeability is formalized through equivalence relations over constructed
entities.
Definition 4.1 (Semantic Equivalence). Two entities are semantically equivalent if, under all
admissible continuations of the event history, they induce identical semantic effects.
Equivalence does not erase history; it constrains future interpretation. When an equivalence
relation is introduced, the system commits to treating certain distinctions as irrelevant for subsequent
semantic purposes, while retaining the ability to recover their origins through replay.
2

Proposition 4.1. Equivalence induces abstraction by reducing the number of representatives required
to realize a class of semantic effects.
This reduction is the essence of abstraction. Importantly, it operates independently of com-
pression. One may introduce equivalence without shortening the event log, and one may compress
construction without introducing new equivalences.
5
Compression and Its Limits
Compression reduces description length or construction cost. In event-based systems, this corresponds
to reducing the number of events required to reach a given state. Compression is undeniably valuable:
it enables efficiency, scalability, and tractability.
However, compression alone does not yield abstraction. A system may compress its history
aggressively while preserving every semantic distinction, resulting in a compact but brittle structure.
Conversely, a system may retain a long construction history while stabilizing high-level equivalences
that enable robust generalization.
This distinction clarifies why systems trained solely to compress observed data often fail to
exhibit understanding. They reduce surface complexity without stabilizing semantic invariants.
6
Stability Under Variation
The hallmark of abstraction is stability. An abstract concept is one whose semantic consequences
remain invariant under a range of variations in construction history or presentation.
Definition 6.1 (Semantic Stability). A semantic effect is stable if it is preserved under a nontrivial
class of perturbations to the event history.
Stability is not absolute invariance; it is invariance relative to a class of admissible varia-
tions. These variations may include reordering of independent events, substitution of equivalent
substructures, or changes in representational form.
Theorem 6.1. Abstraction corresponds to the identification of stable semantic effects under replay.
Sketch. Replay deterministically maps event histories to semantic states. If multiple histories map
to states that induce identical semantic effects, an equivalence class over those histories can be
formed. Stabilizing this equivalence yields abstraction.
7
Memory, Explanation, and Non-Interference
A memory-first framework distinguishes sharply between causal structure and explanation. Expla-
nation is a projection from memory, not an input to it. As a result, explanations may vary without
altering semantic truth.
This separation resolves a fundamental tension in intelligent systems: the need to generate
explanations without corrupting reasoning. When explanation feeds back into state, systems become

susceptible to confabulation and self-deception. When explanation is derived but causally inert,
understanding remains grounded.
8
Why Representation Is Insufficient
Representational systems operate on summaries of construction rather than on construction itself.
They manipulate compressed artifacts whose semantic grounding lies elsewhere. While such systems
may approximate abstraction behaviorally, they lack intrinsic access to the conditions under which
their abstractions remain valid.
By contrast, a replay-based system can test the stability of its abstractions by varying construction
histories directly. It can determine not merely whether a pattern recurs, but whether it survives
perturbation.
This capacity marks a qualitative difference between systems that recognize regularities and
systems that understand structure.
9
Semantic Depth and Generalization
Abstractions differ not only in scope but in depth. Shallow abstractions capture surface regularities;
deep abstractions capture constraints that govern wide classes of constructions.
Definition 9.1 (Semantic Depth). The semantic depth of an abstraction is the measure of how
many independent variations it remains invariant under.
Depth is revealed through replay: the more variations a semantic effect survives, the deeper
the abstraction. This notion aligns generalization with structural robustness rather than statistical
frequency.
10
Implications for Intelligence
On the account developed here, intelligence is not the accumulation of representations nor the
optimization of predictions. It is the capacity to construct, stabilize, and reuse abstractions grounded
in memory.
Systems exhibiting intelligence-like behavior do so because they maintain structures that support
replay, equivalence, and counterfactual variation. Systems lacking these properties may achieve
impressive performance yet remain fragile, opaque, or ungrounded.
This framework suggests that intelligence emerges not from scale alone, but from the organization
of memory and the disciplined management of abstraction.
11
Conclusion
This paper has argued that abstraction is neither synonymous with compression nor reducible to
representation. Instead, abstraction emerges from the stabilization of semantic effects under replay,
grounded in a memory-first ontology of events.
4

By distinguishing compression from abstraction, and reasoning from narration, we obtain a
framework capable of explaining both the successes and limitations of contemporary intelligent
systems. More importantly, we recover a structural notion of understanding that operates below
language and representation, offering a foundation for future systems that reason by construction
rather than description.
