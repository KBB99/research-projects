Spherepop OS:
A Deterministic Semantic Operating System
Formal Specification, Architectural Rationale, and Foundations of Pre-Linguistic Intelligence
Flyxion

Abstract
Spherepop OS is a deterministic semantic operating system whose primary abstraction is
neither the process, nor the file, nor the thread, but an append-only, causally ordered event
substrate from which all semantic state is derived. The system is designed to support collaborative,
time-aware, and introspectable computation by enforcing strict replayability, total causal order,
and a principled separation between authoritative state transitions and non-authoritative views.
This paper presents a comprehensive formal specification of Spherepop OS together with the
architectural rationale underlying each design decision. Beyond operating-system concerns, the
paper situates Spherepop OS within a broader theoretical context, arguing that its event-sourced
semantics instantiate foundational principles of intelligence articulated by Yi Ma and others:
parsimony, self-consistency, and abstraction grounded in structure rather than language.
By distinguishing compression as reduction in causal construction from abstraction as
reduction in semantic representatives, Spherepop OS resolves a longstanding ambiguity in
the theory of intelligence. The resulting architecture functions as a pre-linguistic semantic
substrate, positioned at the level of animal cognition rather than symbolic reasoning. In this
sense, Spherepop OS occupies an intermediate position between an operating system kernel, a
distributed database, and a field-theoretic model of intelligence grounded in time, construction,
and equivalence.
1
Motivation and Design Orientation
Modern operating systems are historically shaped by assumptions that no longer hold in collaborative,
distributed, or semantically rich computational environments.
The dominant abstractions of
processes, address spaces, and mutable global state emerged in an era where computation was
primarily local, sequential, and opaque. While these abstractions have proven effective for managing
hardware resources, they impose severe limitations on reproducibility, introspection, and principled
reasoning about time.
In particular, traditional systems make it difficult to answer questions such as: What exactly
happened to produce the current state? Can that state be reconstructed independently? Can multiple
observers reason about the same history without interfering with one another? These difficulties
1

are not accidental but structural, arising from the use of mutable state, implicit causality, and
side-effect-laden control flow.
Spherepop OS is motivated by the conviction that these limitations can only be resolved
by elevating time and construction to first-class concepts. Rather than treating history as an
implementation detail, Spherepop OS treats it as the sole source of authority. All semantic state
exists only as the deterministic consequence of replaying an immutable, totally ordered event log.
This orientation aligns with a deeper theoretical perspective on intelligence. As emphasized by Yi
Ma, intelligent systems are governed by two foundational principles: parsimony and self-consistency.
Parsimony demands that the system explain the world using as little structure as necessary, while
self-consistency demands that the resulting model be rich enough to reliably recreate and simulate
its environment. Spherepop OS embeds these principles architecturally rather than heuristically:
every unnecessary construction leaves a permanent trace, and every inconsistency is exposed through
replay.
The design goals of Spherepop OS therefore follow not from performance considerations but
from epistemic ones. The system is designed to guarantee deterministic replay, enforce a single
global causal order, separate causes from observations, privilege semantic relations over procedural
artifacts, and support collaborative introspection without destabilization. These commitments
collectively rule out large classes of conventional kernel, database, and version-control architectures,
motivating the log-centric design developed in the sections that follow.

Core Ontology
The ontology of Spherepop OS is intentionally minimal. Rather than introducing a rich taxonomy
of object types, the system defines a small set of primitives from which all higher-level structure is
derived.
Definition 2.1 (Semantic Object). A semantic object is an abstract entity identified by a stable
handle. Semantic objects possess no intrinsic meaning, internal state, or behavior. Their significance
arises solely through participation in relations, equivalence classes, and events.
This definition reflects a deliberate refusal to ascribe semantics at the object level. Objects are
not records, files, or processes; they are structural placeholders whose interpretation emerges only
through context.
Definition 2.2 (Relation). A relation is a typed, directed association between two semantic objects.
Relations are first-class entities and may carry auxiliary metadata, but their semantic force derives
from their position within the relational structure induced by the event log.
Definition 2.3 (Equivalence). An equivalence relation over semantic objects identifies multiple
handles as representing a single canonical representative. Equivalence is induced explicitly by merge
events and maintained via a union-find structure during replay.
Crucially, none of these entities exist independently of the event log. There is no mutable object
table, no hidden registry of relations, and no authoritative state outside replay. This ensures that
the ontology of the system is entirely historical: to exist is to have been constructed.

The Authoritative Event Log
Axiom 3.1 (Append-Only Authority). All authoritative state transitions in Spherepop OS are
recorded as events in a single append-only log. No other structure is permitted to introduce, modify,
or erase semantic state.
Each event is assigned a strictly increasing event identifier by a single arbiter, establishing a
total causal order over all state transitions. This total order is not an emergent property but an
enforced invariant.
Proposition 3.1 (Deterministic State Reconstruction). Given an initial empty state and any finite
prefix of the event log, the resulting semantic state is uniquely determined.
Proof. All kernel transitions are pure functions of prior state and the next event in the log. Since
the log is immutable and totally ordered, replay yields a unique result.
This proposition elevates the event log from a storage mechanism to the primary semantic
artifact of the system. The log is not merely a record of what happened; it is what happened.
In the context of intelligence theory, this construction has a significant implication. Compression
in Spherepop OS is not an abstract information measure but a concrete reduction in causal
construction. Abstraction, as will be shown later, is not symbolic generalization but structural
equivalence over constructed entities. Both are directly visible in the log, rendering the system
introspectable in a way that purely statistical models are not.

Kernel Semantics and Deterministic Execution
At the heart of Spherepop OS lies a deliberately minimal kernel.
Unlike traditional kernels,
which manage processes, memory, and scheduling, the Spherepop kernel performs a single task: it
deterministically interprets the authoritative event log. All higher-level phenomena—state, structure,
abstraction, and even time—emerge from this interpretation.
Definition 4.1 (Kernel). The kernel is the deterministic transition function that maps an event-log
prefix to a semantic state. It possesses no mutable internal state beyond that which is derivable from
replay, and it produces no side effects beyond derived views.
This definition is intentionally austere. The kernel is not a scheduler, a memory manager, or
a policy engine. It is a semantic interpreter whose sole responsibility is to ensure that identical
histories yield identical worlds.
This design enforces self-consistency in the strongest possible sense. Because the kernel is pure,
replayable, and free of hidden state, the system is always capable of reconstructing its own present
from first principles. There is no distinction between how the state was reached and what the state
is; the former fully determines the latter.

Derived State and Non-Authority
Although the kernel maintains derived structures—such as equivalence classes, relation tables, and
metadata maps—these structures are explicitly non-authoritative. They exist only as caches that
accelerate observation. At any time, they may be discarded and reconstructed by replay.
This distinction is essential. In conventional systems, caches frequently become sources of truth
through optimization or necessity. In Spherepop OS, by contrast, authority is rigidly centralized in
the log. The kernel is therefore immune to a wide class of synchronization errors and semantic drift.

4.4
Determinism and Semantic Stability
The operational semantics above satisfy strong determinism guarantees.
Theorem 4.1 (Kernel Determinism). For any kernel state σ and event e, there exists at most one
state σ′ such that σ e−→σ′.
This theorem underwrites every higher-level property of Spherepop OS. It ensures that observers,
regardless of vantage point or reconstruction method, converge on the same semantic world when
given the same history.
From the perspective articulated by Yi Ma, this determinism is not merely a technical convenience
but a prerequisite for intelligence. A system that cannot reconstruct its own world consistently
cannot form reliable models of that world. By embedding determinism into its core, Spherepop OS
enforces self-consistency as an architectural invariant rather than a learned behavior.

Observation, Diffs, and Non-Interference
While the authoritative event log determines all semantic state, a practical system must permit
observation without compromising determinism. Spherepop OS resolves this tension by introducing
a strict separation between authoritative causation and non-authoritative observation.
Definition 5.1 (Diff). A diff is a derived, non-authoritative description of the semantic change
induced by applying a single event to a kernel state.
Diffs are emitted by the kernel as observational artifacts. They are never themselves logged,
never replayed, and never consulted during future kernel execution. Their sole purpose is to permit
observers to maintain an incremental view of semantic state without replaying the entire log.
This separation is enforced by construction rather than convention. The kernel computes diffs as
a pure function of state transition, but diffs have no causal power. They may be dropped, delayed,
reordered, or ignored by observers without affecting the correctness of the system as a whole.
Axiom 5.1 (Observational Non-Interference). Diffs have no effect on authoritative state and do
not influence future kernel execution.
This axiom eliminates an entire class of feedback-induced inconsistencies that plague conventional
systems. Observation is rendered epistemic rather than ontological: it reveals what has occurred
without altering what can occur.

Seek operations instantiate temporary kernel instances that are isolated from the live system.
They permit historical inspection, debugging, and counterfactual reasoning without side effects.
Axiom 7.1 (Temporal Isolation). Seek operations produce no modifications to authoritative state
and emit no diffs.
Corollary 7.1. Historical inspection cannot influence present or future semantic state.

Self-Consistency and World Simulation
The architectural commitments described above jointly enforce a strong form of self-consistency. At
any moment, the system can reconstruct its entire semantic state from first principles by replaying
the event log.
Theorem 8.1 (Strong Self-Consistency). For any time t, the semantic state of Spherepop OS is
uniquely determined by the event log prefix [1..t].
This property directly instantiates the second foundational principle of intelligence articulated
by Yi Ma: a world model must be consistent enough to support accurate simulation. In Spherepop
OS, simulation is not an approximation layered atop mutable state; it is an intrinsic consequence of
replay.
Because seek operations permit reconstruction of past states, and speculative branches (intro-
duced in the next section) permit exploration of possible futures, the system possesses an internal
model of its own temporal evolution. This model operates below language and symbolism. It is
not a narrative or a description, but a constructive history that can be replayed, inspected, and
extended.
In this sense, Spherepop OS implements self-consistency not as a learning objective but as a
structural invariant. Any deviation from consistency would manifest immediately as replay failure
or divergence, rendering the system epistemically unstable. By design, such instability is impossible.

Speculative Branches and Counterfactual Evaluation
An intelligent system must be capable not only of reconstructing its past but of reasoning about
possible futures. Spherepop OS addresses this requirement through the introduction of speculative
branches, which permit counterfactual exploration without compromising causal integrity.
Definition 9.1 (Speculative Branch). A speculative branch is defined by a base event identifier k
together with a client-local sequence of hypothetical events that are evaluated against the authoritative
history but not committed to it.
Speculative branches are evaluated by replaying the authoritative log prefix [1..k] and then
applying the speculative events in sequence. Crucially, this process uses the same deterministic
kernel as authoritative execution. Speculation is therefore not a heuristic approximation but a
faithful simulation of what would occur if the proposed events were to be accepted.
Axiom 9.1 (Speculative Isolation). Speculative branches have no effect on authoritative state unless
their events are explicitly validated and sequenced by the arbiter.
This axiom ensures that counterfactual reasoning remains epistemic rather than ontological.
Speculation explores possibilities without altering reality.
Proposition 9.1 (Counterfactual Soundness). Speculative evaluation preserves semantic consistency
with the base history.

Proof. Speculative execution reuses the same deterministic transition rules as authoritative replay.
Since speculative events are isolated, they cannot introduce contradictions into the authoritative

From the standpoint of intelligence theory, speculative branching plays a role analogous to

Compression and Abstraction as Distinct Structural Operations
A recurring theme in contemporary discussions of artificial intelligence is the ambiguity between
compression and abstraction. Yi Ma has emphasized this ambiguity as a central open problem,
asking what distinguishes mere data compression from genuine understanding.
Spherepop OS provides a structural resolution to this question by making both operations
explicit and orthogonal within the event-derived semantic substrate.
10.1
Compression as Reduction in Causal Construction
In Spherepop OS, compression is defined operationally rather than statistically.
Definition 10.1 (Compression). Compression is the reduction in the number of events required to
construct a given semantic state.
Compression is therefore visible directly in the event log. Two histories that yield identical
semantic states but differ in event count exhibit different degrees of compression. Reuse of existing
objects, avoidance of redundant constructions, and deliberate minimization of causal steps all
manifest as shorter logs.
This notion of compression aligns with parsimony as articulated by Yi Ma. The system is forced
to confront the cost of every construction: unnecessary operations leave permanent traces in the log.
There is no opportunity to retroactively erase inefficiency.
10.2
Abstraction as Reduction in Semantic Representatives
Abstraction, by contrast, operates on a different structural dimension.
Definition 10.2 (Abstraction). Abstraction is the reduction in the number of distinct semantic
representatives required to produce a given semantic effect.
Abstraction is realized in Spherepop OS through equivalence operations. MERGE and COL-
LAPSE events identify multiple constructed objects as representing the same underlying entity.
Importantly, abstraction does not remove events from history; it reinterprets their consequences by
reducing the number of distinct representatives that matter going forward.
Proposition 10.1 (Orthogonality of Compression and Abstraction). Compression and abstraction
are independent structural operations. One may increase without increasing the other.
9

Proof. A system may reuse a single object many times, achieving high compression with minimal
abstraction. Conversely, a system may construct many distinct objects and later collapse them into
a single representative, achieving high abstraction without reducing event count. The operations
act on different aspects of the event-derived structure.
This distinction resolves Yi Ma's question without appealing to semantics or behavior. Com-
pression concerns how much happened; abstraction concerns how many distinct things matter. Both
are visible in the log, and neither subsumes the other.

Avoiding the Language Compression Trap
Yi Ma has argued that contemporary large language models operate primarily by compressing
already-compressed signals. Natural language itself is the product of extensive abstraction and
parsimony, encoding generations of world modeling into symbolic form. Models trained exclusively
on text therefore inherit abstraction without participating in its formation.
Spherepop OS avoids this trap by operating below language. Its primitives precede symbols,
syntax, and narration. Objects are not words, relations are not predicates, and equivalence is not
semantic synonymy but structural identification.
Theorem 11.1 (Pre-Linguistic Abstraction). All abstraction in Spherepop OS arises from explicit
equivalence operations over constructed entities rather than from symbolic compression.
Proof. Equivalence is induced only by MERGE and COLLAPSE events applied to objects that
have been constructed through prior events. No symbolic encoding or linguistic representation is
required.
This positions Spherepop OS at the level of intelligence Yi Ma associates with animals and
humans prior to language: a world model built through interaction, regularity, and structural
consolidation rather than textual description.

Geometry, Layout, and Gauge Freedom
Spherepop OS enforces a strict conceptual separation between semantic structure and geometric
presentation. This separation is not a matter of implementation convenience but a foundational
design principle intended to prevent accidental entanglement between meaning and visualization.
Geometric information—such as spatial layout, clustering, or visual proximity—is treated as
non-causal metadata. Observers may attach, modify, or discard such information freely without
affecting semantic state.
Definition 13.1 (Layout Hint). A layout hint is non-authoritative metadata associated with a
semantic object that proposes a geometric or visual interpretation of that object.
Layout hints are never consulted by the kernel during event interpretation. They do not influence
equivalence, relations, or object existence. As such, they commute with all kernel operations.
Axiom 13.1 (Geometric Gauge Freedom). For any two assignments of layout hints, replaying the
same event log yields identical semantic state.
This axiom parallels gauge freedom in physics: multiple geometric representations may correspond
to the same underlying structure. The semantic content of the system is invariant under changes of
layout gauge.
This distinction is essential for collaborative environments. Different observers may adopt
radically different visualizations suited to their tasks, cognitive preferences, or display constraints,
while remaining in perfect semantic agreement.

Observers in Spherepop OS are epistemic agents rather than causal actors. They receive information
about state transitions but do not participate in authoritative execution.
Definition 14.1 (Observer). An observer is an external process that receives diffs derived from
kernel state transitions and maintains a local semantic view.
Observers are passive with respect to causation. They cannot modify kernel state directly and
cannot introduce events into the authoritative log.
All diffs are delivered to observers in strictly increasing event order. This delivery discipline
ensures that every observer witnesses a causally consistent history.
Proposition 14.1 (Observer Convergence). Any two observers that process the same prefix of diffs
converge to semantically equivalent states.

Arbiter and Collaborative Commit Model
While observation is decentralized, authority in Spherepop OS is centralized by design.
Definition 15.1 (Arbiter). The arbiter is the sole entity authorized to append events to the
authoritative log.
Clients submit proposals to the arbiter, which validates them against the current kernel state
and, if accepted, assigns them sequence identifiers and commits them to the log.
Axiom 15.1 (Single Sequencer). There exists exactly one arbiter per authoritative log.
This axiom enforces total causal order. Unlike distributed consensus systems that permit partial
ordering or eventual convergence, Spherepop OS commits to a single, linear history.
Proposition 15.1 (Proposal Determinism). Given identical kernel state and proposal content, the
arbiter produces the same canonical event or rejection.
This property ensures fairness, reproducibility, and auditability. The arbiter is not a policy
engine but a deterministic mediator of semantic commitment.

Rationale and Comparative Perspective
Spherepop OS occupies a position that is difficult to classify within existing computational paradigms.
It is not a conventional operating system in the POSIX sense, as it does not manage hardware
resources or schedule processes. It is not a database, as it does not support arbitrary mutation or
query-driven updates. It is not a version control system, as speculation is continuous rather than
episodic.
Instead, Spherepop OS is best understood as an operating system for semantic time. It manages
the construction, identification, and stabilization of meaning over time through explicit events,
equivalence, and replay.
In contrast to language-centric artificial intelligence systems, Spherepop OS does not begin with
symbols. It begins with construction. Meaning emerges only after structure has been assembled,
reused, and abstracted. This architectural choice aligns Spherepop OS with theories of intelligence
that locate understanding in interaction and structure rather than in linguistic compression.
ectionAccumulation Regimes and the Stratification of Intelligence
A central contribution of Spherepop OS is that it renders visible, within a single formal substrate,
multiple distinct regimes of knowledge accumulation that are often conflated in both artificial
intelligence and cognitive science. These regimes correspond closely to the stratification identified
by Yi Ma—phylogenetic, ontogenetic, social, and abstract—but are expressed here in strictly
operational and architectural terms.
The key observation is that accumulation is not a unitary process. Different forms of intelligence
arise from different mechanisms of compression, stabilization, and reuse, each operating on different
timescales and with different degrees of internal autonomy. Spherepop OS provides a framework
in which these differences are not merely conceptual but structurally enforced and empirically
observable.
17.1
Phylogenetic Accumulation as Log Externalization
Phylogenetic accumulation corresponds to the slow incorporation of environmental regularities into
the structure of an organism through evolutionary processes. In informational terms, this is a brutal
and highly lossy form of compression, mediated by selection rather than inference.
Within the Spherepop OS framework, phylogenetic accumulation is best understood as log
externalization. Structural commitments are fixed outside the active semantic substrate and appear
to the system as immutable primitives. The system does not record the evolutionary history that
produced these structures; it merely inherits them as given.
This regime is characterized by the absence of introspectable construction history. The reasons
for a structure's existence are not available within the system itself. As a result, phylogenetic
knowledge is maximally stable but minimally revisable. It supports survival but not explanation.
Spherepop OS deliberately does not model this regime internally. Instead, it assumes a fixed
kernel and primitive operation set, mirroring the way biological organisms inherit neural architectures
without access to their evolutionary derivation.
13

17.2
Ontogenetic Accumulation as Event-Sourced Construction
Ontogenetic accumulation corresponds to individual learning through interaction with the environ-
ment. This is the regime in which Yi Ma locates animal and human intelligence at its core.
Spherepop OS is explicitly designed to operate at this level. Ontogenetic learning is modeled as
the accumulation of events in an authoritative log, with semantic objects, relations, and equivalence
classes emerging through replay. Crucially, this accumulation is internally driven. While external
inputs may trigger events, the structural consequences of those events are determined by the system's
existing semantic organization.
Compression at this level is not mere reduction of data volume. It is the progressive elimination
of redundant constructions through equivalence, reuse, and abstraction. The system learns not by
memorizing observations but by stabilizing invariants across events.
Because the full construction history remains available, ontogenetic knowledge in Spherepop
OS is introspectable, revisable, and replayable. This property sharply distinguishes it from both
evolutionary inheritance and static database storage.
17.3
Social Accumulation as Shared Log Alignment
Social accumulation arises when multiple systems align their semantic substrates through shared
symbols, conventions, and artifacts. Language, writing, and institutions belong to this regime.
From the perspective of Spherepop OS, social knowledge corresponds to partial log alignment
across agents. Shared abstractions emerge when distinct event histories induce compatible equivalence
classes and relations. Importantly, this does not require identical histories, only compatible semantic
commitments.
This framing reveals why social knowledge is powerful but fragile. Because it operates above
individual ontogenetic logs, it depends on continuous maintenance, interpretation, and repair.
Misalignment does not appear as a logical contradiction but as semantic drift.
Spherepop OS supports this regime by design through its observer model. Multiple observers
may maintain divergent views, layouts, and speculative branches while remaining anchored to a
common authoritative core.
17.4
Abstraction and the Transition to Formal Knowledge
The highest regime identified by Yi Ma is the transition from empirical compression to abstract,
deductive knowledge—science and mathematics. This transition represents a qualitative shift rather
than a quantitative extension.
In Spherepop OS terms, this corresponds to the emergence of abstraction layers that operate
primarily on representatives rather than on raw events. Once equivalence classes stabilize, reasoning
can proceed by manipulating canonical objects without revisiting their construction histories.
This transition is visible in the log as a change in event character. Early logs are dominated by
object creation and relation formation. Mature logs exhibit a preponderance of merges, collapses,

This distinction explains why large language models, which operate primarily on already-

construction history.
Spherepop OS, by contrast, operates below language. It records the formation of meaning itself,
rather than its symbolic residue. In doing so, it occupies the pre-linguistic substrate of intelligence

Formal Distinction Between Compression and Abstraction
This appendix formalizes a distinction that is central to the theoretical claims of the paper: the
difference between compression and abstraction. While these terms are often used interchangeably
in the literature, they correspond to distinct structural transformations in Spherepop OS.
D.1
Preliminaries
Let ℓ= (e1, . . . , en) be a prefix of the authoritative event log, and let σ(ℓ) denote the kernel state
obtained by replaying ℓfrom the empty initial state.
Let |ℓ| denote the length of the log prefix, and let |O(ℓ)| denote the number of object identifiers
present in σ(ℓ). Let |U(ℓ)| denote the number of equivalence classes induced by the union- find
structure.
D.2
Compression as Event Reduction
Definition D.1 (Semantic Effect). A semantic effect is an equivalence class of kernel states under
observational equivalence, where two states are observationally equivalent if they induce the same
canonical representatives and relations.
Definition D.2 (Compression). Compression is the process of reducing the minimal log length
required to produce a given semantic effect.
Formally, compression occurs when there exists a log prefix ℓ′ such that σ(ℓ′) is observationally
equivalent to σ(ℓ) and |ℓ′| < |ℓ|.
Compression therefore operates on the temporal dimension of construction. It removes redundant
or unnecessary events while preserving semantic outcomes.
D.3
Abstraction as Representative Reduction
Definition D.3 (Abstraction). Abstraction is the process of reducing the number of distinct canonical
representatives required to account for a semantic effect.
Formally, abstraction occurs when, for a fixed semantic effect, |U(ℓ′)| < |U(ℓ)|, typically as the
result of merge or collapse events.
Abstraction operates on the structural dimension of the semantic field. It does not shorten the
log but simplifies the ontology induced by the log.
18

D.4
Independence of Compression and Abstraction
Proposition D.1. Compression and abstraction are logically independent operations.
Proof. Compression may occur without abstraction by removing redundant events that introduce
objects which are later unused, without altering equivalence classes. Conversely, abstraction may
occur without compression when merge events increase log length while reducing the number of
representatives.
Therefore, neither operation implies the other.
D.5
Visibility in the Log
Proposition D.2. The distinction between compression and abstraction is observable in the event
log and kernel state.
Proof. Compression is observable as a reduction in minimal log prefixes producing a given semantic
effect. Abstraction is observable as a reduction in the cardinality of equivalence classes in σ(ℓ).
Both quantities are explicitly computable from the log and kernel state.
D.6
Implications for Learning Systems
This formal distinction explains why systems that optimize for compression alone may fail to achieve
abstraction. Statistical models that reduce data volume without altering underlying representational
commitments compress signals without restructuring semantics.
By contrast, abstraction requires the system to identify and stabilize equivalences across contexts,
an operation that cannot be performed solely at the level of surface compression.
Spherepop OS enforces this distinction architecturally, ensuring that abstraction remains
grounded in replayable construction rather than in opaque parameter adjustment.
