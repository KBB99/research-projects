Replay and Invariance:
Toward a Structural Geometry of Semantic Systems
Flyxion
December 14, 2025
Abstract
Modern physics and mathematics are unified not by ontology but by invariance. Physical laws,
geometric structures, and mathematical objects are defined by what remains unchanged under
admissible transformations. This paper develops a parallel structural framework for semantic
systems, grounded in the notion of replayable construction histories. We treat replay as an
axiomatic constraint analogous to path-independence or flatness conditions in geometry, and
abstraction as the formation of equivalence classes invariant under replay. Without appealing to
representational semantics or physical interpretation, we show that semantic stability admits a
geometric description in terms of quotient structures, invariants, and structural phase transitions.
The resulting framework positions meaning as an emergent invariant of construction, aligning
semantic systems with the structural core of modern mathematics and physics.
1
Introduction
Across physics and mathematics, explanatory power arises not from enumerating objects but from
identifying invariants. From classical mechanics to gauge theory, from topology to algebra, what
distinguishes structure from accident is invariance under transformation. Meaningful structure
persists; noise does not.
Semantic systems—whether scientific theories, mathematical formalisms, or computational
constructions—exhibit a similar distinction. Some differences matter; others do not. Yet unlike
physical systems, semantics has traditionally been treated as interpretive or representational rather
than structural.
This paper advances a different position: semantic structure can be treated geometrically, as an
invariant arising from replayable construction histories. Replay is not a metaphor for time reversal
or simulation; it is an axiomatic constraint ensuring that meaning is defined by necessity rather
than convenience. Abstraction, on this view, is not compression or symbol manipulation, but the
formation of equivalence classes invariant under replay.
The goal is not to reduce semantics to physics, but to show that both obey the same structural
logic.

Structural Invariance as a Unifying Principle
In mathematics, an object is defined by its invariants under a group of transformations. In physics,
laws are constraints invariant under changes of frame, gauge, or coordinates. In both cases, structure
is identified with stability under admissible transformations.
Definition 2.1. An invariant is a property preserved under a specified class of transformations.
The choice of admissible transformations is not arbitrary; it encodes the theory's commitments.
Euclidean geometry permits rigid motions; gauge theories permit local phase shifts; topology permits
continuous deformation.
Semantic systems likewise admit transformations: refactorings, re-derivations, alternative con-
structions. The question is which of these transformations preserve meaning.
3
Replay as an Axiomatic Constraint
We introduce replay not as a dynamical process but as a structural axiom.
Axiom 3.1. All admissible semantic transformations must preserve replayability of construction
histories.
Replayability asserts that a construction can be re-instantiated from its generative history
without ambiguity. This axiom plays a role analogous to integrability conditions in differential
geometry or path-independence in conservative systems.
Proposition 3.1. Replayability enforces a notion of semantic flatness: equivalent constructions
yield identical consequences under all admissible replays.
This flatness does not imply triviality. Just as flat connections may admit rich global structure,
replayable systems may support deep abstraction.
4
Equivalence Classes and Quotient Structure
Abstraction arises when distinct constructions are identified as semantically equivalent. Structurally,
this corresponds to forming a quotient space.
Definition 4.1. A semantic equivalence class is a set of construction histories whose replayed
consequences are identical under all admissible perturbations.
The space of constructions is partitioned into equivalence classes. The abstract object is not any
representative construction, but the class itself.
Proposition 4.1. Semantic quotienting reduces degrees of freedom while preserving invariant
structure.
This mirrors familiar constructions in mathematics, where quotienting by symmetry removes
redundancy while revealing essential form.
Corollary 4.1. Abstraction is neither lossless nor arbitrary: it is constrained by the requirement of
replay invariance.

Geometry of Semantic Space
Although semantic spaces need not be manifolds, they admit geometric intuition. Distance may be
defined operationally as divergence under replay; curvature as sensitivity to perturbation; stability
as resistance to equivalence class fragmentation.
Definition 5.1. Semantic distance between two constructions is the minimal perturbation under
which their replayed consequences diverge.
Regions of low curvature correspond to robust abstractions; regions of high curvature indicate
unstable or context-dependent meaning.
This geometry is not imposed but induced by replay constraints.
6
Phase Transitions and Structural Change
In many systems, abstraction appears suddenly rather than gradually. Structurally, this corresponds
to a phase transition in equivalence structure.
Proposition 6.1. A semantic phase transition occurs when the topology of equivalence classes
changes discontinuously under continuous variation of construction parameters.
Examples include the emergence of conserved quantities, the stabilization of mathematical
formalisms, or the crystallization of scientific theories.
Such transitions are structural, not psychological or sociological.

Relation to Physical Theories
The framework developed here is deliberately interpretation-agnostic. Nevertheless, it resonates
with several physical notions.
Replay resembles reversibility constraints but does not require time symmetry. Semantic flatness
parallels gauge invariance without invoking fields. Quotienting echoes symmetry reduction without
presupposing physical symmetry groups.
Proposition 7.1. Semantic invariance is orthogonal to physical ontology: it constrains structure,
not substance.
This allows semantic systems to be compared structurally to physical systems without reduc-
tionism.
8
Mathematics as Stabilized Replay
Mathematical objects may be viewed as maximally stable semantic equivalence classes. Proofs are
constructions; theorems are invariants under replay.
Proposition 8.1. A mathematical object is semantically defined by the invariance of its consequences
under replay of admissible derivations.
This explains why mathematical meaning is robust to notation, presentation, and proof strategy.

Limitations and Open Directions
This paper does not provide a full formalization of semantic geometry. Nor does it claim that all
semantic systems admit smooth structure.
Rather, it establishes replay invariance as a minimal axiom from which geometric reasoning
becomes possible.
Future work may explore categorical formulations, topological invariants, or connections to
physical information constraints, but such developments are extensions, not prerequisites.
10
Conclusion
By treating replay as an axiomatic constraint and abstraction as invariant quotienting, semantic
systems can be understood within the same structural framework that underlies modern physics
and mathematics. Meaning emerges not from representation or interpretation, but from stability
under transformation.
This perspective does not reduce semantics to geometry or physics. It shows that all three share
a deeper commitment: structure is what survives change.
A
Replay Axiomatics
We begin by formalizing replay as a structural constraint on construction systems. The aim is to
isolate the minimal assumptions required to speak meaningfully about abstraction, equivalence,
and invariance, without presupposing any particular computational, physical, or representational
substrate.
Definition A.1 (Construction System). A construction system is a tuple
C = (E, H, R)
where E is a set of primitive events, H is the set of finite event histories (finite sequences over E),
and R : H →S is a replay map assigning each history a semantic outcome in a space S.
The replay map need not be injective. Multiple distinct histories may correspond to the same
semantic outcome.
Axiom A.1 (Replay Determinacy). For any history h ∈H, the replay map R(h) is uniquely
determined.
This axiom excludes stochastic or underdetermined replay, but does not constrain how histories
are generated, nor does it require that histories be physically reproducible.
Axiom A.2 (Replay Closure). If h ∈H, then any prefix h′ ⪯h is also an element of H.
Replay closure ensures that semantic construction is well-founded and that intermediate stages
of construction are themselves meaningful within the system.

B
Semantic Equivalence and Quotient Structure
Abstraction is formalized as a quotient induced by replay invariance.
Definition B.1 (Replay Equivalence). Two histories h1, h2 ∈H are replay-equivalent, written
h1 ∼h2, if and only if
R(h1) = R(h2).
Proposition B.1. Replay equivalence is an equivalence relation on H.
Proof. Reflexivity and symmetry follow immediately from equality. Transitivity follows from the
transitivity of equality in the semantic space S.
Definition B.2 (Semantic Quotient Space). The semantic quotient space is defined as
Q = H/ ∼,
whose elements are equivalence classes of replay-equivalent histories.
The quotient is induced by necessity, not by optimization or representational convenience.
Proposition B.2. The canonical projection π : H →Q is surjective but generally not injective.
This non-injectivity captures the core notion of abstraction: many constructions collapse onto a
single semantic representative.
C
Replay Flatness and Path Independence
Replay induces a flatness condition analogous to path independence in geometric and algebraic
settings.
Definition C.1 (Semantic Flatness). A construction system is semantically flat if, for any two
histories h1, h2 ∈H that differ only by permutation of independent events, the replay outcomes
coincide:
R(h1) = R(h2).
Proposition C.1. Semantic flatness implies that semantic outcome depends only on equivalence
class, not on construction path.
Corollary C.1. Violations of semantic flatness correspond to semantic curvature, in which local
construction order affects global outcome.
No smoothness, differentiability, or manifold structure is assumed. The analogy to curvature is
purely structural.

D
Semantic Distance and Stability
To reason about robustness and invariance, we introduce a minimal notion of semantic distance.
Definition D.1 (Perturbation). A perturbation is a function
δ : H →H
that modifies a history by a bounded change, such as event substitution, deletion, or insertion.
Definition D.2 (Semantic Distance). The semantic distance between histories h1 and h2 is defined
as
d(h1, h2) = inf{∥δ∥| R(δ(h1)) ̸= R(δ(h2))}.
This function defines a pseudometric on equivalence classes; it need not satisfy the triangle
inequality globally.
Definition D.3 (Semantic Stability). An equivalence class [h] ∈Q is semantically stable if there
exists ϵ > 0 such that for all h′ /∈[h],
d(h, h′) > ϵ.
Stable equivalence classes correspond to abstractions that persist under bounded perturbations.
E
Structural Phase Transitions
We now formalize abstraction emergence as a structural phase transition.
Definition E.1 (Control Parameter). A control parameter λ is a continuous parameter governing
admissible perturbations or construction constraints within a family of construction systems Cλ.
Definition E.2 (Semantic Phase Transition). A semantic phase transition occurs at λ = λc if the
topology or connectivity structure of the quotient space Qλ changes discontinuously at λc.
Proposition E.1. Semantic phase transitions correspond to qualitative changes in equivalence-class
stability, connectivity, or degeneracy.
This definition captures abstraction emergence without appeal to thermodynamic entropy or
statistical mechanics.
F
Mathematical Objects as Invariants
Mathematical objects arise naturally as maximally stable semantic invariants.
Definition F.1 (Admissible Derivation). An admissible derivation is a history whose events
correspond to rule-governed inferential steps within a formal system.
Proposition F.1. A mathematical object corresponds to a semantic equivalence class invariant
under all admissible derivations.
6

Corollary F.1. Mathematical meaning is independent of representational form, proof path, or
syntactic encoding.
This provides a structural account of mathematical objecthood without epistemic or psychological
assumptions.
G
Scope and Non-Commitments
To avoid misinterpretation, we make the following clarifications explicit.
No assumption is made that semantic spaces are smooth manifolds. No reliance is placed
on category theory, though the framework is compatible with later categorical refinement. No
identification is made between semantic space and physical spacetime. No claim is made that replay
is physically realizable in all systems. No reduction of meaning to entropy or information is assumed.
Axiom G.1 (Minimality). Only structural commitments explicitly stated in this paper are assumed.
