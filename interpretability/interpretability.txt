1. **Introduction**

   The essay aims to address the limitations of current mechanistic interpretability frameworks for neural networks. While these methods have introduced a causal approach to understand internal structures responsible for behavior, they remain state-centric and weakly sensitive to irreversibility and history. 2. **Causal Mechanistic Interpretability**

   Causal mechanistic interpretability defines a component as mechanistically relevant if intervening on it changes the model behavior systematically and predictably. This methodology supports causal abstraction, where low-level variable interventions are aligned with high-level algorithmic predictions. However, existing frameworks struggle to explain phenomena like irreversibility, commitment, substitution failure, and regime shifts without treating events as primary causal entities.

3. **Texture-Nodes: Value-Carrying Variables**

   Texture-nodes are variables whose roles are completely defined by the values they carry at any given time. These include neurons, attention heads, MLP activations, and linear directions used in activation steering. Their causal relevance is determined through value-level interventions and mediation analysis. Texture-node manipulations are typically reversible; restoring original values returns the system to its prior functional state.

4. **Event-Nodes: Constraint-Carrying Commitments**

   Event-nodes, on the other hand, impose structural constraints on future admissible states, limiting what the system can do after the event occurs. Unlike texture nodes, their causal influence is not determined by value substitution alone but by changes in allowed future actions. Event-node interventions are generally irreversible; no sequence of subsequent texture-node manipulations can restore the original set of admissible futures once a structural commitment has been made.

5. **Generator Functions and Procedural Structure**

   The distinction between event-nodes and texture-nodes can be understood using a procedural generation analogy. Texture-nodes correspond to parameters (or parameter ratios) in generator functions, altering surface appearance without changing the generative regime when manipulated. Event-nodes represent the selection of the generator itself; once chosen, all subsequent admissible parameterizations are conditioned on this choice, and no tuning can reproduce the output family of a different generator.

6. **Irreversibility and History**

   Standard causal analysis views irreversibility as resulting from information loss. Event-nodes introduce a new kind of irreversibility: structural irreversibility. This type occurs when an intervention reduces admissible futures in a manner that cannot be reversed by later value-level manipulations, regardless of information preservation or destruction.

7. **Causal Abstraction with Typed Nodes**

8. **Failure, Substitution, and Semantic Incompatibility**

Event-nodes explain why this assumption sometimes fails: substitution failure indicates that the substituted value was generated under a different event history and is incompatible with current future constraints. New behaviors under intervention signal an incorrect or incomplete causal graph, often due to unmodeled event-nodes.

9. **What Counts as Understanding**

   Mechanistic understanding involves reconstructing how values mediate effects (texture level), which commitments constrain futures (event level), and their interaction under intervention. This understanding is inherently partial and revisable; no single abstraction fully captures the system's causal structure, but some abstractions fail by ignoring irreversibility and history.

10. **Conclusion**

   The proposed distinction between texture-nodes (value-carrying variables) and event-nodes (constraint-carrying commitments) provides a minimal extension to causal mechanistic interpretability without introducing new metaphysics or abandoning causal abstraction. ### From Inspection to Intervention

Title: From Inspection to Intervention: Causal Mechanisms and Algorithmic Understanding in Neural Networks

This essay proposes a shift in the approach to understanding neural networks, moving from descriptive inspection to causal analysis of their internal operations. The authors argue that mere visibility into weights, activations, or attention patterns is insufficient for genuine understanding. Instead, they advocate for mechanistic understanding rooted in causality rather than correlation.

1. **Mechanistic Understanding**: This concept is defined as the ability to identify internal variables whose manipulation predictably and systematically alters behavior, consistent with an underlying algorithmic description. In other words, it's not enough to observe that a component activates during certain behaviors; one must also be able to manipulate it and see consistent changes in those behaviors.

2. **Causality and Intervention**: Causal analysis enters through interventions – deliberately altering internal variables to understand their downstream effects. Unlike mere observation, interventions break normal dependencies and reveal causal influences, distinguishing genuine causal impact from mere correlation.

3. **Activation-Level Control**: One of the earliest demonstrations of causal influence comes from activation steering – adding vectors to internal activations to bias models towards or away from certain behaviors. This technique reveals that behavioral distinctions often correspond to linear directions in representation space, but it doesn't specify what computation is being performed.

4. **Causal Mediation and Information Flow**: Causal mediation analysis refines intervention by decomposing the total effect of an input on output into contributions carried by intermediate variables. This approach reveals structured pathways by which information is retrieved, transformed, and routed through the network, providing a more detailed causal story than static maps.

5. **Causal Abstraction and Algorithmic Structure**: The strongest form of mechanistic interpretability occurs when neural activity can be related to a higher-level algorithm through causal abstraction. Here, both the neural network and the algorithm are treated as causal models, with the goal of determining if the algorithm faithfully abstracts the network's behavior under intervention. This requires stringent criteria: not just similar behavior, but interventional equivalence (substituting one value with another that would result from a different input).

6. **Events, History, and Irreversibility**: Mechanistic explanation may require attention to events – transformations that irreversibly constrain future behavior. Interventions that cannot be undone without losing explanatory power signal the presence of genuine historical commitment within the system.

7. **Responsibility and Constraint**: Not all mechanisms act by producing outcomes; some suppress, filter, or exclude possibilities. Recognizing such negative or constraining roles requires a notion of causality that treats absence and inhibition as explanatory factors.

8. **Limits of Understanding**: Even successful interventions don't guarantee understanding. A collection of control techniques can enable manipulation without yielding an explanatory model, and a neat abstraction might fail outside its regime of validity. Mechanistic understanding thus admits degrees, improving as abstractions become more stable under distribution shifts, more sensitive to history, and more faithful under counterfactual substitution.

The essay concludes that mechanistic interpretability, when grounded in causality, becomes the study of which internal structures are responsible for behavior and what algorithms they realize – moving the field from visualization towards reverse-engineering neural networks. Understanding is achieved not by looking or correlating, but by intervening and reconstructing the causal logic that produces network outputs.

While this assumption works in many contexts, it's insufficient for computational systems that exhibit regime changes, heuristic switching, or irreversible commitments.

2. **Causal Tracing in Neural Networks**: Causal tracing extends mediation analysis to neural networks by treating internal components as potential mediators. It involves adding noise to input representations, restoring internal states to their clean values, and measuring the degree of output recovery. This technique successfully identifies information pathways within the network but relies on language that exceeds a purely value-based mediation framework's formal expression.

3. **Texture-Nodes and Event-Nodes**: To address this limitation, the authors introduce a typed causal framework with two classes of nodes:

   - **Texture-Nodes**: These carry values, and interventions involve direct value substitution (do(T := t)).
   - **Event-Nodes**: These encode irreversible or semi-irreversible structural constraints on future computation. Interventions on event-nodes involve selecting a regime or generator (do(E := r)).

4. **Generator Semantics and Graph Rewriting**: Event-nodes can be interpreted as graph rewrite triggers, rewriting the effective computation graph once a regime is selected. Two systems with identical texture values may implement different computations due to distinct event histories.

5. **Two-Channel Mediation Theorem**: This theorem decomposes the total effect of X on Y into texture-mediated (MET_Y) and event-mediated (MEE_Y) components: TEY = MET_Y + MEE_Y. Texture mediation alone is sufficient to characterize the total effect if and only if the intervention on X leaves the event state invariant.

6. **Irreversibility and History Sensitivity**: Event-nodes introduce a structural irreversibility, meaning that an event may permanently reduce the space of admissible futures even if all information is preserved. This explains why certain substitutions are ill-posed: the generator required to interpret a substituted value no longer exists.

7. **Extension to Knowledge Networks**: The distinction between texture and event nodes applies to large-scale knowledge systems like collaborative encyclopedias, where articles, links, and edits form texture-level structures, while governance decisions, user bans, policy changes, and community norms function as event-nodes.

8. **Implications**: This framework has several consequences:
   - Mechanistic interpretability requires regime awareness, not just activation tracing.
   - Successful control or prediction doesn't guarantee correct abstraction.
   - Knowledge systems may fail catastrophically through event perturbations even when content appears intact.

In summary, this paper presents a two-channel causal mediation framework that unifies mechanistic interpretability, regime changes, and network resilience under a single causal lens. By distinguishing between value transmission (texture-mediation) and structural commitments (event-mediation), the authors clarify both the successes and limitations of existing techniques and open a path toward historical models of computation and knowledge.
