Venezuela occupies a peculiar position in contemporary geopolitical discourse. It is simultaneously described as a humanitarian catastrophe, a rogue petrostate, a victim of imperial sabotage, a cautionary tale of socialism, and a latent treasure chest of natural wealth waiting to be unlocked. These descriptions coexist without resolution, producing a fog in which incoherent action becomes not merely possible but routine. The absence of a stable policy narrative is not an accident. It is the consequence of a deeper failure to articulate how value is created, how power is exercised, and how legitimacy is maintained in a world where infrastructure, finance, energy, and information are tightly coupled systems rather than independent domains.

\section
{The Petrostate Trap}

To understand Venezuela's present condition, it is necessary to abandon the superficial question of why a country with oil is poor and instead ask how oil came to dominate the structure of Venezuelan life so completely that it displaced almost every other mechanism of value creation. Venezuela did not merely discover oil; it reorganized its political economy around oil to a degree few other nations have attempted. Over the course of the twentieth century, petroleum revenues replaced taxation, industrial policy, and long-term institutional development as the primary interface between the state and society. The result was not prosperity in the durable sense, but a form of revenue anesthesia that delayed structural reckoning.

Oil allowed the Venezuelan state to import what it did not produce, to subsidize what it could not sustain, and to postpone the difficult work of building diversified productive capacity. Agriculture declined, manufacturing stagnated, and domestic technical expertise became increasingly concentrated within the oil sector itself. This narrowing of competence created a brittle economy whose apparent strength depended entirely on external price signals over which it had no control. When oil prices were high, Venezuela appeared functional. When they fell, the entire system convulsed.

The collapse of global oil prices in 2014 was not an unforeseen shock in economic terms, but it was treated as one politically. Oil prices fell from over one hundred dollars per barrel to less than thirty within a matter of months. For a state whose export revenues, fiscal capacity, and foreign exchange reserves were overwhelmingly oil-derived, this was not a downturn but an existential event. Government revenues evaporated, imports became unaffordable, and the monetary system fractured under the strain of attempting to maintain obligations that were no longer grounded in productive output.

The severity of the Venezuelan collapse was compounded by the specific character of its oil. Much of Venezuela's reserves consist of extra-heavy crude, particularly in the Orinoco Belt. This oil is viscous, sulfur-rich, and expensive to extract and refine. It cannot be efficiently processed without specialized infrastructure, blending agents, and energy-intensive upgrading facilities. In favorable market conditions, such oil can still be profitable, but only within a system that is capital-rich, technically competent, and institutionally stable. Venezuela, by the time prices collapsed, was none of these.

Rather than responding to declining margins by investing in refining capacity and downstream value creation, the Venezuelan system doubled down on raw export dependence. The state oil company, increasingly politicized and stripped of technical expertise, became less capable of maintaining even basic operations. Skilled engineers emigrated. Maintenance was deferred. Pipelines degraded. Refineries malfunctioned. Production fell not because oil disappeared, but because the organizational capacity required to move oil from ground to market disintegrated.

This is the core paradox of Venezuelan oil wealth. Oil did not fail Venezuela. Venezuela failed to build the systems necessary to make oil a foundation rather than a crutch. The result was not merely poverty, but a loss of temporal agency. Once oil revenues collapsed, the state lost the ability to plan, to invest, and even to imagine futures beyond immediate survival. Hyperinflation, shortages, and mass emigration followed not as ideological consequences, but as systemic ones.
\section
{Oil as Infrastructure, Not Treasure}

Any serious plan for Venezuela must therefore begin by rejecting the metaphor of oil as treasure. Oil is not wealth waiting to be seized. It is a flow that exists only when supported by infrastructure, labor, law, and energy. Without roads, electricity, courts, data systems, and skilled workers, oil remains underground or becomes a liability rather than an asset. This distinction is not rhetorical. It is causal.

The fixation on oil reserves as a measure of national potential has repeatedly led external actors into strategic error. Possession of reserves does not imply control over production. Control over production does not imply profitability. Profitability does not imply legitimacy. Each step in this chain requires institutional support that cannot be conjured through force or decree. Venezuela's tragedy is that it possessed the first link in abundance while neglecting the others almost entirely.

Heavy crude intensifies this problem. Unlike light oil, which can be refined with relatively standard processes, extra-heavy crude demands sophisticated upgrading facilities capable of converting it into usable fuels. These facilities are capital-intensive, technologically complex, and highly sensitive to power stability and supply chain integrity. Venezuela's existing refining capacity is insufficient and degraded. Without massive reinvestment, Venezuelan oil will continue to sell at steep discounts, enriching intermediaries rather than the country itself.

This observation leads to a counterintuitive conclusion. If Venezuela is to escape the petrostate trap, it must not reduce its engagement with oil in the short term, but deepen it in a very specific way. It must move downstream. It must stop exporting crude and begin exporting products. Refining oil into fuels, petrochemicals, and industrial inputs captures value that crude exports permanently forfeit. This requires refineries, grids, ports, and trained operators, none of which can be built incrementally under conditions of political paralysis and capital flight.
\section
{The Case for Deliberate Overbuilding}

The conventional response to Venezuelan collapse emphasizes stabilization, humanitarian aid, and gradual reform. These approaches fail because they underestimate the depth of infrastructural decay and the speed at which human capital is lost. Venezuela does not need marginal improvement. It needs deliberate overbuilding.

A credible plan would involve the rapid construction of multiple large-scale refineries designed specifically for heavy crude, built not as symbols of sovereignty but as export-oriented industrial platforms. These facilities would anchor a broader reconstruction of electrical grids, transport corridors, data infrastructure, and logistics hubs. The objective would not be self-sufficiency, but reintegration into global production networks on terms that favor value retention rather than extraction.

Such a program cannot be financed domestically. Venezuela's capital stock has been exhausted, its credit destroyed, and its currency rendered unreliable. External investment is unavoidable. The question is not whether foreign capital will shape Venezuela's future, but which capital, under what conditions, and toward what ends. Investment from Gulf states experienced in energy infrastructure and from China, with its tolerance for long time horizons and state-backed financing, is not an ideological choice but a structural one. These actors possess both the capital and the operational capacity to execute projects at the scale and speed required.

The political cost of such arrangements is real. External financiers will demand guarantees, control mechanisms, and priority claims. This is often framed as a loss of sovereignty. In practice, Venezuela has already lost sovereignty through incapacity. The choice is not between autonomy and dependence, but between chaotic dependence and structured dependence with pathways to recovery.
\section
{Using the Chaos}

What distinguishes this plan from conventional development frameworks is its refusal to wait for political resolution as a prerequisite for economic reconstruction. The current Venezuelan political system lacks the legitimacy, coherence, and trust required to coordinate a national rebuilding effort. Pretending otherwise merely extends the stalemate. In such conditions, governance does not disappear; it fragments. Power migrates to whoever can maintain flows of food, fuel, and security.

Rather than denying this reality, a plan for Venezuela must instrument it. This may involve temporary external governance arrangements, imposed not as moral exemplars but as operational stabilizers. The installation of an external authority, even an authoritarian one, is not proposed here as a normative ideal, but as a recognition that coordination failures cannot be solved by appeals to democratic procedure alone when institutions have already collapsed.

This is the most controversial claim of the essay, and deliberately so. The history of development is littered with failures produced by insisting on ideal governance forms in contexts incapable of sustaining them. The alternative is not purity, but paralysis. If Venezuela is to rebuild, someone must have the authority to coordinate infrastructure, guarantee contracts, suppress armed sabotage, and enforce timelines. Whether that authority is local or external is less important than whether it is functional.

The chaos surrounding Venezuela is not merely a problem to be solved. It is a signal. It reveals where systems have failed and where power actually resides. A plan that ignores this will fail. A plan that uses it, channels it, and constrains it may yet succeed.

\section
{Appendix A: Historical Precedents for Resource-Centered State Failure}

Venezuela's trajectory is not anomalous. States that organize themselves around a single extractive commodity repeatedly exhibit the same structural vulnerabilities regardless of ideology, geography, or historical period. The cases of Nigeria, Angola, Equatorial Guinea, and pre-2003 Iraq demonstrate that oil-centered development tends to displace taxation, weaken accountability, and concentrate political power in institutions that are optimized for rent distribution rather than productive coordination. When revenues flow from exports rather than from domestic economic activity, the feedback loop between citizen welfare and state capacity is severed.

In such systems, political legitimacy becomes decoupled from performance. Governments can survive long periods of mismanagement so long as commodity prices remain high. Conversely, when prices fall, collapse can be sudden and catastrophic because there is no diversified base to absorb the shock. Venezuela's post-2014 contraction fits squarely within this pattern. What distinguishes it is not the existence of oil dependence, but the degree to which oil crowded out every alternative form of institutional learning.

The historical lesson is not that resource wealth is inherently destructive, but that it must be subordinated to broader systems of coordination. Where oil revenues were used to build manufacturing capacity, transport networks, and human capital, as in Norway, long-term stability followed. Where oil revenues substituted for these investments, collapse eventually ensued. Venezuela followed the latter path with exceptional thoroughness.
\section
{Appendix B: Heavy Crude Economics and Refining Constraints}

The physical properties of Venezuelan crude impose constraints that cannot be negotiated away by political will. Extra-heavy crude from the Orinoco Belt has high viscosity, elevated sulfur content, and low hydrogen-to-carbon ratios. These characteristics require either blending with lighter hydrocarbons or upgrading through coking and hydrocracking processes before the oil can be converted into usable fuels.

These processes are capital intensive and energy intensive. They require continuous power, specialized catalysts, and highly trained personnel. They also generate waste streams that must be managed under regulatory regimes capable of enforcing safety and environmental standards. In the absence of such regimes, refining becomes not only unprofitable but dangerous.

The tendency to treat Venezuelan oil as immediately monetizable ignores these constraints. Heavy crude is not simply sold at a discount; it is sold at a discount that reflects the buyer's anticipation of political risk, infrastructure unreliability, and legal uncertainty. Without domestic refining capacity, Venezuela permanently exports value while importing finished products at higher prices. This asymmetry is one of the central mechanisms by which oil wealth has translated into poverty.
\section
{Appendix C: Infrastructure as a Legitimacy Engine}

Throughout modern history, legitimacy has followed infrastructure more reliably than ideology. States that provide electricity, transport, water, and predictable administration tend to stabilize even under imperfect political arrangements. States that fail to provide these services lose authority regardless of their formal constitutional structure.

Infrastructure performs three simultaneous functions. It enables economic activity. It creates visible evidence of state capacity. It generates temporal expectations, allowing individuals and firms to plan beyond immediate survival. When infrastructure decays, legitimacy decays with it, not because citizens suddenly reject authority in principle, but because authority ceases to be operationally meaningful.

The proposal to prioritize refineries, grids, transport corridors, and data centers rests on this logic. These systems are not merely economic assets. They are governance substrates. Their construction creates facts on the ground that re-anchor social expectations. Elections conducted in their absence are symbolic. Elections conducted after their restoration have substance.
\section
{Appendix D: External Authority and Transitional Governance}

The use of external authority in post-collapse environments is historically common, though rarely acknowledged without euphemism. From postwar reconstruction in Germany and Japan to international administration in the Balkans and East Timor, external governance has repeatedly served as a bridge between institutional vacuum and domestic self-rule.

The failures of such arrangements are well documented, but so are their successes. The determining factor is not the presence of external authority per se, but whether it is bounded by mission clarity, temporal limits, and measurable objectives. Unbounded occupation breeds resentment. Bounded reconstruction can restore agency.

In the Venezuelan context, the absence of any coordinating authority has already produced fragmentation, informal power centers, and foreign influence without accountability. Formalizing authority under explicit constraints would not introduce domination where none exists. It would replace diffuse domination with structured responsibility.
\section
{Appendix E: Temporal Risk and the Cost of Delay}

Delay is not neutral. In collapsing systems, delay compounds damage. Each year without reinvestment accelerates capital decay, human flight, and institutional forgetting. Skills atrophy. Informal economies entrench. Violence professionalizes. At a certain point, recovery costs grow nonlinearly.

Venezuela is approaching this threshold. The longer reconstruction is postponed in the name of political purity or diplomatic caution, the more expensive and coercive it will become when it eventually occurs. Early, decisive overbuilding is therefore not recklessness but risk minimization.

This appendix underscores the central claim of the essay: that the true danger is not acting too forcefully, but acting too late. Infrastructure, once destroyed, does not wait for consensus. It demands replacement or it imposes collapse.

The ethical discomfort provoked by this plan is not incidental; it is diagnostic. Ethical frameworks built for stable societies fail when applied to systemic breakdown. In such conditions, refusing to choose is itself a choice, one that privileges existing power asymmetries and ongoing harm.

This appendix does not argue that authoritarian coordination, external capital dominance, or infrastructural bypasses are morally ideal. It argues that moral evaluation must include counterfactuals. The relevant question is not whether these measures violate abstract principles, but whether their absence guarantees continued suffering at scale.

Ethics without causality is sentiment. Ethics with causality is responsibility. The plan for Venezuela, as outlined, accepts responsibility for consequences rather than retreating into procedural innocence.

\section{Appendix A: Historical Precedents for Resource-Centered State Failure}

Venezuela's trajectory is not anomalous. States that organize themselves around a single extractive commodity repeatedly exhibit the same structural vulnerabilities regardless of ideology, geography, or historical period. The cases of Nigeria, Angola, Equatorial Guinea, and pre-2003 Iraq demonstrate that oil-centered development tends to displace taxation, weaken accountability, and concentrate political power in institutions that are optimized for rent distribution rather than productive coordination. When revenues flow from exports rather than from domestic economic activity, the feedback loop between citizen welfare and state capacity is severed.

In such systems, political legitimacy becomes decoupled from performance. Governments can survive long periods of mismanagement so long as commodity prices remain high. Conversely, when prices fall, collapse can be sudden and catastrophic because there is no diversified base to absorb the shock. Venezuela's post-2014 contraction fits squarely within this pattern. What distinguishes it is not the existence of oil dependence, but the degree to which oil crowded out every alternative form of institutional learning.

The historical lesson is not that resource wealth is inherently destructive, but that it must be subordinated to broader systems of coordination. Where oil revenues were used to build manufacturing capacity, transport networks, and human capital, as in Norway, long-term stability followed. Where oil revenues substituted for these investments, collapse eventually ensued. Venezuela followed the latter path with exceptional thoroughness.

\section{Appendix B: Heavy Crude Economics and Refining Constraints}

The physical properties of Venezuelan crude impose constraints that cannot be negotiated away by political will. Extra-heavy crude from the Orinoco Belt has high viscosity, elevated sulfur content, and low hydrogen-to-carbon ratios. These characteristics require either blending with lighter hydrocarbons or upgrading through coking and hydrocracking processes before the oil can be converted into usable fuels.

These processes are capital intensive and energy intensive. They require continuous power, specialized catalysts, and highly trained personnel. They also generate waste streams that must be managed under regulatory regimes capable of enforcing safety and environmental standards. In the absence of such regimes, refining becomes not only unprofitable but dangerous.

The tendency to treat Venezuelan oil as immediately monetizable ignores these constraints. Heavy crude is not simply sold at a discount; it is sold at a discount that reflects the buyer's anticipation of political risk, infrastructure unreliability, and legal uncertainty. Without domestic refining capacity, Venezuela permanently exports value while importing finished products at higher prices. This asymmetry is one of the central mechanisms by which oil wealth has translated into poverty.

\section{Appendix C: Infrastructure as a Legitimacy Engine}

Throughout modern history, legitimacy has followed infrastructure more reliably than ideology. States that provide electricity, transport, water, and predictable administration tend to stabilize even under imperfect political arrangements. States that fail to provide these services lose authority regardless of their formal constitutional structure.

Infrastructure performs three simultaneous functions. It enables economic activity. It creates visible evidence of state capacity. It generates temporal expectations, allowing individuals and firms to plan beyond immediate survival. When infrastructure decays, legitimacy decays with it, not because citizens suddenly reject authority in principle, but because authority ceases to be operationally meaningful.

The proposal to prioritize refineries, grids, transport corridors, and data centers rests on this logic. These systems are not merely economic assets. They are governance substrates. Their construction creates facts on the ground that re-anchor social expectations. Elections conducted in their absence are symbolic. Elections conducted after their restoration have substance.

\section{Appendix D: External Authority and Transitional Governance}

The use of external authority in post-collapse environments is historically common, though rarely acknowledged without euphemism. From postwar reconstruction in Germany and Japan to international administration in the Balkans and East Timor, external governance has repeatedly served as a bridge between institutional vacuum and domestic self-rule.

The failures of such arrangements are well documented, but so are their successes. The determining factor is not the presence of external authority per se, but whether it is bounded by mission clarity, temporal limits, and measurable objectives. Unbounded occupation breeds resentment. Bounded reconstruction can restore agency.

In the Venezuelan context, the absence of any coordinating authority has already produced fragmentation, informal power centers, and foreign influence without accountability. Formalizing authority under explicit constraints would not introduce domination where none exists. It would replace diffuse domination with structured responsibility.

\section{Appendix E: Temporal Risk and the Cost of Delay}

Delay is not neutral. In collapsing systems, delay compounds damage. Each year without reinvestment accelerates capital decay, human flight, and institutional forgetting. Skills atrophy. Informal economies entrench. Violence professionalizes. At a certain point, recovery costs grow nonlinearly.

Venezuela is approaching this threshold. The longer reconstruction is postponed in the name of political purity or diplomatic caution, the more expensive and coercive it will become when it eventually occurs. Early, decisive overbuilding is therefore not recklessness but risk minimization.

This appendix underscores the central claim of the essay: that the true danger is not acting too forcefully, but acting too late. Infrastructure, once destroyed, does not wait for consensus. It demands replacement or it imposes collapse.

\section{Appendix F: On the Ethics of Uncomfortable Solutions}

The ethical discomfort provoked by this plan is not incidental; it is diagnostic. Ethical frameworks built for stable societies fail when applied to systemic breakdown. In such conditions, refusing to choose is itself a choice, one that privileges existing power asymmetries and ongoing harm.

{Apéndice A: Precedentes Históricos del Fracaso Estatal Centrado en los Recursos}

La trayectoria de Venezuela no es anómala. Los Estados que se organizan en torno a un solo producto extractivo exhiben de manera reiterada las mismas vulnerabilidades estructurales, independientemente de la ideología, la geografía o el período histórico. Los casos de Nigeria, Angola, Guinea Ecuatorial e Irak antes de 2003 demuestran que el desarrollo centrado en el petróleo tiende a desplazar la tributación, debilitar la rendición de cuentas y concentrar el poder político en instituciones optimizadas para la distribución de rentas más que para la coordinación productiva. Cuando los ingresos provienen de las exportaciones y no de la actividad económica interna, se rompe el circuito de retroalimentación entre el bienestar ciudadano y la capacidad estatal.

En estos sistemas, la legitimidad política se desacopla del desempeño. Los gobiernos pueden sobrevivir largos períodos de mala gestión mientras los precios de los productos básicos se mantengan altos. Por el contrario, cuando los precios caen, el colapso puede ser súbito y catastrófico, ya que no existe una base diversificada capaz de absorber el shock. La contracción venezolana posterior a 2014 encaja plenamente en este patrón. Lo que la distingue no es la existencia de dependencia petrolera, sino el grado en que el petróleo desplazó prácticamente toda forma alternativa de aprendizaje institucional.

La lección histórica no es que la riqueza en recursos sea intrínsecamente destructiva, sino que debe subordinarse a sistemas más amplios de coordinación. Allí donde los ingresos petroleros se utilizaron para construir capacidad manufacturera, redes de transporte y capital humano, como en Noruega, se alcanzó una estabilidad de largo plazo. Allí donde los ingresos petroleros sustituyeron estas inversiones, el colapso terminó por imponerse. Venezuela siguió este último camino con una exhaustividad excepcional.
\section
{Apéndice B: Economía del Crudo Pesado y Restricciones de Refinación}

Las propiedades físicas del crudo venezolano imponen restricciones que no pueden negociarse mediante la voluntad política. El crudo extrapesado de la Faja del Orinoco presenta alta viscosidad, elevado contenido de azufre y bajas proporciones de hidrógeno respecto al carbono. Estas características exigen, antes de convertir el petróleo en combustibles utilizables, su mezcla con hidrocarburos más livianos o su mejoramiento mediante procesos de coquización e hidrotratamiento.

Estos procesos son intensivos en capital y en energía. Requieren suministro eléctrico continuo, catalizadores especializados y personal altamente capacitado. Asimismo, generan corrientes de desechos que deben gestionarse bajo regímenes regulatorios capaces de hacer cumplir normas de seguridad y estándares ambientales. En ausencia de dichos regímenes, la refinación no solo se vuelve poco rentable, sino peligrosa.

La tendencia a tratar el petróleo venezolano como inmediatamente monetizable ignora estas restricciones. El crudo pesado no se vende simplemente con descuento; se vende con un descuento que refleja la anticipación del comprador respecto del riesgo político, la falta de confiabilidad de la infraestructura y la incertidumbre jurídica. Sin capacidad de refinación doméstica, Venezuela exporta valor de forma permanente mientras importa productos terminados a precios más altos. Esta asimetría es uno de los mecanismos centrales mediante los cuales la riqueza petrolera se ha traducido en pobreza.
\section
{Apéndice C: La Infraestructura como Motor de Legitimidad}

A lo largo de la historia moderna, la legitimidad ha seguido a la infraestructura de manera más consistente que a la ideología. Los Estados que proveen electricidad, transporte, agua y una administración predecible tienden a estabilizarse incluso bajo arreglos políticos imperfectos. Los Estados que no logran proveer estos servicios pierden autoridad independientemente de su estructura constitucional formal.

La infraestructura cumple tres funciones simultáneas. Habilita la actividad económica. Produce evidencia visible de la capacidad estatal. Genera expectativas temporales, permitiendo que individuos y empresas planifiquen más allá de la supervivencia inmediata. Cuando la infraestructura se degrada, la legitimidad se degrada con ella, no porque los ciudadanos rechacen de pronto la autoridad en principio, sino porque la autoridad deja de ser operativamente significativa.

La propuesta de priorizar refinerías, redes eléctricas, corredores de transporte y centros de datos se basa en esta lógica. Estos sistemas no son meros activos económicos. Son sustratos de gobernanza. Su construcción crea hechos sobre el terreno que reanclan las expectativas sociales. Las elecciones celebradas en su ausencia son simbólicas. Las elecciones realizadas tras su restauración adquieren sustancia.
\section
{Apéndice D: Autoridad Externa y Gobernanza Transicional}

El uso de autoridad externa en entornos posteriores al colapso es históricamente común, aunque rara vez se reconoce sin eufemismos. Desde la reconstrucción de posguerra en Alemania y Japón hasta la administración internacional en los Balcanes y Timor Oriental, la gobernanza externa ha servido repetidamente como puente entre el vacío institucional y el autogobierno interno.

Los fracasos de estos arreglos están bien documentados, pero también lo están sus éxitos. El factor determinante no es la presencia de autoridad externa en sí misma, sino si dicha autoridad está delimitada por una misión clara, límites temporales y objetivos medibles. La ocupación sin límites genera resentimiento. La reconstrucción acotada puede restaurar la agencia.

En el contexto venezolano, la ausencia de cualquier autoridad coordinadora ya ha producido fragmentación, centros de poder informales e influencia extranjera sin rendición de cuentas. Formalizar la autoridad bajo restricciones explícitas no introduciría dominación donde no existe. Sustituiría una dominación difusa por una responsabilidad estructurada.
\section
{Apéndice E: Riesgo Temporal y el Costo de la Demora}

La demora no es neutral. En sistemas en colapso, la demora amplifica el daño. Cada año sin reinversión acelera la degradación del capital, la fuga humana y el olvido institucional. Las habilidades se atrofian. Las economías informales se consolidan. La violencia se profesionaliza. Llegado cierto punto, los costos de la recuperación crecen de manera no lineal.

Venezuela se aproxima a este umbral. Cuanto más se posponga la reconstrucción en nombre de la pureza política o de la cautela diplomática, más costosa y coercitiva será cuando finalmente ocurra. El sobredimensionamiento temprano y decisivo no es, por lo tanto, imprudencia, sino minimización de riesgos.

Este apéndice subraya la afirmación central del ensayo: que el verdadero peligro no reside en actuar con demasiada fuerza, sino en actuar demasiado tarde. La infraestructura, una vez destruida, no espera consenso. Exige reemplazo o impone colapso.
\section
{Apéndice F: Sobre la Ética de las Soluciones Incómodas}

La incomodidad ética que provoca este plan no es incidental; es diagnóstica. Los marcos éticos diseñados para sociedades estables fracasan cuando se aplican a contextos de descomposición sistémica. En tales condiciones, negarse a elegir es en sí mismo una elección, que privilegia las asimetrías de poder existentes y el daño en curso.

Este apéndice no sostiene que la coordinación autoritaria, la dominación del capital externo o los desvíos infraestructurales sean moralmente ideales. Sostiene que la evaluación moral debe incluir contrafactuales. La pregunta relevante no es si estas medidas violan principios abstractos, sino si su ausencia garantiza la continuidad del sufrimiento a gran escala.

La ética sin causalidad es sentimiento. La ética con causalidad es responsabilidad. El plan para Venezuela, tal como se expone, asume responsabilidad por las consecuencias en lugar de refugiarse en la inocencia procedimental.
