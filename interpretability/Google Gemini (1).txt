In the book
The Brain Abstracted: Simplification in the History and Philosophy of Neuroscience
, M. Chirimuuta explores how the neuroscientific understanding of the brain is inherently shaped by
abstraction
and
simplification
.
The book argues that these processes are not just side effects of research but are fundamental to how neuroscience constructs its models of the mind and brain
.
Core Themes and Arguments
The Role of Abstraction
: Chirimuuta argues that neuroscience relies on abstracting away the vast biological complexity of the brain to create workable theories.
This abstraction allows scientists to focus on specific functional properties while ignoring others
.
Historical and Philosophical Context
: The book traces how different historical eras and philosophical frameworks have influenced which parts of the brain are simplified and which are emphasized.
It suggests that our "objective" knowledge is often a reflection of the simplified models we choose to build
.
Computational and Mathematical Modeling
: A major theme is the use of computational models (like neural networks) as a primary tool for simplification.
The book evaluates how these models serve as "useful fictions" that help explain neural behavior without needing to account for every molecular detail
.
Anti-Reductionism
: Chirimuuta challenges the idea that the brain can be fully understood by simply breaking it down into its smallest parts.
Instead, the book advocates for an "integrative" approach that recognizes how higher-level simplifications are necessary to understand complex neural systems
.
Key Questions Addressed
How do scientists decide which neural details are "noise" and which are "signal"?
In what ways have metaphors (like the brain as a computer) limited or expanded our understanding?
Can a science of the brain ever be "complete" if it must always rely on abstraction?

In
The Brain Abstracted: Simplification in the History and Philosophy of Neuroscience
, M. Chirimuuta argues that the immense biological complexity of the brain necessitates a "perspectival" approach to neuroscience, where scientists must use specific
abstractions
and
simplifications
to make sense of neural data.
Core Arguments and Themes
Necessity of Abstraction:
Chirimuuta posits that because the brain is an "excessively complex" system, no single model can capture it in its entirety. Scientific progress is made by choosing which details to include and which to treat as "noise".
The "Horganism" Problem:
The book explores the tension between "Horganism"—the idea that the brain is too complex to ever fully understand—and the drive for a unified theory of brain function.
Computational Models as Simplifications:
A major focus is on how computational and mathematical models (such as neural networks) act as essential simplifications. Chirimuuta treats these not as literal "maps" of the brain, but as tools that highlight specific functional principles.
Historical Evolution:
The book traces the history of these simplifications, showing how metaphors (like the brain as a telegraph system or a computer) have shaped the questions neuroscientists ask and the theories they build.
Philosophical Implications
Chirimuuta advocates for
"Hylomorphic Solipsism,"
suggesting that our neuroscientific explanations are deeply tied to our own cognitive needs and the specific goals of our research. Rather than aiming for a "perfect" or "objective" view of the brain, she suggests we should value models for their utility and explanatory power within specific contexts.
