\section{Category-Theoretic Formalization of SITH}
\label{sec:sith-category}
The preceding sections motivate SITH conceptually: semantic systems persist when they maintain organizational closure under constrained transformation. The goal of this section is to express that claim in a form that a reviewer can verify: we define the relevant category explicitly, state a single main theorem (viability as invariance), give a concrete example (a legal code), and then explain how the subsequent formalisms (information geometry and RSVP) refine the same categorical structure rather than introducing competing foundations.
\subsection{The semantic category}
Fix a domain of discourse (e.g.\ legal texts, standards, scientific theories, software repositories). Let $Y$ denote the collection of \emph{semantic states} in that domain. A state $y\in Y$ may be a single inscription (a text, a codebase snapshot, a statute) or a structured configuration (a canon plus its commentaries; a repository plus its test suite). Each domain also comes with a criterion of identity preservation: we model this as an \emph{identity observable} $\mathcal{I}:Y\to V$ into a value space $V$ that captures the invariants that define ``the same system'' for that domain (legal validity, interface contracts, canonical meaning, etc.).
\begin{definition}[Semantic category]
\label{def:semantic-category}
The \emph{semantic category} $\mathsf{C}$ associated to a domain is defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item Objects: elements $y\in Y$ (semantic states).
\item Morphisms: a morphism $f:y\to y'$ is an \emph{admissible, identity-preserving transformation} from $y$ to $y'$. Concretely, $f$ is a transformation whose application is recognized by the domain as preserving identity, i.e.\ $\mathcal{I}(y')=\mathcal{I}(y)$.
\item Composition: if $f:y\to y'$ and $g:y'\to y''$ are admissible, then $g\circ f:y\to y''$ is their sequential application.
\item Identities: for each $y$, $\mathrm{id}_y:y\to y$ is the do-nothing transformation.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}
Definition~\ref{def:semantic-category} is intentionally strict: \emph{only} transformations that preserve the domain's identity criterion are morphisms. Transformations that alter identity (corruptions, regime changes, semantic death) are not morphisms in $\mathsf{C}$; they are external interventions that will be modeled separately as perturbations.
\subsection{Perturbation, repair, and the viability operator}
Semantic systems do not evolve only by admissible transformations. They are also subjected to exogenous shocks: new contexts, hostile reinterpretations, technological substrate shifts, political conflict, adversarial incentives. These are not morphisms of $\mathsf{C}$ because they need not preserve identity. To incorporate them without weakening Definition~\ref{def:semantic-category}, we model them as a \emph{set-valued} dynamics that proposes candidates outside the admissible space, after which internal mechanisms attempt to map the result back into the identity-preserving region.
To keep the categorical base clean, we use a standard device: represent nondeterministic dynamics as a functor into a powerset construction.
\begin{definition}[Perturbation--repair transition]
\label{def:transition}
A \emph{perturbation--repair transition} on $\mathsf{C}$ is a function
\[
F:Y\to \mathcal{P}(Y)\setminus\{\varnothing\},
\]
interpreted as: from a current semantic state $y$, one step of the world-plus-system produces a nonempty set $F(y)$ of possible successor states (including variants produced by copying, editing, reinterpretation, enforcement, rollback, and repair).
\end{definition}
In applications, it is useful to factor $F$ into two conceptual stages:
\[
F \approx \mathsf{Rep}\circ \mathsf{Pert},
\]
where $\mathsf{Pert}$ injects noise (possibly identity-breaking), and $\mathsf{Rep}$ applies the system's endogenous correction mechanisms (institutions, standards, tests, courts, version control, rituals) to attempt recovery of identity. This factorization will become quantitative in Section~\ref{sec:info-geometry} and dynamical in Section~\ref{sec:rsvp}.
\subsection{Main theorem: semantic viability as invariance}
We now state the single categorical claim that this paper asks the reader to evaluate. Everything that follows (metrics, fields, architecture) is a way of diagnosing when this claim holds.
\begin{definition}[Identity basin as a full subcategory]
\label{def:basin-subcategory}
Let $y_0\in Y$. An \emph{identity basin} for $y_0$ is a full subcategory $\Omega\subseteq \mathsf{C}$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item (Identity-constant) For all objects $y\in \mathrm{Ob}(\Omega)$, $\mathcal{I}(y)=\mathcal{I}(y_0)$.
\item (Fullness) For any $y,y'\in \mathrm{Ob}(\Omega)$, every morphism $y\to y'$ in $\mathsf{C}$ is also a morphism in $\Omega$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
Fullness is a technical way of saying: once we restrict to the basin, we are not secretly changing what counts as admissible \emph{within} the basin. The basin is defined by objects (states), not by rewriting the rules mid-proof.
\begin{definition}[Invariance under a transition]
\label{def:invariant}
A subcategory $\Omega\subseteq \mathsf{C}$ is \emph{invariant under $F$} if for all $y\in \mathrm{Ob}(\Omega)$ we have
\[
F(y)\subseteq \mathrm{Ob}(\Omega).
\]
\end{definition}
\begin{theorem}[Semantic viability via invariant identity basin]
\label{thm:viability-invariance}
Let $\mathsf{C}$ be a semantic category (Definition~\ref{def:semantic-category}) and let $F$ be a perturbation--repair transition (Definition~\ref{def:transition}). Fix an initial state $y_0\in Y$.
The following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{(Invariant basin)} There exists a nonempty identity basin $\Omega\subseteq \mathsf{C}$ for $y_0$ (Definition~\ref{def:basin-subcategory}) that is invariant under $F$ (Definition~\ref{def:invariant}).
\item \textbf{(Viable trajectory)} There exists an infinite trajectory $y_0,y_1,y_2,\dots$ such that $y_{t+1}\in F(y_t)$ for all $t\ge 0$ and $\mathcal{I}(y_t)=\mathcal{I}(y_0)$ for all $t\ge 0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{remark}[What the theorem is doing]
Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance} turns the informal slogan ``a semantic system is alive if it can absorb perturbations and remain itself'' into a checkable condition: \emph{existence of an invariant full subcategory on which identity is constant}. This is the categorical analogue of organizational closure. In the sequel, we will add (i) a metric structure that measures how close one is to leaving $\Omega$, and (ii) dynamics that predict when invariance breaks.
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}[On proofs and mild finiteness assumptions]
The direction (1)$\Rightarrow$(2) is immediate: invariance provides a trajectory by iterating $F$. The converse (2)$\Rightarrow$(1) is obtained by letting $\Omega$ be the full subcategory generated by the states visited by the trajectory (and their identity-preserving morphisms). If one wants a compactness-style statement (existence of an infinite path from local branching), one can invoke standard graph-theoretic compactness arguments (e.g.\ König's lemma) under finite-branching assumptions; we defer these details to the Appendix.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Concrete example: a legal code as a semantic category}
We now instantiate the definitions with an example that is concrete enough to remove ambiguity.
\begin{remark}[Example: constitutional or statutory code]
Let $Y$ be the set of \emph{recognized states} of a legal code: this includes the official text at time $t$, its codified amendments, and any canonical consolidations (e.g.\ annotated versions maintained by an authority). Define $\mathcal{I}(y)$ to be the \emph{legal-identity class} of $y$: two states $y,y'$ share identity if they are recognized by the relevant authority as instantiations of ``the same code'' (as opposed to a new constitution, a new regime, or a replacement code).
Define the semantic category $\mathsf{C}$:
\begin{itemize}
\item Objects are legal-code states $y\in Y$.
\item Morphisms $f:y\to y'$ are \emph{identity-preserving legal transformations}, such as:
  (i) formally valid amendments enacted under the code's own amendment rules;
  (ii) codifications that reorganize without changing legal effect;
  (iii) authoritative interpretations that resolve ambiguity while remaining within recognized jurisprudential constraints.
\item Composition is sequential enactment/interpretation/codification.
\end{itemize}
Now define a perturbation--repair transition $F$ capturing the world-plus-system:
\begin{itemize}
\item Perturbations include societal shocks that produce pressure toward reinterpretation (new technologies, new conflicts, incentive changes), or attempts to route around procedure (informal practices, selective enforcement).
\item Repair mechanisms include judicial review, appellate structure, procedural constraints, institutional memory, and enforcement feedback, which attempt to map pressures back into admissible, identity-preserving change.
\end{itemize}
A candidate identity basin $\Omega$ consists of all states that the system still recognizes as ``this code'' rather than a replacement. Invariance of $\Omega$ under $F$ states: \emph{after each perturbation--repair cycle, the resulting legal state remains a recognized instantiation of the same code}. When this fails (e.g.\ the code is no longer authoritative, procedure breaks, or competing identity criteria dominate), $\Omega$ ceases to be invariant and the system undergoes semantic death or speciation.
\end{remark}
This example highlights a crucial methodological point: nothing in the definition of $\mathsf{C}$ presupposes that individuals believe in the code. The category is defined by \emph{recognized admissibility and identity criteria}, which can persist as institutional facts even under widespread dissent.

\section{Information Geometry on Semantic Categories}
\label{sec:info-geometry}
Section~\ref{sec:sith-category} characterized semantic viability in purely structural terms: a system persists if and only if there exists an identity basin that is invariant under the perturbation--repair transition. In this section, we refine that result by equipping the same categorical base with a \emph{metric structure} that quantifies how close a system is to leaving its identity basin.
The role of information geometry in SITH is not foundational. It does not redefine viability. Rather, it provides:
(i) a notion of distance to basin exit,
(ii) a quantitative error threshold,
and (iii) sufficient conditions under which the categorical invariance condition of Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance} fails with high probability.
\subsection{Observable representations of semantic states}
The categorical objects of $\mathsf{C}$ are semantic states $y\in Y$, which need not themselves be numerical. To introduce a metric, we proceed via observables.
\begin{definition}[Observable representation]
\label{def:observable-representation}
An \emph{observable representation} of a semantic category $\mathsf{C}$ is a map
\[
\mathcal{O}:Y\to \mathcal{M},
\]
where $\mathcal{M}$ is a space of empirical or induced distributions (or, more generally, feature measures) that can be estimated from data associated with the semantic state $y$.
\end{definition}
Examples of observables include:
- term-frequency or topic distributions induced by a corpus of texts,
- citation or reference distributions induced by scholarly practice,
- usage or invocation distributions induced by software execution,
- interpretation-frequency distributions induced by judicial rulings.
The choice of $\mathcal{O}$ is domain-dependent and explicit; different observables induce different geometric structures on the same semantic category.
\subsection{Information divergence on semantic states}
Given an observable representation, we introduce a divergence that measures semantic displacement.
\begin{definition}[Information divergence]
\label{def:info-divergence}
Let $\mathcal{O}:Y\to\mathcal{M}$ be an observable representation. An \emph{information divergence} is a function
\[
D:Y\times Y\to [0,\infty]
\]
of the form
\[
D(y,y'):=\delta\bigl(\mathcal{O}(y),\mathcal{O}(y')\bigr),
\]
where $\delta$ is a divergence or pseudometric on $\mathcal{M}$ (e.g.\ Jensen--Shannon divergence, total variation distance, Wasserstein distance).
\end{definition}
No assumption is made that $D$ is symmetric or satisfies the triangle inequality; all results below require only that $D(y,y)=0$ and that larger values correspond to greater semantic displacement.
\begin{remark}[Specialization to Fisher geometry]
If $\mathcal{O}(y)$ is a smooth parametric family of probability distributions $p_\theta$ with identifiable parameters $\theta(y)$, and $\delta$ is chosen as the infinitesimal Kullback--Leibler divergence, then $D$ locally induces a Fisher information metric. This specialization is optional and invoked only when the statistical assumptions required for Fisher geometry are satisfied.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Identity basins as geometric sublevel sets}
The categorical identity basin of Definition~\ref{def:basin-subcategory} can now be given a geometric boundary.
\begin{definition}[Geometric identity basin]
\label{def:geometric-basin}
Fix a reference state $y_0\in Y$ and divergence $D$. For $\varepsilon>0$, define the \emph{geometric identity basin}
\[
\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0):=\{y\in Y:\ \mathcal{I}(y)=\mathcal{I}(y_0)\ \text{and}\ D(y,y_0)\le \varepsilon\}.
\]
\end{definition}
When $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$ is nonempty and closed under identity-preserving morphisms, it defines a full subcategory of $\mathsf{C}$. The parameter $\varepsilon$ measures tolerance to semantic drift: larger $\varepsilon$ corresponds to weaker identity constraints.
\subsection{Error threshold and loss of invariance}
We now state the central quantitative refinement of Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance}. Informally, the error threshold is the point at which perturbations overwhelm the system's ability to remain within its identity basin.
\begin{proposition}[Geometric error threshold]
\label{prop:error-threshold}
Let $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$ be a geometric identity basin and let $F$ be a perturbation--repair transition. Suppose there exists a stochastic representation of $F$ such that successive states $y_{t+1}\in F(y_t)$ induce a stochastic process on $Y$.
If there exists $\varepsilon^\ast>0$ (the \emph{error threshold}) such that for all $\varepsilon<\varepsilon^\ast$,
\[
\mathbb{P}\bigl(y_{t+1}\in \Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)\mid y_t\in \Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)\bigr) < 1
\]
and the exit probability is bounded away from zero uniformly in $t$, then with probability one the process eventually leaves $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$.
Consequently, for $\varepsilon<\varepsilon^\ast$, $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$ is not invariant under $F$, and the semantic system is not viable at tolerance $\varepsilon$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{remark}[Connection to Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance}]
Proposition~\ref{prop:error-threshold} provides sufficient conditions for the failure of the invariant-basin condition of Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance}. In other words, information-geometric drift beyond the error threshold implies categorical non-viability. Conversely, maintaining invariance requires that perturbation--repair dynamics remain confined within a basin whose geometric radius exceeds the effective noise scale.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Worked example: scientific paradigms}
We illustrate the construction with a simplified but concrete example.
\begin{remark}[Example: scientific paradigm]
Let $Y$ be the set of recognized states of a scientific paradigm (e.g.\ classical mechanics, plate tectonics, the standard model), where each state includes:
(i) a canonical set of concepts,
(ii) a body of accepted results,
(iii) an associated literature.
Define $\mathcal{I}(y)$ to encode paradigm identity (``this is still Newtonian mechanics''). Let $\mathcal{O}(y)$ be the empirical distribution of terminology and citation patterns extracted from the active literature at state $y$.
Choose $\delta$ as the Jensen--Shannon divergence, yielding an information divergence $D(y,y')$ between paradigm states. The geometric identity basin $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$ then consists of all states whose empirical practice remains within divergence $\varepsilon$ of the reference paradigm $y_0$.
Perturbations include anomalous results, new instruments, or external explanatory pressures; repair mechanisms include reinterpretation, auxiliary hypotheses, and pedagogical normalization. When accumulated divergence exceeds the error threshold $\varepsilon^\ast$, the basin ceases to be invariant: attempts at repair fail, and a paradigm shift occurs. This corresponds to semantic death or speciation in the categorical sense.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Role of information geometry in SITH}
We emphasize that information geometry does not replace the categorical notion of viability. It answers a different question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Given a semantic system that is viable in principle, how close is it to losing invariance under realistic perturbations?}
\end{quote}
In Section~\ref{sec:rsvp}, we turn from static geometry to dynamics, introducing a field-theoretic model whose attractors correspond to invariant identity basins and whose instabilities realize the error-threshold phenomena described here.

\section{RSVP Dynamics and the Temporal Stability of Semantic Systems}
\label{sec:rsvp}
Sections~\ref{sec:sith-category} and~\ref{sec:info-geometry} characterized semantic viability in structural and geometric terms. Viability was shown to be equivalent to the existence of an invariant identity basin, while information geometry quantified proximity to basin exit via an error threshold. In this section, we introduce a dynamical model that governs \emph{when and how} those invariance conditions are maintained or lost over time.
The RSVP framework does not redefine semantic viability. Rather, it proposes a class of dynamical systems whose stable attractors correspond to invariant identity basins in the sense of Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance}, and whose instabilities realize the error-threshold phenomena described in Proposition~\ref{prop:error-threshold}. RSVP thus provides a mechanism-level account of semantic persistence and collapse.
\subsection{Domain of the RSVP fields}
The categorical objects of $\mathsf{C}$ are semantic states $y\in Y$, while Section~\ref{sec:info-geometry} introduced observable representations $\mathcal{O}:Y\to\mathcal{M}$ into a space of empirical distributions or features. RSVP dynamics are defined on this observable space $\mathcal{M}$, which is the level at which distance, drift, and accumulation of uncertainty are measurable.
Concretely, we assume that $\mathcal{M}$ admits a spatial or relational structure (e.g.\ a manifold, graph, or metric space) on which local variation and transport are meaningful. Points of $\mathcal{M}$ correspond to observable semantic configurations, and trajectories in $\mathcal{M}$ correspond to the evolution of empirical practice, interpretation, or usage associated with a semantic system.
\subsection{RSVP fields}
We introduce three coupled fields on $\mathcal{M}\times \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$:
\emph{The coherence potential} $\Phi(x,t)$ measures the local strength of identity enforcement at location $x\in\mathcal{M}$ and time $t$. High values of $\Phi$ correspond to strong institutional, procedural, or structural mechanisms that resist semantic drift and promote repair.
\emph{The propagation flow} $\vec v(x,t)$ is a vector field describing directed transport of semantic material across $\mathcal{M}$. It captures processes such as diffusion of interpretations, migration of practices, or spread of conventions through a population or corpus.
\emph{The entropy density} $S(x,t)$ measures the local rate of distinguishability loss, ambiguity accumulation, or proliferation of incompatible interpretations. High entropy corresponds to semantic fragmentation and erosion of identity.
These fields are not primitives of meaning; they are coarse-grained descriptors of how observable semantic configurations evolve under perturbation and repair.
\subsection{A minimal coupled dynamical system}
To make the framework explicit, we write a simplified but representative system of coupled partial differential equations. The precise coefficients and functional forms are application-dependent; the purpose here is to show that RSVP constitutes a concrete dynamical system rather than metaphorical notation.
We consider:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial t}
&= -\alpha\, \Phi\, S + \beta\, \nabla^2 \Phi + \gamma\, R(x,t),
\label{eq:phi}\\
\frac{\partial S}{\partial t}
&= \delta\, |\vec v|^2 \Phi - \varepsilon\, S + \zeta\, \eta(x,t),
\label{eq:s}\\
\frac{\partial \vec v}{\partial t}
&= -\nabla \Phi - \kappa\, \vec v + \mu\, \nabla S.
\label{eq:v}
\end{align}
Here $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta,\varepsilon,\kappa,\mu$ are nonnegative constants. The term $R(x,t)$ represents endogenous repair or correction mechanisms (e.g.\ judicial review, testing infrastructure, editorial standards), while $\eta(x,t)$ represents exogenous noise or perturbation sources.
Equation~\eqref{eq:phi} expresses the idea that coherence is degraded by local entropy but replenished by repair and smoothed by diffusion. Equation~\eqref{eq:s} models entropy growth driven by propagation and suppressed by dissipative mechanisms. Equation~\eqref{eq:v} couples flow to gradients in coherence and entropy, producing directed transport toward or away from coherent regions.
\subsection{Invariant basins as dynamical attractors}
We now state the central connection between RSVP dynamics and the categorical notion of viability.
\begin{proposition}[RSVP attractors and categorical invariance]
\label{prop:rsvp-invariance}
Suppose the RSVP system \eqref{eq:phi}-\eqref{eq:v} admits a compact attractor $A\subset \mathcal{M}$ such that:
(i) $\Phi(x,t)$ is uniformly bounded below by a positive constant on $A$,
(ii) $S(x,t)$ is uniformly bounded above on $A$,
and (iii) trajectories initialized in $A$ remain in $A$ for all future time.
Then the preimage $\Omega=\mathcal{O}^{-1}(A)\subseteq Y$ defines an identity basin that is invariant under the perturbation--repair transition $F$. Consequently, the corresponding semantic system is viable in the sense of Theorem~\ref{thm:viability-invariance}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:rsvp-invariance} formalizes the intuitive claim that stable semantic systems correspond to dynamical regimes in which coherence dominates entropy and perturbations are continually absorbed. RSVP dynamics do not define viability; they realize it when their attractors satisfy the stated bounds.
\end{remark}
\subsection{Instability and the geometric error threshold}
The geometric error threshold of Section~\ref{sec:info-geometry} corresponds, in RSVP terms, to a loss of stability of the attractor.
\begin{proposition}[RSVP instability and semantic collapse]
\label{prop:rsvp-collapse}
If parameter drift or increased noise causes $\Phi$ to decay or $S$ to grow such that no compact attracting set satisfies the conditions of Proposition~\ref{prop:rsvp-invariance}, then trajectories in $\mathcal{M}$ exit every bounded neighborhood corresponding to an identity basin. In this regime, the associated geometric basin $\Omega_\varepsilon(y_0)$ is not invariant, and semantic collapse or speciation occurs.
