Here is a summary of the paper "Gallery Before Feed: Why Engagement-Optimized Interfaces Destroy Coherence and How Merge-Aware Systems Restore Meaning":
---
Core Thesis
The paper argues that the dominant feed-based architecture of social media platforms—optimized for engagement—systematically undermines coherence, memory, and identity. In response, it proposes a gallery-first, merge-aware system that treats content as persistent semantic objects, enabling meaning to accumulate rather than fragment.
---
Key Problems with Feed-Based Architectures
1. Semantic Fragmentation: Posts are treated as isolated, chronological instances rather than as references to persistent objects.
2. Forced Duplication: Reposting creates new instances instead of reinforcing a single referent.
3. Degraded Search & Memory: Search returns surfaces (instances) rather than essences (objects), making retrieval incoherent.
4. Monetization Intrusion: Platforms embed promotional affordances directly into artifacts, framing content as performance rather than memory.
5. Identity Fragmentation: Profile elements (e.g., cover photos) are duplicated across surfaces without shared identity.
---
Theoretical Foundations
The critique is grounded in three frameworks:
1. Conceptual Blending Theory: Feeds act as perpetual blend spaces, preventing meaning from stabilizing.
2. Remix Culture: Platforms privilege propagation and transformation over preservation, eroding archival integrity.
3. Analogy & Essence Recognition: Humans recognize essences across variations; feed architectures bind meaning to instances, blocking this cognitive process.
---
Proposed Alternative: Gallery-First, Merge-Aware Systems
· Semantic Objects: Content (images, text) is treated as persistent objects with stable identity.
· Events as References: Posting, liking, commenting are events that reference objects, not create new instances.
· Merge Operations: Systems can consolidate multiple references to the same object, preventing duplication.
· Projections, Not Feeds: Feeds are one view among many (e.g., gallery, search, timeline), not the primary structure.
· Latent Affordances: Interaction prompts appear only after deliberate user action, preserving quiet viewing.
---
Formal Guarantees
· Semantic Accumulation: Meaning strengthens through repetition and revisitation.
· Memoization: Frequently revisited objects become easier to retrieve.
· Search Correctness: Search operates over objects, not instances, ensuring coherent retrieval.
· State Transitions: Explicit rules govern shifts between gallery, interaction, and annotation modes, preventing accidental engagement.
---
Formal Proofs
The paper includes a formal appendix proving:
· Semantic drift is inevitable in feed-based systems under minimal assumptions.
· Semantic accumulation is guaranteed in merge-aware systems.
· Engagement optimization and meaning preservation are structurally incompatible without object-level identity and merge operations.
---
Conclusion
Meaning is an architectural property, not an engagement metric. Feed-based systems prioritize stimulation and monetization at the expense of coherence and memory. Gallery-first systems restore the conditions for durable meaning by separating objects from events, enabling merge, and prioritizing recognition over reaction. The shift required is not incremental but foundational—a move from feeds as ontology to feeds as one projection among many.
---
Implication
The design of social systems must choose between optimizing for engagement and supporting meaning. The paper advocates for architectures that conserve meaning by design, aligning digital infrastructure with human cognition and long-term sense-making.

the formal proofs established in the appendix demonstrate a structural (rather than empirical) necessity for how digital interfaces affect meaning over time

Semantic Drift vs. Semantic Accumulation
The paper contrasts two outcomes based on the underlying architecture of a social system
:
Semantic Drift:
This is the inevitable outcome of
feed-based architectures
.
Because these systems treat content as "feed-ordered atoms"—isolated, chronological instances rather than persistent objects—meaning is tied to a specific context at a specific time
.
As the content is reposted or recontextualized, it creates new, unrelated instances
.
Consequently, meaning "dissolves" or "drifts" because the system cannot recognize that these multiple surface encounters refer to the same underlying essence
.
Semantic Accumulation:
This is the guaranteed outcome of
merge-aware systems
.
In this architecture, content (images, text) is treated as a persistent
semantic object
with a stable identity
.
Interactions like posting, liking, or commenting are treated as
events
that reference these objects rather than creating new instances
.
This separation allows meaning to strengthen and "accumulate" through repetition and revisitation rather than fragmenting
.
Summary of Formal Proofs
The paper's formal appendix includes a "structural theorem" that arrives at three primary conclusions
:
Inevitability of Drift in Feeds:
Under minimal assumptions, semantic drift is unavoidable in feed-based systems, regardless of the quality of moderation or the accuracy of recommendation algorithms
.
Guaranteed Accumulation in Merge-Aware Systems:
Meaning preservation is mathematically guaranteed when systems adhere to merge-aware constraints, such as explicit object identity and lossless consolidation
.
Structural Incompatibility:
The proofs establish that
engagement optimization
and
meaning preservation
are structurally incompatible
.
A system that binds reactions to "instances" (posts) rather than "semantic objects" (essences) is fundamentally incapable of preserving meaning over time
.
Ultimately, the paper argues that for a digital system to support memory and identity, it must abandon the "feed-ordered atom" and adopt an architecture that allows for
merge operations
, enabling a move from "feeds as ontology" to "feeds as one projection among many"
.
