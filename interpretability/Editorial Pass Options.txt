I.
Labels are tricky, duplicitous things. This has been a theme in my writing, and maybe one of the most useful graphics I've ever made is the one below (although it might prove less useful if I just throw it at you without explanation, which I am unfortunately going to do this time):




For instance: how do you decide whether someone is a Christian?

The simplest and most straightforward way is "they say so."

But this feels ... dissatisfying, to me. It feels like Christianity ought to mean something, beyond being a pure self-label. It feels like saying that you're a Christian ought to convey information.

The next most obvious tack to take, in my opinion, is "okay, someone is a Christian if they believe in Christ, and follow the teachings of the mythical Christ as recorded in the various religious texts that make up the New Testament."

This ... also doesn't work, mostly because it would disqualify the majority of people-we-mean-when-we-say-Christians (at least in my country), and, uh. Words mean what they're used to mean, not what some technical definition wishes they meant.

So maybe "Christian" is just "what people agree are Christians." But—

[insert multiple centuries of war in Europe]

—yeah.

Ultimately, it seems to me that the best tradeoff for understanding what a Christian is/what the label "Christian" means is my diagram.

In other words:

There are a bunch of different ways to end up under the umbrella of "Christian." They overlap some, and they disoverlap some, and some of them maybe even don't overlap at all, but they all fall within some definable contiguous space. When you say "So-and-so is a Christian," this is a sazen, and it will cause people to both a) project onto that person some things that aren't true about them, and b) miss some things about that person that are.

And that's just ... how it works. That's what it means to adopt and use a label. There doesn't seem to be any way around it, at current levels of tech.

I'm trans.

I've hesitated to say this out loud for a while, because of [multiple centuries of war in Europe]. I have trans friends and trans colleagues, some of whom I expect will be mad at me for calling myself trans—who will feel suspicious and possessive and somehow undermined or threatened.

But in the "you're Christian if you believe in Christ and follow the teachings of Christ" sense, I just am an instance of the thing that is meant by the word "transgender."

An instance.

Not all of the instances.

A somewhat weird instance, even.

But I'm straightforwardly inside that big dotted circle. I'm one of those odd blob shapes, and I overlap substantially with a lot of the other blob shapes.

III.
In the spirit of completeness, it seems worth pausing to note a couple of the bits of evidence that cause me to assert the true fact that I belong under the label, and a couple of the bits of evidence that I think make me vulnerable to delegitimization.

Some common-to-many-if-not-all-trans-folk experiences that I have:

Body dysmorphia. The body that I see in the mirror doesn't fit me. The face I see in the mirror isn't right. If you were to plug me into the construct program from The Matrix, my brain would rebuild a noticeably different avatar-of-me, like how in the original script the character Switch was going to be (iirc) physically female, but show up in the system as male (I might have it backwards).

Objections to puberty. Many trans folk feel like they got the wrong puberty, and would've been genuinely happy with a different one, and my objection is a bit different from that. But trans folk often have a good comrades-in-suffering time, sitting around the campfire telling horror stories and talking about how biology is a shitshow and needs to be fixed, in a way that 90+% of the population doesn't understand but I do.

Gender dysphoria. People look at me, and they (very reasonably, and with tremendous justification based on all of their experiences) assume things about what kind of person I am. What my internal experience is like. What my values and goals are. What roles I will be comfortable playing, in certain social machines. And they are wrong, and their expectations chafe, and I feel crushed into a box that is not the right shape for me, and I feel confused and sad because boxes that are the right shape for me are right there, why can't I just go be in those boxes—

(I have written about this before:)




My discomfort with "he/him" pronouns.
Societal revulsion and suspicion. People (often) do not understand me, and they (often) respond to their own lack-of-understanding with judgment and fear. People sometimes think that e.g. the experience I am attempting to describe is a weird sex thing, instead of a whole personality thing. People sometimes think that the ways in which I defy the standard categories are dangerous, either in the general sense of "if this guy breaks these rules he might break other rules, who knows," or in more specific senses like "ah we shouldn't let this guy talk to e.g. young impressionable children."

There are also some fairly standard-to-trans-folk experiences that I don't have. For instance:

Growing up with the wrong childhood. I didn't. I had zero problems with my boyhood. Indeed, I have at least once been described as "actually the only cisgender person I know," given that the person speaking to me knew no other people who still identified as the gender they were assigned early in life.

Agonizing over transition. I don't. I can't. There's technology out there to help the more common sort of trans person fix some of the object-level problems. I'm sure there's some technology out there to help me, but it's way more niche and experimental and generally the problems are much harder and I basically don't even get the option of agonizing over whether or not I should pour multiple years' worth of salary into trying to fix things. (I kind of wish I did.)

...I'm going to stop there. I could expand both lists to a dozen-or-more bullet points, but that's not what this essay is for. I just wanted to get a little bit concrete, with the claim that I'm one of the blob shapes.

IV.
What's changed, then? Why now specifically?

Part of it is some shifts that I've been making over the past two-or-so years, that have made me somewhat braver and somewhat more stoic. Part of it is just ... getting used to the idea. But the biggest part is:




I hope to be pleasantly surprised, about how many of my trans brethren do acknowledge and accept me.

I'm braced for them rejecting me, and considering me not-as-much-one-of-them as I honestly think that I am.

(Approximately nobody in trans communities says that a trans woman who is pre-transition and closeted isn't a trans woman, and I think I am no more distant from the central, median trans person than that.)

But regardless of whether they acknowledge or deny the validity of my claim to the label, we're on the same team. We already were, in that I'm queer and neurodivergent, but fuck it, I can add this flag to the flagpole, too.
