The coherence-first architecture presented in "Coherence Before Engagement" is a formal alternative to feed-based social media that prioritizes persistent meaning and structural integrity over engagement metrics
.
The architecture is built on three core pillars: structured semantic representation, constrained evolution of meaning, and spatial navigation
.
1. The Core Interaction Unit: Semantic Objects
In this architecture, the primary unit of interaction is the
semantic object
(
σ
) rather than a post or message
.
Structured Entity
: A semantic object is defined by an immutable identity (
I
), required modalities (
M
, e.g., text, data, code), a provenance graph (
P
), and a scalar value representing its semantic entropy (
E
)
.
Well-Formedness
: For an object to be "well-formed," every required modality must have assigned content
.
For instance, a scientific claim requiring "text" and "data" is not well-formed until both are provided
.
Persistence
: Unlike ephemeral feed items, these objects persist across time and accumulate structure through controlled transformations
.
2. Principles of Evolution and Coherence
Meaning evolves through formal rules designed to prevent the "semantic drift" seen in traditional social media
.
Typed Semantic Transformations
: Changes to an object (revisions, merges, translations) are "typed" operations with explicit input/output requirements
.
Entropy-Bounded Evolution
: Every transformation has a fixed "entropy budget" (
ϵ
)
.
A transformation is only accepted if it does not increase the object's internal inconsistency or ambiguity beyond this budget
.
Monotonic Provenance
: Every change is recorded in a directed acyclic graph (DAG)
.
This ensures that all content has a traceable history that cannot be erased or repudiated
.
Conflict Resolution (Forking and Merging)
: When irreconcilable changes occur, the system creates parallel branches
.
These can later be "merged" through reconciliation transformations that must also satisfy entropy constraints
.
3. Navigation and Discovery: Semantic Neighborhoods
The architecture replaces "feeds" with a spatial model of discovery
.
Latent Semantic Space
: Objects are embedded in a high-dimensional manifold based on their meaning and provenance
.
Neighborhood Navigation
: Users discover content by moving through "semantic neighborhoods"—regions of space defined by similarity in topics or transformation history
.
Decoupling Relevance from Recency
: Because objects persist in stable locations, high-quality material remains discoverable long after its creation, rather than being buried by a chronological feed
.
4. Redefining Trust and Identity
Trust is a structural property rather than a popularity signal
.
Persistence-Based Trust
: Instead of likes or follower counts, trust is measured by a user's "provenance-anchored identity"
.
It accrues through demonstrated consistency, entropy stability, and successful reconciliations over time
.
Accountability
: Because every movement or transformation is recorded in semantic space, users are held accountable for their cumulative contributions
.

Essentially, "Coherence Before Engagement" proposes a social system where the
stability of meaning
is more important than how many people see or "like" it
.
Instead of a traditional social media feed, imagine a system built like a digital library or map where information has to follow strict rules to exist and grow
. Here is a simpler breakdown of how it works:
1. From "Posts" to "Semantic Objects"
On regular social media, you share "posts" that are quickly buried by newer content
.
The Change
: This system uses
semantic objects
—structured units of information that stay in one place and evolve over time
.
Example
: Instead of posting 100 separate tweets about a scientific discovery, there would be one "object" for that discovery
.
Any new data, counter-arguments, or corrections are attached directly to it
.
2. "Entropy" as a Speed Limit for Chaos
"Semantic drift" is when a discussion becomes a messy argument where nobody agrees on the facts
.
The Change
: The system measures
entropy
—a score for how much confusion, contradiction, or ambiguity is in an object
.
The Rule
: You can only change or add to an object if your contribution doesn't make it too messy
.
If a change adds too much confusion, the system mechanically rejects it
.
3. Maps Instead of Feeds
Traditional platforms use algorithms to push "engaging" (often controversial) content into your feed
.
The Change
: There are no feeds
.
Instead, objects occupy a stable spot in a
semantic neighborhood
based on what they are about
.
Discovery
: You find things by "walking" through this digital map
.
If you are interested in physics, you go to the physics "neighborhood" where the information is stored permanently, rather than waiting for it to pop up on a timeline
.
4. Trust Based on History, Not Popularity
Currently, "trust" is often just a high follower count or a blue checkmark
.
The Change
: Trust is
provenance-anchored
.
The system keeps an unchangeable record (a provenance graph) of every single change made to an object and who made it
.
How it works
: You earn trust by consistently making information clearer and more accurate over a long period, not by going viral
.

That is a fascinating connection. By framing the system as a specification for "persistent meaning," you've effectively adapted the core logic of
Version Control Systems (VCS)
like Git and applied it to the "real world" of social discourse.
The architecture you described uses several key metaphors from version control and the physical world to fix the problems of modern social media:
1. Version Control as a Metaphor for Truth
In your system, meaning is treated like
source code
.
Just as a programmer doesn't just "post" a line of code and move on, a user in your system interacts with "semantic objects" that have a permanent history
.
The "Git" of Ideas
: You draw direct inspiration from Git's
provenance tracking
and
branching
.
Monotonic Provenance
: Every change is "append-only," meaning you can't delete the past to change a narrative; you can only add a new "commit" (transformation) that explains or retracts it
.
Semantic Merging
: Unlike Git, which deals with text changes (syntactic), your system uses
entropy-bounded merging
to ensure that when two ideas are combined, the resulting meaning is still clear and coherent (semantic)
.
2. The Physical World: "Neighborhoods" vs. "Feeds"
You replace the metaphorical "firehose" or "feed" of social media with a
spatial metaphor
.
Digital Cities
: You compare the system's growth to a physical city that scales by adding
neighborhoods
rather than forcing everyone down one main street (the global feed)
.
Spatial Accountability
: In the real world, if you say something, it happened at a specific place and time.
Your system mimics this by anchoring objects in a stable "semantic space"
.
You can't just "troll" and disappear; your actions are recorded as movements in that space, creating a form of
geographic accountability
.
3. Programming as the New Literacy
One of the most unique aspects of your specification is that it treats
programming as a first-class social function
.
SpherePOP Calculus
: You've designed a language where social interaction
is
the execution of a program
.
Learning as Participation
: In current social media, "using" the app just means scrolling.
In your system, "using" the app actually teaches you how to
construct and navigate meaning
through code
.
Time spent on the platform produces a durable skill (programming literacy) rather than just "engagement"
.
4. Determinism and the "Late-Joiner"
Finally, the metaphor of a
ledger or event log
ensures that the "real world" facts are consistent for everyone
.
Replay Semantics
: Because the system is deterministic, any "late-joiner" can replay the history of events and arrive at the exact same state of truth as everyone else
.
This mirrors the scientific method or a legal record—anyone can "check the work" to see how a conclusion was reached
.

Geometric Bayesianism with Sparse Heuristics
In biological systems, sparsity emerges as a natural consequence of physiological pressures, including energetic constraints, signal noise, and chemical gradients, obviating the need for explicit computational penalties to enforce minimal network complexity.
Metabolic costs, such as ATP consumption in neural and cellular activity, favor sparse activation patterns, acting as an implicit prior for minimal energy configurations. Environmental noise and signal overlap amplify high-salience, sparsely distributed signals, while thermodynamic gradients drive systems toward low-entropy states.
This Natural Sparsity Principle underpins the efficiency of biological cognition and informs computational models like Geometric Bayesianism with Sparse Heuristics (GBSH), suggesting that sparse proxy navigation can be an emergent property in biomimetic systems, enhancing their adaptability and resilience in noisy, resource-constrained environments.
