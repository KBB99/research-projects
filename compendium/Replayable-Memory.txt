Replayable Memory and the Cognitive Basis of Abstraction
Flyxion
December 14, 2025
Abstract
Abstraction is often treated as a linguistic or symbolic capacity, emerging late in cognitive
development and absent in non-human animals. At the same time, artificial systems demonstrate
strong compression and generalization without exhibiting robust abstraction. This paper argues
that abstraction is neither linguistic nor representational in origin, but arises from a more basic
cognitive capacity: replayable memory of construction. We propose that abstraction consists in
stabilizing semantic equivalence across distinct experiential histories, and that this stabilization
requires access to replayable internal traces. This account explains why abstraction appears early
in development, is present in animals, and remains elusive in many artificial systems despite
scale and data. The framework integrates findings from memory research, learning theory, and
comparative cognition, offering a unifying account of abstraction as a functional, pre-linguistic
capacity.

Introduction
Abstraction is central to human cognition. It allows individuals to recognize sameness across variation,
to generalize beyond surface features, and to transfer knowledge across contexts. Traditionally,
abstraction has been associated with symbolic reasoning, language, or explicit concept formation. On
this view, abstraction is a late-emerging cognitive achievement layered atop more basic perceptual
and associative processes.
At the same time, empirical evidence challenges this picture. Infants exhibit abstraction-like
capacities prior to language acquisition. Non-human animals demonstrate flexible generalization
across perceptually dissimilar stimuli. Conversely, many artificial systems that excel at pattern
recognition and statistical learning fail to exhibit robust abstraction under recombination or
perturbation.
These observations suggest that abstraction is neither equivalent to language nor reducible
to compression. This paper proposes an alternative account: abstraction arises from replayable
memory of construction. On this view, abstraction is a functional property of systems that can
reconstruct, compare, and stabilize the consequences of their own past interactions.

Compression and Its Cognitive Limits
Compression plays a central role in theories of learning and memory. From classical associative
learning to modern neural network models, cognitive efficiency is often understood in terms of
reducing redundancy and extracting regularities from experience.
Compression supports prediction and generalization within familiar regimes. However, compres-
sion alone does not determine which regularities should remain stable under novel conditions. A
compressed representation may entangle causal and incidental features, yielding brittle behavior
when circumstances change.
In biological cognition, compression is constrained by additional mechanisms that regulate which
distinctions matter. These mechanisms are not purely statistical. They depend on how experiences
were generated, not merely on their aggregate structure.
This distinction becomes evident in developmental trajectories. Children often overgeneralize
early, then refine abstractions through interaction and feedback. What changes is not merely repre-
sentational efficiency, but sensitivity to the consequences of alternative actions and interpretations.

Abstraction as Semantic Stability
We define abstraction in terms of semantic stability rather than representational form.
Definition 3.1 (Semantic Effect). A semantic effect is any consequence of an internal structure on
future perception, action, inference, or learning.
Definition 3.2 (Semantic Equivalence). Two internal structures are semantically equivalent if they
produce indistinguishable semantic effects across the system's future interactions.
Abstraction consists in recognizing and enforcing such equivalence. This recognition reduces the
number of distinct internal states that must be tracked, without collapsing distinctions that matter
for future behavior.
Crucially, semantic equivalence is counterfactual. It concerns how the system would behave under
alternative trajectories, not only what it has already experienced. Determining such equivalence
requires access to the processes by which internal structures were formed.

Replayable Memory
Memory is often treated as storage: a repository of past states or experiences. However, extensive
evidence from neuroscience and psychology suggests that memory is reconstructive rather than
archival. Past experiences are replayed, reactivated, and recombined in ways that support learning,
planning, and generalization.
We formalize this capacity as replayable memory.
Definition 4.1 (Replayable Memory). Replayable memory is the capacity of a system to reconstruct
the semantic consequences of past experiential histories, potentially under variation or recombination.
2

Replay need not be exact or deterministic at the neural level. What matters is functional access
to the structure of past interactions sufficient to evaluate alternative continuations.
Proposition 4.1. A system lacking replayable memory cannot, in general, determine semantic
equivalence across distinct experiential histories.
Proof. Semantic equivalence depends on future consequences under alternative trajectories. Without
replayable access to how internal structures were formed, the system lacks the information required
to evaluate such counterfactuals. Equivalence judgments must therefore rely on surface similarity or
statistical coincidence.
Replayable memory provides a mechanism by which abstraction can be grounded in experience
rather than imposed symbolically.

Developmental and Comparative Evidence
The proposed account aligns with evidence from developmental psychology. Infants demonstrate
abstraction-like abilities before acquiring language, such as recognizing numerical equivalence or
object permanence across transformations. These capacities emerge alongside the maturation of
memory systems that support replay and consolidation.
In comparative cognition, animals exhibit abstraction in domains relevant to survival, such as
tool use or social inference, despite lacking symbolic language. These abilities are closely tied to
experience-dependent learning and memory replay, particularly during rest or sleep.
The present framework predicts that abstraction should correlate with the capacity for replayable
memory rather than with linguistic ability. This prediction is consistent with observed dissociations
between language competence and abstract reasoning across species and developmental stages.

Implications for Artificial Systems
Artificial systems trained via large-scale optimization often exhibit impressive compression but
limited abstraction. They generalize within the training distribution but struggle with compositional
novelty or causal intervention.
From the present perspective, this limitation arises because such systems lack replayable access
to their own construction histories. Training traces are typically discarded once parameters are
updated. The system retains the result of learning but not the process by which it was achieved.
This design choice precludes principled evaluation of semantic equivalence. Without replay, the
system cannot distinguish stable invariants from accidental correlations. Abstraction must therefore
be approximated indirectly, often with limited success.
The framework suggests that incorporating replayable memory into artificial systems—whether
through explicit event traces, structured episodic memory, or reconstructive learning dynamics—may
be necessary for achieving robust abstraction.

7
Discussion
By grounding abstraction in replayable memory, this account reconciles several otherwise puzzling
observations. It explains why abstraction appears early in development, why it is present in animals,
and why it remains elusive in many artificial systems despite scale.
Abstraction is not a matter of possessing symbols or concepts, but of having access to one's own
experiential construction. Language amplifies abstraction by externalizing and sharing stabilized
equivalences, but it is not their source.
This view shifts the focus of cognitive theory from representational format to memory dynamics.
What matters is not what is stored, but what can be reconstructed and compared.
8
Conclusion
We have proposed that abstraction is a functional consequence of replayable memory. By defining
abstraction in terms of semantic equivalence across experiential histories, we obtain a framework
that unifies findings from development, animal cognition, and artificial intelligence.
This account reframes abstraction as a pre-linguistic, experience-grounded capacity rather than
a symbolic achievement. It also clarifies why compression alone is insufficient for abstraction, and
why replayable construction plays a central role in intelligent cognition.
Understanding abstraction in these terms offers a principled basis for comparing natural and
artificial systems and suggests concrete directions for future cognitive and computational research.
