Never Predict Noise:
Manifold-Aligned Prediction as Generative Inference
Flyxion
November 2025
Abstract
Generative and cognitive systems succeed when they exploit the fact that empirical reality occupies
a highly structured, low-dimensional manifold embedded in a much higher-dimensional observation
space.
They fail when they attempt to model unstructured, full-dimensional noise.
This paper
develops a uniﬁed mathematical theory of manifold-aligned generative inference, drawing on: (i)
geometric learning and manifold theory, (ii) generative modelling and active inference, (iii) variational
analysis of semantic potentials, and (iv) category-theoretic models of cognitive loops. We introduce
a rigorous account of why "never predict noise is a structural requirement, not a heuristic, and we
show that generative ﬂows must remain tangent to a learned manifold. We additionally introduce
a new theoretical construction in which CLIO functors—cognitive loop operators—are realized as
Morse ﬂows on semantic manifolds and, more generally, on Whitney-stratiﬁed spaces. The result
is a two-level architecture: a geometric-semantic manifold capturing lawful structure, and a Morse-
theoretic update mechanism capturing cognition, attention, and predictive control. We connect this
framework to recent empirical ﬁndings on Just image Transformers (JiT), which demonstrate that
directly predicting clean data—rather than noise—enables apparently under-capacity networks to
operate eﬀectively in extremely high-dimensional spaces.

Geometric Re-Interpretation
of JiT
We now reinterpret the ﬁndings of Li and He [6]
in the MAGI framework.
Under the manifold assumption, natural im-
ages form a smooth manifold M ⊂Rn.
Noise-
corrupted images lie oﬀM in NxM. Classical dif-
fusion models train networks to predict noise or
noised quantities, eﬀectively learning vector ﬁelds
with substantial support in NxM. Our analysis
predicts such models are geometrically unstable,
especially in high dimensions.
JiT ("Just image Transformers") takes the op-
posite approach: it trains a large-patch Trans-
former to directly predict clean images, without
tokenizers, pre-training, or auxiliary losses. This
forces the learned vector ﬁeld to lie approximately
in TxM, since any normal component would move
predictions away from the ground-truth manifold.
Empirically, JiT produces competitive samples on
high-resolution ImageNet, where noise-prediction
regimes can fail catastrophically.
From a MAGI perspective, JiT operates as a
discretized gradient ﬂow on a learned potential
deﬁned along M. Each generative step remains
tangent to M, implementing a Morse-like ﬂow to-
ward semantically consistent regions.
The suc-
cess of such a simple architecture is therefore ex-
plained not by its capacity, but by the geometric
constraint imposed by the clean-data objective.
JiT thus provides concrete empirical support for
the central theorem of this paper: generative sys-
tems succeed when their dynamics remain on the
data manifold and fail when they are forced into
noise-normal directions.

Related Work
11.1
Manifold Learning and Geometric
Representations
Classical manifold learning methods such as
Isomap, LLE, and Laplacian eigenmaps estab-
lished the empirical plausibility of locally smooth
embeddings for natural data.
Neural mani-
fold models, implicit neural representations, and
diﬀeomorphic generative maps extend this to
deep networks. Our framework diﬀers in explic-
itly incorporating stratiﬁed structure and Morse-
Whitney regularity, enabling semantic transitions
across boundaries and singularities.
11.2
Diﬀusion and Score-Based Models
Denoising diﬀusion models [4] and score-based
generative models [8] learn the score ∇x log pt(x)
of progressively noised data. These methods im-
plicitly predict noise or velocity, encouraging vec-
tor ﬁelds that span NxM. As Li and He [6] argue,
this becomes problematic in high dimensions. Our
geometric formulation identiﬁes noise prediction
with learning vector ﬁelds in the normal bundle
and explains catastrophic failure as an inevitable
consequence of oﬀ-manifold dynamics.
11.3
Flow Matching and ODE-Based
Generative Dynamics
Flow-matching
methods
learn
time-dependent
vector ﬁelds that transport a simple base distri-
bution to the data distribution. While many im-
pose smoothness or regularity constraints, they
rarely enforce tangent-space alignment with M.
MAGI can be viewed as a tangent-constrained
ﬂow-matching framework, where vector ﬁelds are
explicitly projected onto TxM and derived from
Morse potentials.
11.4
Geometric Deep Learning
Geometric deep learning emphasizes symmetry,
invariance, and manifold structure in neural ar-
chitectures [1]. Our work extends this perspective
by emphasizing stratiﬁcation and sheaf coherence:
global consistency of predictions across overlap-
ping contexts, rather than merely local equivari-
ances.
5

11.5
Energy-Based Models and Neural
Potentials
Energy-based models [5] deﬁne an unnormalized
energy function whose gradient guides inference.
By constraining the energy to be a Morse or
stratiﬁed Morse function, we obtain stable, in-
terpretable ﬂows with well-structured equilibria.
CLIO updates then correspond to discrete gradi-
ent ﬂows on such potentials.
11.6
Cognitive Control, Active Infer-
ence, and Sheaves
Active inference and predictive processing con-
ceptualize cognition as recursive error-minimizing
loops.
Sheaf-theoretic models provide a formal
language for contextual integration and consis-
tency. MAGI uniﬁes these ideas in a geometric
setting: cognitive loops are realized as Morse ﬂows
on a semantic manifold, and contextual consis-
tency is enforced by sheaf coherence.

Conclusion
We have developed a uniﬁed geometric framework
for generative inference based on a simple but
powerful principle: a generative model must pre-
dict only within the intrinsic semantic manifold of
natural data, and never along its orthogonal noise
directions. We formalized this in terms of mani-
fold geometry, Morse potentials, Whitney stratiﬁ-
cations, sheaf coherence, and CLIO functors.
On this view, semantic alignment is not primar-
ily about objectives, loss functions, or architec-
tural scale; it is about geometry. Aligned gener-
ative systems are those whose dynamics remain
tangent to the data manifold and whose update
rules prohibit excursions into noise-normal direc-
tions. Recent empirical results such as JiT pro-
vide compelling support for this claim, showing
that even apparently under-capacity models can
generate high-quality samples when constrained
to predict clean data on the manifold.
The MAGI framework suggests several direc-
tions for future work:
empirical evaluation of
stratiﬁed CLIO dynamics in large-scale diﬀusion
and ﬂow models; scalable algorithms for manifold
and stratum recovery; and geometric approaches
to alignment, in which maintaining semantic in-
variants across cognitive loops is enforced as a
structural property rather than an after-the-fact
correction.
In summary, the message of this paper is that
never predicting noise is not a mere engineering
heuristic, but a mathematically principled condi-
tion for stable, coherent, and semantically aligned
generative behavior.
