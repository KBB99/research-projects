Abstract
This monograph investigates constraints as generative mechanisms shaping subjectivity, percep-
tion, and meaning across ethological, psychological, narrative, artistic, developmental, systemic,
and computational domains. It synthesizes the contributions of Jane Goodall, Torey Hayden, Ur-
sula K. Le Guin, Betty Edwards, Jacqueline Goodnow, and Alicia Juarrero, complemented by Gre-
gory Bateson and Lev Vygotsky, and computational models including Monte Carlo Tree Search,
5D Ising Synchronization, and Markov blankets. Subjectivity is conceptualized as a recursive pro-
cess emerging from interactions with social, material, narrative, and algorithmic constraints. The
analysis includes cross-disciplinary syntheses and concludes with a practical framework for self-
authorship through a personal textbook and course, enabling individuals to reconﬁgure constraints
for emergent identity formation.

Foundations of Constraint
1.1
Constraint as Catalyst
Subjectivity emerges within a complex network of constraints-social, material, cognitive, narrative,
and computational-that channel interactions into coherent, emergent phenomena. This monograph
constructs a hexagonal framework, integrating the ethological observations of Jane Goodall, the ther-
apeutic interventions of Torey Hayden, the narrative structures of Ursula K. Le Guin, the perceptual
methods of Betty Edwards, the developmental analyses of Jacqueline Goodnow, and the systemic
theories of Alicia Juarrero. These perspectives are enriched by Gregory Bateson's recursive learning
theory (Bateson, 1972) and Lev Vygotsky's sociocultural mediation (Vygotsky, 1978), alongside com-
putational models such as Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), 5D Ising Synchronization, and Markov
blankets. Constraints are framed as causal structures enabling subjectivity, perception, and meaning
across disciplines. The subject, shaped by iterative encounters with various others, is a relational
system navigating a multidimensional ﬁeld of constraints, culminating in a proposed framework for
self-authorship.
Constraints challenge traditional notions of freedom as unbounded agency. Bateson's ecological pat-
terns position constraints as generative mechanisms that produce coherent outcomes through recursive
feedback (Bateson, 1972). Vygotsky's sociocultural framework emphasizes constraints-language, cul-
tural tools-as mediators of cognitive and identity development (Vygotsky, 1978). This monograph

1.2
Subjectivity and Systems
Subjectivity, as a dynamic system, is characterized by recursive feedback loops, where constraints
shape and are shaped by interactions with social, material, and symbolic others. Bateson's theory of
learning as a meta-pattern suggests that identity emerges through iterative encounters with difference-
the other, whether social, environmental, or cognitive (Bateson, 1972). Vygotsky's emphasis on me-
diated development positions cultural tools as constraints that scaffold internal processes (Vygotsky,
1978). These frameworks conceptualize the subject as a relational network, where constraints enable
coherence without ﬁxed essence. The hexagonal framework bridges these perspectives, analyzing how
constraints produce emergent subjectivity across disciplines.
1.3
Monte Carlo, Myth, and Maps: Computational Metaphors
Computational models provide structural analogs for constraint-driven systems. Monte Carlo Tree
Search (MCTS) is a heuristic algorithm used in decision-making, iteratively sampling constrained
paths to optimize outcomes (Silver et al., 2016). For example, in a game like chess, MCTS explores
possible moves within the rules, balancing exploration (testing new paths) and exploitation (reﬁning
known strategies). This mirrors narrative and developmental processes, where choices constrain future
possibilities. The 5D Ising model, from statistical mechanics, models emergent order through con-
strained particle interactions in a high-dimensional lattice (Onsager, 1944). In ﬁve dimensions, the
model stabilizes collective behavior, analogous to social or cognitive systems achieving coherence.
Markov blankets, from Bayesian inference, deﬁne boundaries between a system and its environment,
mediating interactions (Friston, 2010). These models map onto the thinkers' frameworks, providing a
computational lens for subjectivity.

Six Lenses on Constraint
2.1
Goodall: Ethological Perception
2.1.1
Biography and Context
Jane Goodall, a pioneering ethologist, reshaped primatology through her empathetic observations of
chimpanzees in Gombe, Tanzania, beginning in 1960 (Goodall, 1971). Rejecting behaviorist paradigms
that reduced behavior to stimuli-response mechanisms, Goodall documented emotional and social
complexity, including tool use and cultural transmission (Goodall, 1990). Her work challenged an-
thropocentric assumptions, inﬂuencing anthropology, psychology, and environmental ethics (Goodall,
1999). Goodall's narrative-based method, grounded in patience and empathy, offers a model for under-
standing relational constraints in shaping subjectivity.
2.1.2
Theoretical Framework
Goodall's ethology positions social constraints-grooming rituals, dominance hierarchies-as mecha-
nisms for emergent behaviors, such as tool use and cultural practices (Goodall, 1971). Her observa-
tions align with Bateson's ecological patterns, where relationality shapes identity through recursive
feedback loops (Bateson, 1972). Chimpanzee sociality, constrained by group dynamics, produces cul-
tural phenomena that mirror human innovation. Subjectivity, in this framework, is a dynamic process
constituted through reciprocal exchanges with the social other, mediated by empathetic observation.
Goodall's rejection of behaviorism underscores the role of context in enabling agency, positioning the
subject as inherently relational. This perspective resonates with Vygotsky's sociocultural mediation,
where social interactions scaffold cognitive development (Vygotsky, 1978).
2.1.3
Case Study
Goodall's observation of David Greybeard, a chimpanzee using a stick as a spear to hunt, illustrates
cultural emergence within constrained social systems (Goodall, 1971). David's behavior, shared within
the group, demonstrates how social constraints enable innovation, paralleling Vygotsky's mediated
learning through cultural tools (Vygotsky, 1978). The empathetic gaze, as a constraint, facilitates the
observer's integration into the system, reshaping their subjectivity. This case highlights the role of
relational constraints in producing emergent cultural and individual identities.

Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Goodall's relational dynamics connect to Hayden's therapeutic witnessing, where the other catalyzes
identity reconstruction, and Juarrero's systemic constraints, where social structures produce emergent
coherence. Her empathetic observation aligns with Le Guin's narrative empathy, emphasizing relation-
ality across ethological and narrative domains. Edwards' perceptual methods also resonate, as both
prioritize direct engagement over preconceived categories.
2.2
Hayden: Trauma and Narrative
2.2.1
Biography and Context
Torey Hayden, a special education teacher and psychologist, worked with nonverbal, traumatized chil-
dren in the 1970s and 1980s, developing narrative-based therapeutic methods (Hayden, 1980). Her
approach, rooted in relational trust rather than clinical detachment, transformed special education by
prioritizing the child's voice (Hayden, 2006). Hayden's case studies, drawn from classroom experi-
ences, highlight narrative as a tool for reconstructing identity in the face of trauma (Hayden, 1991).
Her work bridges psychology, education, and narrative theory, offering insights into constraint-driven
subjectivity.
2.2.2
Theoretical Framework
Hayden's work frames trauma as a constraint on symbolic expression, disrupting narrative coherence
and isolating the subject from the symbolic order (Hayden, 1980). Drawing on Vygotsky's socio-
cultural theory, she positions the therapeutic relationship as a mediating scaffold, enabling narrative
reconstruction (Vygotsky, 1978). The silenced subject, marked by unarticulated experience, navigates
a ﬁeld where meaning is obstructed. Through iterative dialogue, Hayden facilitates re-entry into the
symbolic framework, positioning subjectivity as a recursive process shaped by relational constraints.
This aligns with Bateson's recursive learning, where feedback loops generate new patterns of mean-
ing (Bateson, 1972). Hayden's approach underscores the productive tension of constraints, where the
relational other-therapist, witness-catalyzes identity reformation.
4

2.2.3
Case Study
In One Child, Hayden describes Sheila, a six-year-old mute child with a history of abuse, who begins
to communicate through structured interactions (Hayden, 1980). Sheila's gradual articulation of her
story illustrates how relational constraints-trust, patience, dialogue-enable narrative emergence. This
case mirrors Goodall's empathetic observation, where the other facilitates behavioral expression, and
Le Guin's narrative structures, where storytelling reconﬁgures identity.
2.2.4
Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Hayden's narrative reconstruction parallels Le Guin's mythic structures, both emphasizing storytelling
as a constrained process for identity formation. Her focus on relationality connects to Goodall's so-
cial dynamics and Juarrero's systemic emergence, highlighting the other as a catalyst for subjectivity.
Edwards' perceptual methods also resonate, as both address symbolic barriers to expression.
2.3
Le Guin: Myth, Fiction, and Nonlinear Subjectivity
2.3.1
Biography and Context
Ursula K. Le Guin, a novelist and essayist active from the 1960s to the 2010s, reshaped speculative
ﬁction through feminist and Taoist perspectives, exploring alternative social and narrative structures
(Le Guin, 1989). Her work, including The Dispossessed and Always Coming Home, inﬂuenced literary
theory, anthropology, and philosophy (Le Guin, 1974; Le Guin, 1985). Her 'Carrier Bag Theory'
challenges linear, heroic narratives, offering a model for pluralistic subjectivity (Le Guin, 1989). Le
Guin's interdisciplinary impact positions her as a key thinker in constraint-driven narrative systems.
2.3.2
Theoretical Framework
Le Guin's 'Carrier Bag Theory' reconceptualizes narrative as a non-linear container for diverse expe-
riences, rejecting phallocentric, linear arcs (Le Guin, 1989). In The Dispossessed, constrained narra-
tive worlds-utopian and capitalist-enable social experimentation, shaping subjectivity through struc-
tured limits (Le Guin, 1974). Her approach aligns with Bateson's recursive patterns, where narrative
feedback loops generate meaning (Bateson, 1972). Subjectivity is a dynamic navigation of narrative
constraints, producing plural identities. This resonates with Deleuze's rhizomatic structures, where
narratives branch non-hierarchically, fostering multiplicity (Deleuze & Guattari, 1987). Le Guin's

framework positions narrative as a technology for reconﬁguring identity within constrained systems.
2.3.3
Case Study
In The Dispossessed, the protagonist Shevek navigates dual societies, each with distinct social and
narrative constraints, illustrating how these structures shape identity (Le Guin, 1974). The utopian
society's communal constraints contrast with the capitalist society's competitive limits, producing dis-
tinct subjectivities. This case parallels Hayden's therapeutic narratives, where constrained storytelling
enables identity reconstruction, and Goodnow's spatial constraints, where limits shape creative output.
2.3.4
Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Le Guin's narrative constraints connect to Hayden's therapeutic storytelling and Goodnow's spatial
constraints, all emphasizing recursive structures. Her pluralistic epistemology aligns with Edwards'
perceptual defamiliarization, challenging symbolic dominance. Juarrero's systemic constraints also
resonate, as both explore freedom within limits.
2.4
Edwards: Perception and Visual Constraint
2.4.1
Biography and Context
Betty Edwards, an art educator, developed perceptual training methods in the 1970s, impacting art edu-
cation and cognitive psychology (Edwards, 1979). Her book Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain in-
troduced techniques to bypass symbolic cognition, inﬂuencing pedagogy, neuroscience, and aesthetics
(Edwards, 1986). Edwards' work bridges psychology and art, offering insights into constraint-driven
perception.
2.4.2
Theoretical Framework
Edwards positions symbolic thinking as a cognitive constraint obstructing direct perception (Edwards,
1979). Her methods-contour drawing, negative space analysis, upside-down drawing-use constraints
to reconﬁgure perception, enabling subjects to access unmediated visual experience (Edwards, 1986).
This aligns with Merleau-Ponty's embodied phenomenology, where perception is an active, bodily
process (Merleau-Ponty, 1945). Subjectivity is a process of navigating cognitive limits, producing
new perceptual capacities through structured practice (Edwards, 2004). Vygotsky's mediated learning
6

complements this, as drawing tools reshape cognition (Vygotsky, 1978).
2.4.3
Case Study
Edwards' upside-down drawing task, where learners copy an inverted image, disrupts symbolic inter-
ference, enabling accurate observation (Edwards, 1979). For example, copying an inverted Picasso
drawing forces attention to lines and shapes, bypassing preconceived notions of 'face' or 'body'. This
case illustrates how constrained practice transforms perception, paralleling Goodnow's spatial con-
straints and Le Guin's narrative defamiliarization.
2.4.4
Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Edwards' perceptual methods align with Goodnow's spatial constraints and Le Guin's narrative defamil-
iarization, all challenging symbolic structures. Her focus on embodied practice connects to Goodall's
empathetic observation, emphasizing direct engagement with the other. Hayden's therapeutic methods
also resonate, as both address barriers to expression.

Goodnow: Developmental Systems and Recursive Drawing
2.5.1
Biography and Context
Jacqueline Goodnow, a developmental psychologist, studied children's drawings in the 20th century,
contributing to cognitive and cross-cultural psychology (Goodnow, 1977). Her work on spatial and cul-
tural constraints shaped developmental theory, emphasizing context in cognitive processes (Goodnow,
1995). Goodnow's interdisciplinary approach bridges psychology, anthropology, and education.
2.5.2
Theoretical Framework
Goodnow frames drawing as a recursive process, where each mark constrains future choices within
a material ﬁeld (Goodnow, 1977). The page acts as a structuring other, shaping cognitive strategies,
aligning with Vygotsky's mediated development (Vygotsky, 1978). Subjectivity emerges through it-
erative interactions with spatial and social constraints, producing dynamic patterns of thought and
action (Goodnow, 1977b). This resonates with Bateson's recursive learning, where constraints gener-
ate emergent complexity (Bateson, 1972). Goodnow's framework positions the subject as a navigator
of constrained ﬁelds, where limits enable creative and cognitive development.

2.5.3
Case Study
Goodnow's study of children avoiding overlap in drawings reveals how spatial constraints shape cog-
nitive strategies (Goodnow, 1977). For example, a child drawing a house may hesitate to add a tree if
it overlaps, reﬂecting implicit spatial rules. This case parallels Juarrero's systemic constraints, where
limits enable coherent action, and Edwards' perceptual methods, where constraints reconﬁgure cogni-
tion.
2.5.4
Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Goodnow's spatial constraints connect to Juarrero's systemic dynamics and Edwards' perceptual meth-
ods, emphasizing recursive structures. Her developmental focus aligns with Hayden's narrative re-
construction, as both explore constraints in identity formation. Le Guin's narrative constraints also
resonate, as both involve navigating structured ﬁelds.

Juarrero: Complex Systems and Enabling Constraints
2.6.1
Biography and Context
Alicia Juarrero, a philosopher of complex systems, developed theories of intentionality and identity in
the late 20th century, drawing on dynamical systems and second-order cybernetics (Juarrero, 1999).
Her work bridges philosophy, cognitive science, and systems theory, offering insights into constraint-
driven agency (Juarrero, 2008). Juarrero's interdisciplinary approach inﬂuences cognitive and organi-
zational studies.
2.6.2
Theoretical Framework
Juarrero frames constraints as causal mechanisms enabling coherent identity in complex systems (Juar-
rero, 1999). Subjectivity is a dynamic process, emerging through recursive interactions with contextual
constraints, aligning with Bateson's cybernetic patterns (Bateson, 1972). Agency arises from navigat-
ing enabling constraints, producing coherence without ﬁxed essence (Juarrero, 2002). This perspec-
tive positions the subject as a relational system, where constraints-social, material, cognitive-catalyze
emergent complexity. Juarrero's framework draws on second-order cybernetics, emphasizing feedback
loops in system dynamics.

2.6.3
Case Study
Juarrero's analysis of constrained decision-making in institutions-e.g., role-based choices in organizations-
illustrates emergent agency (Juarrero, 1999). For example, a manager's decisions are shaped by insti-
tutional constraints, yet enable innovative leadership. This case parallels Goodall's social constraints,
where group dynamics enable cultural emergence, and Goodnow's spatial constraints, where limits
shape creative output.
2.6.4
Cross-Disciplinary Connections
Juarrero's systemic constraints align with Goodnow's spatial dynamics and Goodall's social structures,
emphasizing emergent coherence. Her focus on agency connects to Le Guin's narrative freedom and
Hayden's therapeutic reconstruction, highlighting constraints as catalysts for identity.

Computational Models
4.1
Monte Carlo Tree Search
Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a heuristic algorithm used in decision-making, iteratively sam-
pling constrained paths to optimize outcomes (Silver et al., 2016). In a game like Go, MCTS explores
possible moves within the rules, balancing exploration (testing new paths) and exploitation (reﬁning
known strategies). This mirrors Le Guin's narrative branching, where plot choices constrain future
possibilities, and Goodnow's drawing, where each mark limits subsequent actions. Hayden's thera-
peutic iterations resemble MCTS's feedback loops, as relational constraints reﬁne narrative coherence.
MCTS provides a computational analog for recursive, constraint-driven processes in subjectivity.
4.2
5D Ising Synchronization
The 5D Ising model, from statistical mechanics, models emergent order through constrained parti-
cle interactions in a high-dimensional lattice (Onsager, 1944). Particles align based on local rules,
producing collective behavior. In ﬁve dimensions, the model stabilizes order, reducing ﬂuctuations,
analogous to social or cognitive systems achieving coherence. This aligns with Juarrero's systemic co-
herence, where constraints enable emergent agency (Juarrero, 1999), and Goodall's social emergence,
where group dynamics produce cultural patterns (Goodall, 1971). Edwards' perceptual stabilization
through constrainedStopping practice also resonates (Edwards, 1979).

4.3
Markov Blankets
Markov blankets, from Bayesian inference, deﬁne boundaries between a system and its environment,
mediating interactions (Friston, 2010). For example, in cognitive systems, a Markov blanket separates
internal states (beliefs) from external states (sensory data), shaping perception through constrained
inference. This maps onto Hayden's therapeutic boundaries, where relational constraints enable nar-
rative reconstruction, and Juarrero's systemic constraints, where boundaries produce coherent identity.
Markov blankets provide a computational model for subjectivity as a bounded, adaptive system.

Conclusion: The Relational Hexagon and Self-Authorship
The hexagonal framework-integrating Goodall's ethological observations, Hayden's therapeutic in-
terventions, Le Guin's narrative structures, Edwards' perceptual methods, Goodnow's developmental
analyses, Juarrero's systemic theories, Bateson's recursive patterns, Vygotsky's sociocultural media-
tion, and computational models (MCTS, 5D Ising, Markov blankets)-positions constraints as genera-
tive mechanisms for subjectivity. Social, narrative, cognitive, material, and systemic constraints shape
the subject through iterative interactions with relational, material, and algorithmic others, producing
dynamic coherence.
To synthesize and extend these insights, we propose a personal textbook, Rewriting the Page: A Self-
Made Curriculum in Perception and Pattern, and a six-week course, Drawing the Mind: Six Experi-
ments in Recursive Perception. The textbook is structured into three parts: Foundations (perception,
hexagonal framework), Six Lenses (Goodall, Hayden, Le Guin, Edwards, Goodnow, Juarrero), and
Applications (daily practices, workshops, recursive identity). Exercises include: silent observation
journaling (Goodall), inner dialogue writing (Hayden), object-based narrative rewriting (Le Guin),
blind contour drawing (Edwards), spatial drawing interventions (Goodnow), and habit mapping with
constraint modiﬁcation (Juarrero). The course implements these through weekly practices, fostering
recursive identity formation through structured exercises. This framework invites individuals to recon-
ﬁgure their constraints, producing emergent subjectivity within a multidimensional ﬁeld.
