This book is not an addition to the existing catalog of physical theories. It does not propose a
new particle, a hidden dimension, or a more elaborate mechanism layered atop familiar equations.
Instead, it is an audit.
For much of modern intellectual history, explanation has advanced by externalization. When
a model fails to close, we introduce a new entity.
When an account grows unstable, we add
parameters. When meaning becomes diﬃcult to justify, we relocate it to subjectivity. The result
is a body of knowledge whose equations balance, but whose explanations grow steadily heavier.
The Relativistic Scalar-Vector Plenum (RSVP) framework begins from a simpler observation:
structure is never free. Wherever order persists, a cost has been paid. Wherever that cost is not
explicitly tracked, it reappears elsewhere as mystery, force, or exception.
Across physics, cognition, computation, and culture, the same pattern recurs. Expansion is
invoked to explain redshift.
Curvature is invoked to explain weight.
Dark sectors are invoked
to save conservation laws. Intelligence is invoked without a mechanism for stopping. Ethics is
invoked without a physical constraint. In each case, a real expenditure—of entropy, resolution, or
control—has been displaced rather than eliminated.
This book reintroduces the ledger.
RSVP treats space as a ﬁxed plenum, not a fabric that stretches. What evolves is not geometry
itself, but the operational resolution with which distinctions can be maintained. Structure appears
as localized sequestration of negentropy. Motion and force appear as compensatory ﬂows required
to preserve global balance. Observation appears as a thermodynamic transaction. Meaning appears
as invariance under transformation. Termination appears as ﬁxed-point closure.
These are not metaphors. They are re-indexings.
The essays that follow are arranged as a single causal loop. Early chapters remove expensive
assumptions in cosmology and gravity. Middle chapters place the observer inside the system and
account for time, memory, and agency.
Later chapters identify the biological machinery that
performs these transactions, formalize failure modes when control is lost, and derive practical
criteria for stopping runaway processes in machines, institutions, and discourse.
The order matters. Each identiﬁcation constrains the next. By the time the reader reaches the
ﬁnal essays, the earlier distinctions can no longer be reinstated without reopening debts that have
already been settled.

• history-dependent,
• internally enforced.
Systems that always act when prompted cannot possess agency.

APPENDIX F. TECHNICAL APPENDIX
F.9
Externalized Constraint Does Not Count
A common proposal is to enforce constraints externally:
• via human oversight,
• via infrastructure rules,
• via governance layers.
This does not confer worldhood.
If irreversible constraint is enforced outside the system, the system remains world-less.
External enforcement does not reduce the systems internal possibility space. Internal reversibil-
ity is preserved. By the No-World Theorem, worldhood does not arise.
F.10
Minimal Architectural Implications
Any AGI satisfying the criteria of this book must accept:
• ﬁnite internal capacity,
• irreversible internal state transitions,
• loss of future options as a result of action,
• inability to reset without loss,
• termination via ﬁxed-point detection.
These properties are not bugs. They are the cost of meaning.
F.11
Why This Is an Alignment Result
A world-bearing system is automatically aligned in a non-trivial sense: it can be harmed by its own
actions.
Alignment emerges not from constraint optimization, but from exposure to irreversible conse-
quence.
A system that can suﬀer loss of its own future is a system for which behavior matters.
F.12
Summary for AGI Reviewers
• Behavioral equivalence is insuﬃcient for intelligence.
• Reversibility trivializes history.
• Scaling does not introduce stakes.
• Worldhood requires irreversible internal constraint.
