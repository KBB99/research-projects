Intrinsic and extrinsic 
reference frames refer 
to different ways of 
describing the position or 
movement of an object in 
space, and they are often 
used in the context of 
neuroscience, robotics, and 
physics.
Intrinsic Reference Frame:
This is a coordinate system 
that is based on the object 

itself. For instance, in 
motor control, an intrinsic 
reference frame would 
describe the angles of 
joints, muscle contractions, 
or other properties 
inherent to the body. When
you say "bend your elbow 
to a 90-degree angle," 
you're using an intrinsic 
frame of reference. The 
description is based on the 
object's own axes or 

internal properties, and it 
doesn't require any 
information about the 
object's position relative to
external landmarks.
Extrinsic Reference 
Frame: This describes an 
object's position or 
movement in relation to an 
external coordinate system
or some external point of 
reference. For example, 

GPS coordinates use an 
extrinsic reference frame;
they tell you where you 
are in relation to the 
Earth as a whole. In the 
context of motor control, 
this could involve 
describing a hand's position 
in terms of Cartesian 
coordinates (X, Y, Z) within 
a room.

In the context of the 
article, the authors 
discuss how neural activity 
in the PPC (Posterior 
Parietal Cortex) has been 
traditionally thought to 
encode motor intentions 
primarily in an intrinsic 
frame of reference (joint

data suggest that an 

extrinsic frame of 
reference (like Cartesian 
coordinates) is also encoded
by neurons, suggesting that
a more comprehensive 
model of motor control 
should include both.
In summary, intrinsic 
frames are object-
centered, focusing on 
properties internal to the 
object itself, while 

extrinsic frames are 
world-centered, focusing 
on the object's position or 
movement within some 
larger context.
