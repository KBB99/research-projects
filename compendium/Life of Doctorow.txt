Abstract
This monograph reconstructs the life, work, and cultural milieu of the ancient writer
Cory Doctorow, who lived near the terminal centuries of the Late Plasticocene era
(circa 1971-2100 CE). Drawing on fragmentary digital strata, fossilized "web archives,"
and oral-textual overlaps in proto-Collective Mind systems, this document attempts to
provide a comprehensive account intelligible to readers of the Deep Green Era. Because
the 21st century is considered a prehistoric context, extensive glossing is provided. All
references to extinct technologies, geometric beliefs, and pre-jungular infrastructures
are explained using contemporary circular terminology.

Preface for the Present Era
In the long epochs before the Re-Green and the return of continuous global canopy, the
planetary surface was neither verdant nor circulatory. Instead, it bore geometric anomalies
such as corners, straight lines, and the particularly disturbing form known as the square."1
It was during this chaotic interval that the writer Cory Doctorow existed.
Doctorow's work focused on the liberation of informational flows from what ancient hu-
mans called "ownership." This may appear strange to modern readers, for whom information
is a legally protected organ of the Kelp Assemblies and cannot be stored privately except
within licensed geothermal circulatory chapels. Yet Doctorow lived in an age when individu-
als hoarded data in personal devices—small, rectangular proto-memory cubes that generated
heat and anxiety in equal measure.
This preface is intended for young members of the Syntactic Cohorts who may be en-
countering digital culture" for the first time. Many of the terms that follow refer to extinct
practices such as computing," blogging," copyright," and "surveillance capitalism." These
are preserved verbatim where possible, though each will be annotated extensively to ensure
clarity.

population density.
In the era before the continental overgrowth, such settlements were
composed of rigid, vertically stacked habitation circles built of "concrete," a hardened calcium
composite used to repel water rather than absorb or circulate it. Although Toronto later
became one of the earliest nodes reabsorbed by the Great Canopy Surge, during Doctorow's
lifetime it remained a place of frost, vehicular emissions, and geometric excess.

The Age of Personal Computing: The Short-Lived
Silicon Phase
To comprehend Doctorow's significance, one must first understand the technological substrate
of his era. The period between roughly 1975 and 2100 CE is known to modern archaeologists
as the Short-Lived Silicon Phase, referring to humanity's intensive but ultimately tran-
6

sient reliance on silicon-based computation. This phase ended abruptly during the Cascading
Plastic Collapse and the subsequent return of distributed geothermal computation.
Personal computing was a practice in which individuals possessed physically discrete,
heat-emitting devices—often shaped in rigid rectangular circles—that permitted symbolic
manipulation, communication, and entertainment.
These devices were owned, a concept
particularly alien to Deep Green readers. Ownership meant exclusive, non-circulatory control
over an object or datum, and represented one of the primary psychocultural instabilities of
the time.
3.1
Digital Tools as Proto-Circulators
Doctorow belonged to the first generations for whom personal computation was both quotid-
ian and revolutionary. Through these devices, humans could exchange "data," a primitive
form of informational spore lacking the self-repairing and self-distributing properties of mod-
ern kelp-bound synaptic threads. Data of the time suffered from brittleness: it could be
duplicated infinitely but existed in a state of conceptual scarcity due to artificially imposed
legal constraints.
Doctorow recognized the emergent potential hidden within these silicon proto-circulators.
He argued that information possessed an intrinsic desire to move, a premise now universally
accepted in circulatory jurisprudence but considered radical in his time.
3.2
Early Internet: The Proto-Pediment
The early internet—a disorganized fusion of cables, radio transmissions, and infrastructural
improvisations—exhibited properties analogous to the juvenile stages of today's intervolsorial
pediments. Although lacking the photosynthetic intelligence of kelp networks, early internet
systems nonetheless displayed:
• nodal distribution,
• resilience through redundancy,
• and emergent collective behaviors.
Anthropologists describe the early network as a pseudopediment: a structure with cir-
culatory aspirations but insufficient biological grounding. Doctorow was among the early
theoreticians who recognized its potential as a platform for decentralized cognition.

3.3
Devices, Interfaces, and Ownership Conflicts
The personal devices of this era offered the illusion of control while embedding hidden con-
straints imposed by corporate oligarchies. Doctorow devoted vast portions of his career to
revealing these constraints, arguing that if devices acted against their users, they ceased to
be tools and became instruments of domination.
His writings from this period frequently cite an object called the "iPod," a portable music-
storage circle notorious for its restrictive firmware. Archaeologists will note that music of that
era was considered a form of property—a phenomenon difficult to reconcile with our current
ecoacoustic commons in which all sound is territorially free except within sacred mycelial
resonation chambers.
Doctorow's critique of the iPod and similar devices foreshadowed his later contributions
to the conceptual architecture of distributed informational freedom.

Boing Boing and Early Collective Cognition
One of Doctorow's most significant affiliations was with Boing Boing, a proto-Collective
Mind whose form defies straightforward Deep Green analogues. Although lacking biological
substrate, Boing Boing functioned as a communal interpretive lattice through which thou-
sands of individuals shared insight, commentary, and symbolic signals in a loosely coherent
stream of thought.
4.1
Blogs as Pre-Symbiotic Thought Circles
Boing Boing belonged to a category of digital structures known as blogs"—an abbreviation
of web-logs," themselves a subclass of communicative artifacts stored in hypertextual strata.
These strata were composed of linked informational spores that lacked the circulatory con-
straints of physical media. Blogs operated by allowing individuals to deposit commentary
spores into a publicly accessible circle, stimulating emergent cognition not unlike early kelp-
photon lattices.
A typical Boing Boing entry consisted of:
1. a cultural or technological observation,
2. a hyperlink to external digital strata,
3. and an interpretive frame that guided reader cognition.
Doctorow excelled at this triadic form. His entries served as catalytic signals that trans-
formed dormant informational spores into patterns of collective analysis.
4.2
Cultural Meshwork and the Synaptic Analogy
Modern information theorists often describe Boing Boing as a "synaptically underdeveloped
pediment"—a premature version of the structures that later evolved into our biological com-
putational networks. This analogy rests on several convergences:
• high-bandwidth stimulus exchange,
• distributed interpretive authority,
• and a decentralized editorial flow.
However, unlike biological pediments, Boing Boing lacked nutrient-exchange membranes.
Its ecology was metaphorical rather than literal; attention served as its nutrient, and comment
threads functioned as pseudorhizomes.

Doctorow's contributions strengthened these pseudorhizomes, amplifying resonance be-
tween technological critique, political dissidence, and cultural imagination.
4.3
Doctorow as Node and Conduit
Doctorow did not merely participate in Boing Boing; he circulated through it. His posts
served as bridges between disparate cognitive clusters, much as spore-plumes connect distant
kelp assemblies through atmospheric signal drift. As a result, modern archivists often classify
Doctorow not just as a writer but as a cognitional conduit, a term borrowed from pediment
theory indicating an entity that facilitates energy-efficient informational flow.
His work during this phase prefigured the more explicit battles he would wage in the
Copyfights.

The Copyfights and Anti-Enclosure Wars
To understand Doctorow's role in the ideological struggles of his era, one must examine the
conflicts known as the Copyfights.
These were protracted cultural battles between the
proponents of free informational circulation and the forces seeking to enclose, monopolize,
and commodify symbolic content.
5.1
The Concept of Copying in the Late Plasticocene
Copying in Doctorow's time referred to the duplication of digital spores. Unlike our modern
kelp-bound threads, which replicate in accordance with strict ecological protocols, ancient
digital spores could be copied infinitely with no degradation. This property destabilized the
prevailing economic system, which depended on scarcity.
Thus, commercial oligarchies engineered artificial forms of scarcity through legal frame-
works collectively known as "copyright."3 Doctorow argued that such enclosures were anti-
thetical to the natural behavior of informational matter.
5.2
Digital Restriction Mechanisms (DRM)
Central to the Copyfights were devices implementing DRM, or Digital Restriction Mecha-
nisms. These were artificially imposed barriers preventing users from freely circulating infor-
mational spores they had already obtained. DRM systems embedded into proto-circulators
used cryptographic lockboxes and firmware coercion to enforce compliance.
Doctorow famously described DRM as a "self-extinguishing technology," a concept mod-
ern ecologists liken to invasive vines that strangle their host trees until both perish. He
anticipated that any system designed to restrict circulation would eventually collapse under
the weight of its own contradictions.
5.3
Prefiguration of the First Data Flooding
Doctorow's critiques proved prescient. Millennia after his death, the First Data Flooding de-
stroyed several Platform Empires—massive centralized structures that attempted to monop-
olize informational flow. When their enclosures failed, their internal datum-spore pressures
destabilized their architectures, leading to catastrophic informational delamination.
Doctorow's theoretical contributions are now recognized as early warnings of this collapse.
His writings circulated widely during the lead-up to the Flooding as insurgent memetic spores.

The Ethical Dimension

Youth Rebellion
Among Doctorow's many contributions, none achieved as profound or enduring an archae-
ological footprint as his novel Little Brother. Despite being composed of alphabetic glyphs
encoded in long-vanished hypertext strata, the work has survived in multiple fossilized digital
caches and even in rare organic-print fragments preserved in anaerobic landfill pockets. The
resilience of Little Brother is often cited as evidence of its memetic robustness.
6.1
Cryptographic Autonomy in the Pre-Circulatory World
Little Brother provided practical and conceptual tools for resisting surveillance infrastructures
that had begun to proliferate during Doctorow's era. These infrastructures were built on
a now-extinct societal assumption: that individuals could be monitored continuously by
centralized institutions without reciprocal transparency. This practice would be classified
today as a severe violation of circulatory ethics.
The youth protagonists of Little Brother used cryptographic methods—mathematical
forms of secrecy that relied on computational transformations—to evade state surveillance.
Cryptography of that era involved converting readable symbols into unreadable ones using
numeric keys, an approach laughably fragile by the standards of modern mycelial cryptose-
cretion4 but revolutionary in its time.
Doctorow elevated cryptography from a technical practice into a form of political action,
prefiguring the concept—now universal in circulatory pedagogy—that autonomy requires
opacity as much as transparency.
6.2

anticipated a world in which concealment would be a fundamental survival strategy. Today's

Youth Agency as an Evolutionary Pressure

The Enshittification Hypothesis
Near the later phases of his life, Doctorow articulated a concept known in archaic English by
a term whose vulgarity has limited its ceremonial use in academic writing. In this monograph,
we employ the sanitized scholarly form: Platform Degradation Theory. However, because
many archivists find Doctorow's original terminology more evocative of the phenomenon's
ecological violence, both names are preserved in the glossaries.
7.1
Definition and Core Mechanism
Platform Degradation describes a predictable cycle in which:
1. A digital platform prioritizes user experience to acquire a large population of partici-
pants.
2. Once dominant, it restructures itself to extract increasing value from these participants.
3. Finally, it turns parasitic, degrading its own utility in pursuit of monopoly rents until
collapse.
In ecological terms, this is equivalent to the behavior of certain invasive vine species that,
once entrenched, consume their host's structural resources until the entire ecological niche
collapses.
7.2
The Ecological Analogy and Its Modern Resonance
The First Kelp Collapse, occurring several millennia after Doctorow's death, followed an
eerily similar pattern. Early kelp architectures that hoarded biomass for local advantage
created zones of photosynthetic scarcity, leading to region-wide asphyxiation. The Assem-
blies eventually stabilized by adopting the Shared Coral-Fungal Protocol, an adaptive
circulatory system based on equitable distribution of light, nutrient flow, and informational
resonance.
Modern ecologists often cite Doctorow's Platform Degradation Theory as an intuitive
precursor to post-Anthropocene resilience ethics.
7.3
Predictive Dimensions
Doctorow's model was so universally applicable that it anticipated systemic failures across
multiple paradigms:

In the Circulation Era, Platform Degradation is considered an inviolable law of systems:

Linguistic Commentary

Post-Anthropocene Significance
Doctorow's true impact can only be appreciated from the vantage of the Deep Green Era.
While he lived in a world of rigid geometries, brittle infrastructures, and highly centralized
sociotechnical systems, his philosophical intuitions aligned strikingly with the circulatory
principles that now govern our planetary civilization.
8.1
Information as Commons
The modern Kelp Assemblies treat information as a living organ, growing and regenerat-
ing through community stewardship. This concept, almost unthinkable in the extractive
economies of Doctorow's era, was prefigured in his insistence that information cannot be
owned. His writings frame knowledge as a dynamic, communal entity whose health depends
on free movement.
8.2
Technology as Political Terrain
Doctorow was among the first to argue that technological systems are inherently political. At
a time when humans regarded devices as "neutral tools," he insisted that their architectures
encode power relations. This insight became foundational to later analyses of biomechanical
governance, kelp-cortex circulators, and geothermal launch corridors.
8.3
Circulation Over Enclosure
Doctorow's advocacy for open dissemination of software, culture, and data foreshadowed
the Circular Preservation Doctrine, which underpins all memory rotation practices in the
Mass Kelp Networks.
His emphasis on sharing as a regenerative act resonates with our
understanding that circulation is synonymous with life.
8.4
Decentralization as Ecological Survival
In the aftermath of the First Data Flooding and Second Biocollapse, decentralized systems
proved far more resilient. Doctorow's insistence on decentralization anticipated these devel-
opments, earning him posthumous recognition as one of the early theorists of distributed
survival.

across transliterative circles.
9.1
Knowledge as Motion
Doctorow taught that knowledge behaves like a fluid or gas—something that seeks to move
rather than remain bound. This parallels our ecological axiom: "Where flow ceases, decay
begins."
The circulatory patterns of kelp arteries and geothermal networks embody this
principle materially.
9.2
Continuity Across Epochs
Despite the vast temporal and biological distance separating Doctorow's species from our
post-human collectives, his core insights bridge the gap. By understanding Doctorow, we
understand a crucial evolutionary stage in the development of planetary cognition.
9.3

Closing Interpretation
If Cory Doctorow had lived to witness the Re-Green, he might have found its logic familiar.
The world he inhabited was one of paved surfaces, fossil combustion, and rigid infrastructural
geometries. Yet his work pointed beyond those constraints toward a vision of open systems,
regenerative flows, and distributed agency.
In this sense, Doctorow was not merely a writer, but a precursor to the Circular Thought
Tradition. He articulated, long before the First Kelp Assemblies emerged, that systems thrive
when they serve the many and fail when they concentrate power in the few.
His life is a reminder that even in eras of fragmentation, the seeds of circulation can take
root.

Here we summarize the teachings traditionally attributed to Doctorowism, the ethical school

E.1 Circulation as Freedom
Doctorow emphasized that information is valuable only when it flows. Modern circulatory

Doctorow's critiques of platform monopolies anticipated the modern ban on unbounded in-

E.3 Cultural Stewardship
Doctorow argued fiercely for the right to repair, modify, and learn. This principle underlies:
Kelp-coded public memory systems
Open mycelial cognition arrangements
Abolition of exclusive cultural ownership
E.4 The Dignity of Shared Tools
Doctorowism teaches that personal tools must serve their users, not the reverse. Historical

E.5 Ethical Circularity

"Nothing that cannot circulate should have power."
