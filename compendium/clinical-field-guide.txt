Yarncrawlers
Fixed-Point Failure, Cognitive Drift, and Semantic Termination

Clinical Scope
This document provides a functional framework for identifying, understanding, and interven-
ing in forms of cognitive dysregulation characterized by excessive interpretation, persevera-
tion, or semantic drift. It is intended for clinical contexts including psychiatry, psychology,
cognitive neuroscience, and psychotherapy.
The framework does not replace diagnostic categories. It provides a cross-cutting dy-
namical lens that applies across disorders, developmental stages, and cognitive styles.
1
Conceptual Orientation
Human cognition operates by continuously transforming internal representations in response
to external and internal signals. These transformations are ordinarily constrained by invari-
ance: cognitive activity tends toward states where further elaboration produces no additional
functional distinction.
Clinical difficulty arises when this termination condition fails.
We model cognitive interpretation as a traversal process over a space of semantic states.
Within this model, two functional regimes are distinguished: the Yarncrawler and the
Noiselord.
These are not traits or diagnoses. They are operational modes.

The Yarncrawler Mode
In Yarncrawler mode, cognition exhibits lawful traversal.
Thought progresses, explores,
and elaborates, but remains anchored to invariant structures such as goals, perceptual con-
straints, or contextual boundaries.
Clinically, Yarncrawler mode corresponds to productive reasoning, imagination, planning,
and narrative construction. The individual can explore alternatives while retaining a sense
of completion.
Importantly, Yarncrawler cognition can stop. It reaches states where further thought
adds no value and naturally settles.
3
The Noiselord Mode
In Noiselord mode, cognitive traversal loses its termination criterion. Thought continues
despite having reached functional invariance.
This mode is characterized not by confusion, but by excessive coherence. Associations
proliferate. Interpretations remain internally consistent while becoming externally unteth-
ered.
Clinically, Noiselord mode may present as rumination, obsessive ideation, confabulation,
paranoid elaboration, manic ideation, or certain forms of anxiety-driven over-analysis. The
common feature is not content, but dynamics.
The patient does not report ignorance. They report certainty, urgency, or inability to
disengage.
4
Core Diagnostic Indicator
The central diagnostic indicator is invariance blindness.
If successive cognitive transformations do not materially alter the patient's functional
state, yet cognitive activity continues, the system is operating in Noiselord mode.
This can often be detected phenomenologically by asking what would count as "enough."
In Noiselord mode, the patient cannot specify a stopping condition.
5
Differential Diagnosis
The Noiselord framework does not correspond one-to-one with diagnostic categories.
2

Depression may involve reduced traversal rather than excessive traversal. Psychosis may
involve failures of anchoring rather than termination. ADHD may involve premature switch-
ing rather than drift.
The Noiselord pattern specifically concerns continued semantic elaboration in the absence
of new distinctions.
This allows it to cut across diagnoses and to explain symptom overlap that is otherwise
difficult to classify.
6
Termination as a Clinical Capacity
Termination is not suppression. It is not avoidance. It is the capacity to recognize semantic
closure.
Clinically, termination corresponds to the ability to say "this thought is complete," "this
explanation suffices," or "nothing further is required."
Loss of this capacity is not a moral failure or lack of insight. It is a dynamical imbalance.
7
Intervention Strategy
Intervention does not aim to eliminate interpretation. It aims to restore stopping.
The first intervention is invariance recognition. The clinician helps the patient identify
when further thought is no longer changing outcomes, emotions, or decisions.
The second intervention is cost awareness. The patient is guided to notice the cognitive,
emotional, or physiological cost of continued elaboration.
The third intervention is refusal. The patient practices intentionally not following inter-
pretive paths that do not alter lived outcomes.
Refusal is framed not as repression, but as commitment to closure.
8
Worldhood and Grounding
Restoration of termination produces what this framework calls worldhood: a sense of inhab-
iting a stable reality rather than an open semantic field.
Clinically, this often manifests as relief rather than insight. The patient reports reduced
urgency, reduced cognitive load, and increased capacity for action.
Worldhood is not achieved by correct interpretation, but by bounded interpretation.

Relationship to Existing Therapies
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy implicitly trains termination through cognitive restructuring
and thought-stopping.
Mindfulness-based therapies train invariance recognition by cultivating awareness of repet-
itive thought patterns.
Psychodynamic approaches may reduce Noiselord dynamics by resolving interpretive
drivers.
The present framework unifies these approaches by identifying termination as the shared
functional target.
10
Clinical Caution
Not all continued thinking is pathological. Yarncrawler exploration is essential for creativity,
problem-solving, and identity formation.
Intervention is indicated only when traversal persists without producing new functional
distinctions and imposes measurable cost.
The goal is not silence. The goal is sufficiency.
Clinical Summary
Cognitive health requires not only the ability to interpret, but the ability to stop interpreting.
A Noiselord is not a disordered mind. It is a mind without a braking system.
Clinical work restores that brake by reintroducing invariance, cost, and refusal.
Termination is not the end of thought. It is the condition that allows thought to matter.
