Abstract
The Spherepop calculus formalizes Jacques Ellul's The Technological Society as an alge-
braic system of spheres undergoing iterative merge (pop) operations under efficiency-driven
regimes. We define spheres with measurable interiors and boundaries, introduce pop as a par-
tial operator with explicit cost and entropy constraints, and prove convergence, monotonic
collapse, and anti-admissibility thresholds. Extensions include non-flattening pops (pop+)
for prefigurative evolution. A discrete simulation framework and Playcosm case studies (toy
car, Barbie, glider) demonstrate resistance design and empirical predictions. The calcu-
lus provides rigorous tools for analyzing technological closure, constructing anti-admissible
domains, and engineering prefigurative systems.

Introduction
Jacques Ellul's The Technological Society (1964) describes Technique as a totalizing, autonomous
system that absorbs all domains through efficiency, standardization, and semantic flattening.
The Playcosm manuscript (Anonymous, 2025) conceptualizes play—Barbie dolls, toy cars, Age
of Empires—as unified simulations within a single-shard ecosystem stratified by privilege gates.
This paper presents the Spherepop calculus: a formal merge algebra that reconstructs Ellul's
dynamics and enables resistance design. Our goals are threefold:
1. Analysis: Model Technique as iterative pop operations converging to a low-entropy clo-
sure T .
2. Resistance: Prove conditions for anti-admissible spheres that remain outside T .
3. Prefiguration: Construct non-flattening merges (pop+) that bootstrap future ontologies,
aligning with Playcosm's prefigurative affordances.

Core Definitions and Primitives
Definition 3.1 (Sphere). A sphere is a triple S = (I, B, Σ), where:
• I = (ΩI, FI, µI) is a probability space modeling interior states (semantic, tacit, embodied
content) with entropy HI(S) = H(µI).
• B is a finite or countable discrete interface space (tokens, protocols) with distribution µB,
and boundary entropy HB(S) = H(µB).
• Σ : I →B is a stochastic channel with conditional entropy H(I | B) and mutual informa-
tion I(I; B).
Consider a toy car in the Playcosm. Its interior I encodes tacit navigation heuristics (bal-
ancing, momentum), while boundary B exposes tokens {simulateJourney(), accelerate()}.
The map Σ has high H(I | B), indicating significant tacit loss under compression.
Definition 3.2 (Friction and Cost). Friction Cfriction(S) = αEb∼µB[exec_cost(b)] + βHB(S),
where α, β ≥0 are tunable weights. Merge cost:
cost(M) = Cfriction(M) −λHB(M),
λ > 0.
Definition 3.3 (Pop Operator). The pop operator is partial:
pop(S1, S2) = M ⇐⇒∃M s.t. A(S1, S2, M),
where admissibility A requires:
1. Interface compatibility: |BM ∩(B1 ∪B2)|/|B1 ∪B2| ≥θ.
2. Cost reduction: cost(M) < min(cost(S1), cost(S2)).
3. Fidelity bound: I(IM; BM) ≥γ(I(I1; B1) + I(I2; B2)) (optional, 0 ≤γ ≤1).

Algebraic Properties of Pop
Pop defines a partial monoid on S under composition, with undefined products where A fails. It
is neither associative nor commutative in general, but satisfies weak associativity when merges
preserve interface tokens.
Definition 4.1 (Pop-Closure). The pop-closure T = S0 is the minimal superset closed under
defined pops.

Dynamics and Pop Regimes
Definition 5.1 (Pop Regime). A pop regime R = (S, adj, Cfriction, HB, λ, τ) with stochastic
update:
1. Sample adjacent pair (Si, Sj) with probability ∝|Bi ∩Bj|.
2. If admissible merges exist, sample M∗= arg min cost(Mk).
3. Accept with probability p = sigmoid(−γ(cost(M∗) −τ)).
Theorem 5.2 (Monotonic Boundary Collapse). If every admissible pop satisfies HB(M) ≤
HB(S1) + HB(S2) −δ for fixed δ > 0, then total boundary entropy strictly decreases along pop
sequences. No infinite decreasing chain exists.
2

Proof. Each pop reduces total HB by at least δ. Since entropy is bounded below by 0, sequences
terminate.
Theorem 5.3 (Fixed Point Existence). For finite S and deterministic pop selection, the process
reaches a fixed point in finite time.
Proof. Define Lyapunov function L(S) = P HB(S) + κ|S|. Each pop decreases L by at least
min(δ, κ). Finite state space implies termination.

Resistance, Anti-Admissibility, and Proofs
Definition 6.1 (Resistance Vector). r(S) = (r1, . . . , rk) where coordinates increase Cfriction or
reduce adjacency (e.g., r1: semantic depth, r2: cryptographic hardness).
Theorem 6.2 (Anti-Admissibility Threshold). Let initiator budget B limit attack steps.
If
resistance ri requires Ω(2ri) steps to break, then for ri ≥log2 B + 1, Pr[pop success] ≤2−|B|.
Proof. Union bound over attack paths; each coordinate contributes exponential cost.
