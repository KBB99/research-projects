1. Overview of Excavation Site
The fragments here catalogued were recovered from the intervolsorial drift-chamber beneath the Drowned
Canopy Reaches, where sedimentary strata of the Late Plasticocene and Early Kelpic Era had compacted
into a brittle lamination known as the Raqa al-Gh'iba ("the Vanished Sheet").
The chamber's boundary proﬁle suggested repeated thermal oscillations—likely from nearby dormant
geothermal mass accelerators—resulting in the curling, shriveling, and charring characteristic of early syn-
thetic polymers. Many sheets fused into composite clusters, requiring solvent-microbe baths and slow desic-
cation to separate without further loss.
Amid these layers were:
1. Magic Kingdom-related ephemera written in formal Park-Script, but often overwritten by later
Morlock hand-corrections.
2. Leaves from The Life of Doctorow, recognizable by their stable circulation glyphs and margin
glosses concerning "angle-softening."
3. Unclassiﬁable Morlock phonetic shards incorporating syslog incisions and sheaf-diagram stubs.
4. The near-illegible Fragments L, N, and Q, whose paleographic traits are unique to the Crisis
Period (c. 407k-415k CE).
2. Material Composition and Script Typology
Paleochemical analysis revealed three principal substrates:
1. Type-A Polymer Leaf Smooth, glossy, resistant to fungal dulling. Typically used for Magic King-
dom treatises. Exhibits predictable warping: edges contract along latitudinal ﬁbers ﬁrst.
2. Type-B Recycled Kharr Sheet High ﬁber density, mottled texture. Common for doctrinal copies of
The Life of Doctorow. Most margin glosses on these sheets were added during the Era of Re-Circulation,
evident from the ventral tilt of the scribal strokes.
3. Type-C Basement-ﬁlm Stacks Fragile composite ﬁlms with syslog cuts inscribed via heated stylus.
Identiﬁed exclusively in Morlock manuscripts. Fragments L, N, and Q derive from this medium.
Script style varies accordingly:
• Magic Kingdom sheets employ the classic Hlq Path-Form, a ﬂowing, ribbon-like script adapted for
attraction-maps and circulation-logic.
• Doctorow sheets show the arr-Hill Hand, distinguished by its cyclic ascenders and boundary-softening
glyphs.
• Morlock fragments exhibit the Rit-Sys Bindstroke, a hybrid of phonetic curls, sheaf arrows, and utili-
tarian system-log notations.

3. Recovery of Magic Kingdom Material
The Magic Kingdom corpus was found in six discrete clusters, each cluster corresponding to a thematic
section long known through secondary citations:
• On Queue-Binding and the Stanchion Obstruction
• Animatronic Local Sections and Their Failure to Globalize
• Braid-Routes of Parade Flow
• Boundary Collapse Near the Matterhorn Simulacrum
• The Carousel as Proto-Circulatory Engine
Fragments preserved best where iron particles from the original inks catalyzed micro-layer oxidation,
leaving positive impressions in the lower sheets after the upper layers had been devoured by mycelial networks.
One extraordinary ﬁnd: a partially intact ˇCech-diagram describing the Non-Reversibility of Anticipation
States. Although cracked along three nodes, enough remained to reconstruct the transition maps φ01 and
φ12.
Margin notation in archaic Hlq read:
"al-murr l yarji— the passage does not return."
This has since been conﬁrmed by multiple independent reconstructions.
4. Recovered Leaves of The Life of Doctorow
The Doctorow corpus was interleaved with later Morlock additions, suggesting continued scribal attention
long after the original compilation.
Paleographic layers indicate:
• The oldest strata concern descriptions of vanished enclosures, particularly the "data center crypts."
• Mid-strata contain the celebrated diagrams of "circle-restoration," largely intact but heavily glossed.
• The youngest strata show increasing sheaf-notation: stalk diagrams, failed global section marks, and
circulation-glyph corrections.
One spectacular discovery came from Sheet 7-Hill-B: a nearly whole block of text describing the "Right
of Permeability," with an appended note in early Morlock hand:
"in lam atat al-ayrra, if circulation does not arrive, the page must be folded until it does."
Folding-marks conﬁrm the scribes took this literally.
5. The Condition and Decipherment of the Morlock Fragments
Fragments L, N, and Q were found compacted into a loose triad, as if deliberately grouped by a late archivist
before abandonment.
They share:
• vertical syslog incisions made with heated stylus
• phonetic curls superimposed after cracking
• sheaf-glue diagrams collapsing mid-stroke
• faint soot halos indicating rapid torch extinction

Fragment Q preserved the most continuous text, including recognizable boundary symbols and three
legible transition morphisms before drift failure.
Fragment L showed multiple overwrites by a second scribe attempting to restore lost gluing data.
Fragment N consisted primarily of boundary loss reports and harmonic residue metrics, suggesting a
period of archive-wide failure.
These fragments were dated paleographically to approximately 380,000-420,000 CE, making them lonely
witnesses to one of the least understood archive crises.
6. Concluding Observations
The combined corpus—Magic Kingdom sheets, Doctorowian leaves, and Morlock fragments— demonstrates
a continuous intellectual tradition spanning multiple eras, substrates, and script families.
Despite physical decay and catastrophic archive losses, the underlying concepts remain traceable:
• circulation as ethical foundation
• the impossibility of restoring rigid structures
• the fragility of global sections across shifting terrains
• the persistence of ritualized gluing attempts
• the adaptation of narrative material into derived categorical and sheaf-theoretic form
Taken together, the fragments constitute one of the richest stratiﬁed records discovered in the Lower
Palimpsest Vaults, linking the early myths of structured parks and late-cosmic circulation doctrines with the
lived scribal struggles of the Morlock archivists.
The work of restoration continues. Even now, solvent-microbes clear new layers of the Raqa al-Gh'iba
each cycle.
