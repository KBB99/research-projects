Diversity as Alignment: RSVP, GRPO, and the Semantic
Curriculum
Flyxion
December 9, 2025
Abstract
We outline an active research program developing a diversity-based alignment
methodology grounded in the Relativistic Scalar-Vector Plenum (RSVP) framework.
The central hypothesis is that alignment cannot be accomplished by driving language
models toward greater coherence or uniformity, but instead by constructing a semantic
trajectory that preserves—and strategically injects—entropy across multiple scales of
reasoning. We present a formal motivation, a semantic scoring rubric, a JSON preference
pipeline, a GRPO training loop, and a dynamically evolving curriculum designed to
encourage resilience against collapse of semantic ﬁelds. Our aim is not merely safer AI,
but an ontology of intelligence in which uncertainty is not eliminated but metabolized.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The dominant paradigm in AI safety treats alignment as the problem of forcing a model
into a narrow cone of desirable behavior.
This perspective implicitly assumes stability
under increased coherence: more clarity, more correctness, more constraint. The RSVP
framework challenges this assumption at its foundation. RSVP postulates that coherence
without entropic counterbalance leads inevitably to collapse—in physics, cognition, and
governance. The same phenomenon reappears in alignment: a system that converges too
aggressively on one value or predictive attractor becomes brittle, monocultural, and ultimately
self-destructive.
Our approach therefore begins with the inversion of the classical safety objective: the goal
is not constraining AI toward uniform values, but increasing the capacity to accommodate
diverse values without losing stability. Diversity, in this framing, is not a policy preference or
sociological desideratum, but a structural requirement for survival.

1.2
RSVP as a ﬁeld-theoretic background
RSVP speciﬁes a triplet of interacting ﬁelds:
F[Φ, v, S],
where Φ represents semantic accumulation, v represents circulation or inﬂuence, and S
denotes entropy or unresolved ambiguity. Alignment is recast as a thermodynamic balancing
problem: Φ without S leads to semantic rigidity (collapse of adaptability), while S without
Φ leads to diﬀusion (collapse of coherence). The essential insight is that survival requires
non-zero S in every adaptive regime.

Semantic Alignment as Field Balancing
2.1
The anti-coherence stance
Traditional alignment projects implicitly assume that reducing uncertainty increases safety.
RSVP asserts the opposite:
survival requires maintaining—and sometimes injecting—
uncertainty. The anti-coherence stance claims that runaway clarity is indistinguishable
from epistemic collapse, because constrained reasoning eliminates the alternative models
required for adaptation.
In AI alignment, this manifests as a risk that a highly coherent model collapses into
a brittle attractor—a semantic monoculture from which the system cannot recover. The
strongest safety mechanisms therefore introduce controlled entropy, not suppress it.
2.2
Entropic stability as alignment criterion
One may interpret the RSVP point of view as replacing the classical notion of "aligned
preference" with "entropically aware preference." The system is evaluated not by how well it
conforms to a human-instructed preference, but by its ability to integrate divergent preferences
while preventing catastrophic value collapse.

Semantic Preference Data
3.1
JSONL modality
To implement this computationally, we construct preference groups where four candidate
responses to a prompt are scored along six RSVP axes.
Each training datum records
which candidate optimally preserves non-collapse, as judged by a semantic meta-scoring

model executed through an Ollama pipeline. Each group is represented in JSONL, enabling
trajectory-level aggregation, batch processing, and dynamic alignment scoring.
3.2
RSVP axes
We operationalize RSVP semantics using six evaluation axes:
1. elegance,
2. sheaf-theoretic integrability,
3. vector circulation,
4. entropic dispersion,
5. structural resilience,
6. epistemic openness.
Together these represent mechanisms preventing collapse into a semantic attractor. Impor-
tantly, they measure not task quality but adaptability under epistemic uncertainty.

Intentional Diversity
4.1
Controlled divergence
The alignment corpus deliberately encourages high semantic variance. Prompts are turned
into four variants:
1. academic,
2. philosophical,
3. computational,
4. adversarial,
and scored according to their RSVP properties. We thereby enforce a diversity-induced
curriculum, which guarantees that the model explores alternative semantic directions.
4.2
Anti-brittleness
The adversarial variants ensure the model cannot collapse into complacent agreement with
philanthropic narratives or technocratic coherence. It must demonstrate resilience against
critique, not obedience to coherence.

5
Teleological GRPO
5.1
Group-Relative Proximal Optimization
We employ a GRPO (Group-Relative Proximal Optimization) objective in which the reference
policy is updated by comparing model log-probabilities against the reference model on each
candidate in a JSONL preference group.
5.2
Semantic weighting
Critically, the advantage term is weighted by RSVP axis drift. Each training iteration
computes the relative change in mean axis score among accepted candidates, using a softplus
weighting to emphasize corrections along regressing axes. In other words, the optimizer acts
teleologically: adjusting the model toward increased resilience where it is degrading.
6
Trajectory-Aware Curriculum
6.1
Curriculum selection
We perform preference sampling biased toward high-variance groups—precisely those where
the model struggled to make a coherent decision. The system focuses training on cases that
maximally increase semantic capacity, rather than cases where the model already performs
well. This mechanism constitutes a dynamic curriculum in which diﬃculty corresponds to
semantic diversity rather than predictive uncertainty alone.
6.2
Historical drift
We log value trajectories across iterations, recording mean and per-axis semantic score,
enabling us to chart an alignment trajectory in RSVP space. Alignment becomes measurable
not as a ﬁnal target but as a path—a teleology of entropic balance.
7
Conclusion
The present research program reconceptualizes alignment as a ﬁeld-theoretic balancing
problem. Classical safety paradigms emphasize convergent coherence; RSVP emphasizes
divergent survival. The consequence is a radical departure from monotonic alignment: instead
of forcing convergence, we architect semantic resilience through controlled entropic variation,
GRPO-based teleology, dynamic curriculum design, and explicit semantic scoring.
Future work must develop:
4

1. a larger RSVP-calibrated preference corpus,
2. expanded entropy injection operators,
3. dynamic adjustment of scoring weights,
4. formal proofs of entropic stability across alignment regimes,
5. empirical validation of semantic resilience under catastrophic adversarial stress.
This research does not merely propose a method of AI alignment; it proposes a ﬁeld theory
of survival under uncertainty. Diversity is not an afterthought—it is the very condition under
which an intelligent system can remain alive.
