Abstract. Jacques
Ellul saw advertising
as a machine that
shapes not markets
but minds. Helen
Rollins sees cinema
as a psychic engine
that frames what
desire can mean.
Both trace how
modern media turn
freedom into feedback—how
persuasion becomes
infrastructure.
This essay links
Ellul's "indoctrination
into technique"
with Rollins' "psychocinema,"
showing that the
drive for efficiency
and the dream of
narrative share
one hidden goal: to
keep contradiction
smooth enough to
sell.
1. Ellul: Teaching
the Logic of Technique
Advertising, for
Ellul, is the nervous
system of industrial
life. It doesn't
answer needs; it
teaches people to
need. Once machines
can make more than
people want, ads
must make people
want more than
they need. Each
slogan rehearses a
ritual: progress
equals novelty,
novelty equals
virtue. The ad
trains reflexes—consume,
refresh, repeat.
The purpose is
not belief but
adaptation: to
feel at home inside
acceleration.
2. Rollins: Cinema
as the Dream of
Capital
Rollins reads film
as a public unconscious.
Movies let viewers
live their conflicts
as comfort. Every
cut resolves what
life cannot. Desire
is staged, confessed,
forgiven. The screen
translates anxiety
into beauty, making
alienation look
sublime. Cinema

3. Shared Machine
Both forms discipline
attention. Ads
build rhythmic
wants. Films build
rhythmic empathy.
Each takes excess—of
goods or emotion—and
loops it back into
order. The result
is a stable system:
endless motion
without rebellion.

5. The Politics of
Smoothness
Ellul saw dissent
softened by design;
Rollins sees revolt
filmed into fantasy.
In both, conflict
becomes content.
Anger becomes an ad,
rebellion becomes a
movie. The system

6. Counter-Technics
Freedom requires
friction. A counter-
technical art or
ethics must resist
closure, must slow
the loop. To type,
to think, to make
without haste—this
itself becomes a
gesture of resistance:
restoring delay to
a world addicted to
speed.

Abstract. Ellul

frames film as
dreamwork for capital.
Each reveals how
technique trains
feeling—how we
learn to crave
efficiency and
enjoy our own constraint.
This piece parallels
Ellul's "indoctrination"
and Rollins' "psychocinema,"
reading both as
engines that tune
emotion to order.
1. Ellul: Need by
Design
For Ellul, promotion
is pedagogy. Once
production outruns
purpose, persuasion
must invent the
urge. Ads sing a
single key: new
equals good. They
loop comfort and
motion—want, get,
want again. The
outcome is obedience
disguised as choice.
The ad doesn't
speak to thought;
it scripts habit.
2. Rollins: Film as
Fantasy Regulator
Rollins treats
the reel as dream
syntax. The frame
folds fear into
pleasure, showing
lack as luxury.
Cinema's rhythm
trains the pulse:
breath, beat, resolve.
Viewers rehearse
surrender until it
feels like insight.
Screen and psyche
mirror one machine.
3. Common Circuit
Advertising converts
labor into appetite;
cinema converts
anxiety into image.
Both recycle tension
as order. Ads polish
demand. Film polishes
despair. The result:
perpetual calm
through perpetual
churn.
