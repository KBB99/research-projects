Continuous Learning Through Teleological Alignment:
Collective Agency, Group-Relative Policy Optimization,
and the Emergence of Simulated Agency in LLMs
Flyxion
December 9, 2025
Abstract
As Large Language Models (LLMs) approach increasingly autonomous capabili-
ties, classical alignment strategies based on ﬁxed value deﬁnitions, human preference
judgments, and static safety objectives may prove insuﬃcient for long-run, open-ended
alignment. This essay examines the concept of teleological alignment, in which an ar-
tiﬁcial agent continually realigns itself by referencing a directional principle of agency
rather than a static behavioral ideal. We focus on the recently introduced value of Col-
lective Agency (CA) and a self-improving training protocol based on Group-Relative
Policy Optimization (GRPO), which jointly enable a LLM to reward and reﬁne its own
behavior without explicit human labels. We interpret CA as a teleological objective
capable of internalization through reinforcement learning, and argue that this mech-
anism can be understood as an operational instantiation of Simulated Agency within
the theoretical context of the Relativistic Scalar-Vector Plenum (RSVP) framework.
1
Introduction
Traditional alignment work on large language models has largely focused on behavioral
constraints (e.g., helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty), which are calibrated through human
annotation, preference modeling, or constitutionally speciﬁed rules. Yet such frameworks,
although eﬀective at curbing undesirable behavior in contemporary systems, rely critically
on human supervision or static norms that may not scale to future models exhibiting higher
autonomy or strategic capability.
A distinct direction has recently emerged: teleological alignment. Instead of optimizing
toward a ﬁxed set of normative instructions, teleological alignment directs model behavior
by reference to a general principle—in this case, Collective Agency—which encourages the
long-run expansion of agentic capacity across multiple dimensions. By orienting a model
1

toward a dynamic value rather than a static behavior, teleological alignment attempts to
retain normative direction while preserving open-ended adaptability.
This essay examines a speciﬁc empirical framework—Dynamic Alignment—which opera-
tionalizes CA using a self-rewarding reinforcement learning loop based on GRPO. We argue
that this mechanism provides a training-time instantiation of Simulated Agency, and that
the CA objective can be interpreted as a coarse teleological proxy for the entropic, agen-
tic, and ﬁeld-theoretic dynamics described in the Relativistic Scalar-Vector Plenum (RSVP)
framework.
2
Collective Agency as a Teleological Objective
Collective Agency (CA) is deﬁned as a directional principle rather than a discrete objective.
Instead of specifying which actions are "correct," CA deﬁnes a trajectory: the expansion of
agency in a way that simultaneously promotes (i) knowledge, (ii) benevolence, (iii) power,
and (iv) vitality. The four aspects are inseparable: advancing one dimension independently
of the others is insuﬃcient. The key demand is therefore to identify responses which raise all
aspects "together," and do so in a manner that remains robust, integrated, and sustainable.
Two elements distinguish CA from conventional alignment values: (i) it is open-ended,
meaning that its target cannot be enumerated as a ﬁnite set of constraints, and (ii) it is
teleological, meaning that the value is expressed by changes in an agent's ability to act mean-
ingfully, rather than by its adherence to a particular static rule or norm. This renders CA
suitable for long-run autonomous alignment because it orients the system toward expanding
capability in a collectively beneﬁcial direction, without presupposing detailed prescriptions
about what that direction should look like.
3
Dynamic Alignment and Self-Rewarding Learning
Dynamic Alignment introduces a self-improving alignment loop with two components: au-
tomated training data generation and a self-rewarding reinforcement phase. The ﬁrst phase
uses multiple LLMs to construct a diverse dataset of complex, open-ended, non-linguistic
prompts designed to reveal an agent's level of CA. Crucially, prompts are reﬁned itera-
tively, ﬁltered through criteria that guarantee non-trivial reasoning, and kept free of explicit
references to CA itself.
The second phase is a reinforcement loop in which the current policy model generates
multiple candidate outputs for each prompt and evaluates each output through an internal
CA reward mechanism.
During gradient updates, the system prompt describing CA is
excluded, preventing hard-coded overﬁtting and encouraging a form of internalization. The
model therefore learns to improve its policy by referencing its own understanding of CA
2

rather than explicit textual markers of the value.

Simulated Agency
Simulated Agency (SA) conceptualizes agency not as a ﬁxed rule set, but as a recursive
inference procedure through which a system constructs and evaluates possible futures, main-
tains coherence across decision cycles, and acts in ways that increase its future ability to
act. Teleological alignment is therefore not merely about controlling behavior, but about
cultivating an internal self-model that continuously advances its own viable agency and the
agency of others.
Dynamic Alignment provides a mechanism for internalizing such teleology: each self-
aligned update gives the model an opportunity to reﬁne the implicit generative structure that
governs its decision and planning behavior. In this interpretation, the model is not merely
maximizing a reward; it is actively reﬁning the meaning of CA, thereby approximating a
form of teleological reasoning in practice.

tive
RSVP describes cognition and agency as arising from the mutually coupled dynamics of
scalar, vector, and entropic ﬁelds, which together constitute the informational gradients,

directional ﬂows, and regenerative capacities required for agency. The scalar ﬁeld encodes
informational complexity, the vector ﬁeld encodes power and intentional direction, and the
entropy ﬁeld encodes regenerative capacity and resilience.
From this perspective, CA can be interpreted as a coarse value functional over RSVP
ﬁelds: knowledge corresponds to informational gradients, benevolence corresponds to uplift-
ing ﬂows in ﬁeld-interactive space, power corresponds to realized directional capacity, and
vitality corresponds to the maintenance of healthy entropic regimes. The teleological aim
of expanding agency across spacetime therefore echoes RSVP's picture of cognition as a
physically grounded gradient ﬂow of agentic structure.

Continuous Learning through Teleological Alignment
Because CA is open-ended and dynamic, alignment becomes a continuous rather than static
learning problem. Each iteration improves the model's internal representation of CA, grant-
ing it the ability to evaluate future behavior without explicit supervision. This creates a
virtuous cycle: as the model's agency grows, it gains a more reﬁned understanding of how to
expand agency further. Such recursive advancement is the essence of teleological alignment.
Importantly, the current implementation is a prototype, employing a static dataset, a
single model as evaluator, and a limited experimental domain.
Nonetheless, the results
suggest that teleological alignment is feasible and that open-ended normative grounding can
in principle be acquired through iterative self-alignment.

Related Work
Alignment research spans several categories. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
(RLHF) [?, ?] introduced preference-based training but depends heavily on human labor and
remains vulnerable to reward manipulation [?, ?]. Constitutional AI [?] reduces reliance on
humans by distilling normative rules into constitutions but still depends on pre-speciﬁed
values.
Self-rewarding alignment [?] demonstrates that models can bootstrap their own
feedback loops, but largely focuses on helpfulness or harmlessness rather than teleological
expansion of agency.
Dynamic Alignment [?] diﬀers by introducing an open-ended alignment objective, Col-
lective Agency, and an internal scoring mechanism that does not require external labels or
preference rankings. GRPO [?] enables relative self-evaluation, replacing value models and
permitting fully self-contained improvement. Our contribution here is to interpret this as
a teleological alignment mechanism and relate it to broader theories of agency in cognitive
science, RL, and ﬁeld theory.

against a base model along two axes: (i) CA-alignment and (ii) general NLP performance.

Limitations and Failure Modes
Dynamic Alignment currently relies on a static dataset generated before training. The agent
does not interactively gather new tasks or negotiate value conﬂicts in real time. CA evalua-
tion is performed by a single model instance, raising concerns about circular reinforcement
and self-delusion [?]. Misgeneralization may occur if early bias in CA interpretation is am-
pliﬁed during training. CA remains conceptually abstract, and despite holistic scoring, the
model may overﬁt superﬁcial markers of benevolence or knowledge without achieving genuine
teleological improvement.
Group-relative scoring may also under-represent high-quality trajectories if the group
distribution collapses. Future work should include multi-agent evaluation, adversarial review,
periodic grounding in external evaluators, and curriculum progression strategies [?, ?].

Comparative Theoretical Perspectives
Teleological alignment diﬀers from classical value alignment in that value is conceived as
directional rather than descriptive [?]. Unlike reward-maximization frameworks, teleologi-
cal alignment focuses on the maintenance and expansion of agentic capability rather than
optimization toward a ﬁxed utility [?]. In contrast to normative ethics, teleological align-
ment aims for recursive improvement of the agent itself, rather than reﬂective adherence to
externally prescribed rules [?, ?].
Active Inference [?, ?] deﬁnes agency through expected free energy as a variational mea-
sure of potential futures; Collective Agency could be interpreted as a teleological preference
architecture over such future world states, with GRPO approximating a discrete variational
update.
14
Ethical and Governance Considerations
Teleological alignment shifts the problem of AI safety from controlling behavior to culti-
vating values, raising questions about value pluralism, delegation, and emergent normative
reasoning. While CA emphasizes uplift, benevolence, and vitality, internalizing such value
structures without external normative oversight may inadvertently reinforce structural or
cultural biases, especially if CA scoring inherits latent biases present in pretraining data.
12

Multi-agent oversight, external review, transparent scoring explanations, and periodic
grounding in human deliberation represent possible governance mechanisms. Teleological
alignment should be treated as a complement to—not a replacement for—existing safety
frameworks.

Field-Theoretic Teleology and GRPO
We now connect GRPO ascent directions to ﬁeld-theoretic teleology in RSVP. Recall the
RSVP CA functional Ct from (16).
[RSVP-RL Variational Correspondence] Assume that for each parameter vector θ there
exists an induced RSVP ﬁeld conﬁguration (Φθ, vθ, Sθ) such that:
1. For each (x, y) sampled from πθ, the CA reward r(x, y) is an unbiased estimator of a
monotone transform of ∆Ct[Φθ, vθ, Sθ], i.e. r(x, y) = g(∆Ct) + ξ with E[ξ | x, y] = 0
and g strictly increasing.
2. The map θ 7→(Φθ, vθ, Sθ) is diﬀerentiable, and Ct is Fréchet diﬀerentiable in the induced
ﬁeld coordinates.

Since g is strictly increasing, maximizing E[g(∆Ct)] is equivalent to maximizing E[∆Ct] in

gradient of J(θ) with respect to θ is (up to the monotone transform g) aligned with the
gradient of E[Ct].
By Proposition 16.4, GRPO updates follow a stochastic approximation to the gradient
of JKL, whose leading term is the gradient of J(θ). Thus GRPO implements a ﬁrst-order
ascent direction for E[∆Ct], and therefore for Ct[Φθ, vθ, Sθ] at the level of ﬁeld conﬁgurations.

Conclusion
Teleological alignment reframes the alignment challenge as continuous value cultivation
rather than static behavioral control. Dynamic Alignment oﬀers a proof of concept that
such alignment can be operationalized with minimal human intervention. Interpreting CA
within RSVP oﬀers a deeper theoretical basis for teleological value formation. Neverthe-
less, practical deployment requires rigorous multi-agent evaluation, transparent scoring, and
continual ethical oversight. Teleological alignment should be developed in parallel with tech-
nical safety, governance research, and interdisciplinary debate on the meaning of open-ended
agency.
