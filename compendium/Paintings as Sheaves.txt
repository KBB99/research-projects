Paintings as Sheaves of Latent Physical Structure
Flyxion
November 8, 2025
Abstract
We formalize a view of painted artwork as an information-dense spatial model specifying im-
plicit physical constraints. A painting induces a sheaf of locally coherent physical interpretations
over the image plane. Animation is modeled as global section selection followed by a functorial
lift to dynamical evolution. Ambiguities in motion are shown to correspond to non-uniqueness
of global sections, while stylistic consistency corresponds to sheaf coherence and rigidity of re-
striction morphisms. This model explains why generative animation systems recover physically
plausible motion from artwork containing strong internal physics priors, even when the scenes
are imagined rather than observed.
1
Image Space as a Topological Domain
Let X be the 2-dimensional support of a painting, treated as a topological space whose open sets
U ⊆X correspond to spatial regions on the canvas. Local visual data in U (color, brush stroke
orientation, shading gradients, anatomical cues, specular highlights) imply a set of compatible
physical interpretations of that region.
Define a presheaf
F : Open(X)op →Set
where F(U) is the set of all local interpretations of U consistent with:
• implied lighting and reflectance models,
• locally plausible geometry and surface normals,
• partial motion priors encoded by pose and articulation,
• any painterly conventions functioning as perceptual physics.
For U ⊆V , restriction maps
ρV,U : F(V ) →F(U)
project a larger patch's interpretations down to subregions by retaining only compatible constraints.
2
Sheaf Condition as Internal Consistency
The painting exhibits physical coherence when F satisfies the sheaf axioms:
1. (Locality) If sections s, t ∈F(U) satisfy ρU,Ui(s) = ρU,Ui(t) for all Ui in an open cover of U,
then s = t.
1

2. (Gluing) For an open cover {Ui} of U, any compatible family of local sections
si ∈F(Ui),
ρUi,Ui∩Uj(si) = ρUj,Ui∩Uj(sj)
admits a global section s ∈F(U) with ρU,Ui(s) = si.
A painting is:
• Physically realizable iff F(X) ̸= ∅.
• Deterministic in its implied physics iff |F(X)| = 1.
• Ambiguous iff |F(X)| > 1 (multiple valid global worlds).
• Contradictory iff F(X) = ∅.
Ambiguous elements, such as forms without directional bias (e.g., a dinosaur lacking a privileged
head-tail axis), correspond precisely to the case |F(X)| > 1.

Animation as Functorial Dynamics
Let Dyn be a category whose objects are dynamical world models (state spaces endowed with
admissible motion fields) and whose morphisms preserve physical continuity.
An animation model implicitly factors as:
1. Global section selection: choose
s ∈F(X)
a single coherent physical interpretation of the painting.
2. Temporal lift: apply a functor
T : Set →Dyn
whose action on s produces a dynamical evolution T(s) consistent with local constraints
encoded by the sheaf.
Thus, animation is not synthesis from nothing, but prolongation of latent structural constraints
into a time-evolving physics.

Visual light cues induce structured compatibility conditions in F analogous to rendering operators:
ρlight

These behave like discrete approximations of light transport:
• specular elongation →convolution with anisotropic kernels,

A painted image whose optical restriction maps approximate these operators will be animated

Ambiguity as Moduli of Global Sections
Let
M = F(X)
be the moduli space of admissible global interpretations. Local disambiguating cues act as con-
straints reducing M by defining subsheaves F ′ ⊆F such that:
M′ = F ′(X),
|M′| ≤|M|.
A painting decision (e.g., a biased stance or gaze direction) decreases |M| without compromising

When |M′| = 1, motion becomes determinate. When |M′| > 1, dynamics resolve arbitrarily
among valid sections, producing effects like directionally invertible gait.

Conclusion
A painting acts as:
• a partially specified physical theory compressed into image space,
• whose consistency is measured by sheaf coherence,
• whose ambiguities are global section multiplicity,
• and whose animation is a functorial lift to dynamics.
Thus, generative animation is not inventing motion but unfolding the painter's implicit physics.
Motion does not enter the painting.
It leaves it.

Higher Sheaf Structure and Homotopical Ambiguity
The space of valid global interpretations of a painting often carries higher-order ambiguity, not
merely multiplicity but deformation paths between interpretations.
To capture this, we promote the sheaf
F : Open(X)op →Set
to an ∞-sheaf
F : Open(X)op →Gpd∞,
whose values are ∞-groupoids encoding not only distinct interpretations, but homotopies between
them.
Under this refinement:
• Individual world-interpretations are 0-simplices.
• Continuous deformations between interpretations (e.g., forward- vs backward-walking di-
nosaurs) are 1-simplices.
• Higher coherence conditions on interpretation shifts form k-simplices.
A painting with binary but distinct resolutions has a discrete 0-truncated ∞-sheaf.
A painting whose ambiguities admit smooth deformation lies in a connected coalescent of
π0(F(X)), but possesses nontrivial π1, giving rise to homotopy classes of interpretations.
Animation now factors as:
T : |F(X)| −→Dyn,
where |F(X)| is the geometric realization of interpretation space. The selected animation cor-
responds to choosing a path-connected component, then lifting along a contractible trajectory to a
dynamical section.
Thus, ambiguous motion is not a pathological failure but the presence of a nontrivial homotopy
class in interpretation space.
Interpretation stability correlates with homotopical rigidity:
F(X) is rigid
⇐⇒πk(F(X)) = 0 ∀k ≥1.
This reframes animation errors as higher-sheaf invariants rather than reconstruction defects.
8
Theorem: Existence and Uniqueness of Animated Continuations
Let F be the ∞-sheaf of physical interpretations induced by a painting on canvas X, and let
T : |F(X)| →Dyn be a functor assigning dynamical evolutions to realizable global sections. Then:
1. A physically coherent animation exists if and only if π0(F(X)) ̸= ∅. 2. The animation is
unique up to homotopy if and only if F(X) is 0-truncated and contractible. 3. The animation ex-
hibits directional ambiguity if |π0(F(X))| > 1. 4. The animation exhibits continuously deformable
ambiguity if π1(F(X)) ̸= 0.
4

Proof. (1) By definition, an animation requires a global physical interpretation. This corresponds
to a global section, which exists precisely when F(X) is non-empty, i.e., π0(F(X)) ̸= ∅.
(2) If F(X) is 0-truncated and contractible, then it has exactly one connected component and
no higher homotopy. Thus any two sections are homotopic, implying a unique dynamical evolution
up to deformation.
(3) If |π0(F(X))| > 1, there exist multiple disconnected sections. Selection of section prior to
animation produces non-equivalent motions, e.g., forward- vs backward-propagating gaits.
(4) If π1(F(X)) ̸= 0, interpretations lie in the same connected component but admit nontrivial
loops. This corresponds to smoothly deformable ambiguity rather than discrete choice (e.g., limb
articulation that can continuously reverse without discontinuity).
Hence the structure of observed animation variance corresponds exactly to the homotopy type
of F(X).

Painting Layers as Subsheaf Decomposition
To guide animation toward intended motion, the artist constructs the painting in three layered
constraint systems, each corresponding to a subsheaf of the total physical interpretation sheaf F.
9.1
Macro Directionality Subsheaf
This subsheaf Fdir ⊆F encodes forward-backward orientation. Local sections assign to each open
U a directed axis consistent with:
• head-body polarity,
• gaze or snout vector,
• asymmetric mass distribution,
• dominant thrust line,
• staggered limb positions.
Restriction maps ρdir
V,U preserve directional bias. A single asymmetry (e.g., one limb advanced)
collapses |π0(Fdir(X))| = 1, eliminating directional ambiguity.
9.2
Mechanical Constraints Subsheaf
The subsheaf Fmech ⊆F specifies admissible articulations. For open U, sections include:
• joint hinge loci,
• muscle contraction axes,
• spinal flexion limits,
• ground contact normals,
• occlusion-derived depth order.
These enforce biological plausibility; restriction ensures no invalid deformations propagate.
5

9.3
Light and Material Subsheaf
The optics subsheaf Fopt ⊆F models discrete light transport. Local sections over U contain:
• directional falloff gradients,
• specular anisotropy,
• interreflection zones,
• atmospheric attenuation,
• shadow consistency.
Restriction maps ρopt
V,U mimic rendering operators, enabling temporal extrapolation as if ray-
traced.
The total sheaf is the fiber product F ≃Fdir × Fmech × Fopt over compatible local data.
Intentional motion arises when each subsheaf is rigid (|π0(Fi(X))| = 1, πk(Fi(X)) = 0 for k ≥1)
in relevant domains.
10
Workflow as Iterative Subsheaf Refinement
The painting process proceeds as:
1. Initial expressive sketch defines base presheaf F0.
2. Identify ambiguous loci where |π0(F0(U))| > 1.
3. Introduce local cues to define subsheaf F ′ ⊆F0 with reduced |π0(F ′(X))|.
4. Iterate until desired rigidity.
This preserves aesthetic ambiguity outside motion-critical regions.

Lavender Planet Geology

Dragon on Cliff

structurally encoded by head orientation and visual axis alignment, yielding a rare case where the
global section not only determines motion but also point of address.

Conclusion
A painted work encodes a finite-complexity ∞-sheaf whose homotopy invariants determine:
- whether animation exists, - whether motion is unique, reversible, or multivalent, - and whether
ambiguities resolve discretely or continuously.
Animation is therefore not generative hallucination but functorial unfolding of implicit homo-
topical physics embedded in paint.
