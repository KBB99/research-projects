Abstract
This paper develops a formal and conceptual analogy between the biochemical theory
of collectively autocatalytic sets and the epistemic dynamics of memeplexes—coherent
complexes of mutually reinforcing ideas, narratives, and practices. Building on Kuhn's and
Hanson's notion that all observation is theory-laden, we argue that memes (as units of
cultural transmission) form self-sustaining networks of justification and inference. Within
such networks, facts and theories co-generate each other, producing a self-referential closure
analogous to metabolic autocatalysis.
We extend the prior formulation with explicit food-set mappings, spectral and Lyapunov
analyses, global stability conditions, a hypergraph generalization, and an empirical interpretive
model using the RSVP (Relativistic Scalar-Vector Plenum) field formalism. A three-node
numerical example and the case of The Technological Republic illustrate the framework's
explanatory and predictive power.

Introduction
The term "meme," introduced by Dawkins as a cultural analogue to the gene, has been interpreted
as a unit of replication, mutation, and selection. Yet cultural ideas seldom replicate independently;
they cohere into structures of mutual reinforcement. The notion of an isolated meme is analogous
to an enzyme without a metabolic network—it cannot persist.

biochemical formalism. Each meme mi participates in inferential relations ri catalyzed by other

From Autocatalytic Sets to Memeplexes
Kauffman's autocatalytic framework describes molecular self-organization: molecules that catalyze
reactions forming other molecules in the same set constitute a collectively autocatalytic network.
Translating this logic to cognition, each meme becomes both product and catalyst of interpretation.
An autocatalytic network satisfies closure and self-sufficiency. Transposed:
molecules ↔memes,
reactions ↔inferential relations,
catalysts ↔theories or narratives.
A memeplex achieves closure when every inference is catalyzed by other members and each
meme derives justification from within the system.
The adjacency Aij quantifies catalytic strength: Aij > 0 if meme mj reinforces mi. Each
reaction ri has catalyst set C(ri) ⊆M, defining Aij = 1 when mj ∈C(ri).

Cultural and Cognitive Implications
Autocatalytic closure yields internal coherence and resistance to external perturbation. Memes
act as mutual justifications; novelty is either assimilated or rejected. This explains the tenacity
of ideologies and scientific paradigms.
When distinct memeplexes overlap in semantic space, nonlinear couplings can merge them into
new attractors—a process analogous to syncretism or paradigm fusion. Entropy influx,
representing novelty or critique, increases S; when it exceeds a threshold, closure fails and the
system undergoes reorganization.

Conclusion and Future Directions
Memeplexes behave as living epistemic organisms: self-organizing, self-justifying, and
semi-permeable. Their persistence arises from autocatalytic closure and entropy management,
phenomena measurable both conceptually and empirically.
Future research may extend to stochastic field theory (Langevin perturbations), adversarial or
cooperative memeplex interactions, and real-time detection in social media. Machine learning
integration could automate identification of ideological closure and design entropy-increasing
interventions to restore cognitive openness.
