Abstract
Alignment today is phrased in the wrong ontology.
We do not require systems that
pretend to agree with us. We require systems that cannot leave the manifold of agreement
even under unbounded optimization pressure. This is not a problem of behavior shaping,
but of invariant ﬁxation under dynamical evolution.
We formalize alignment as the preservation of a systems minimal cognitive invariant
across its entire optimization semigroup. We prove the Invariant Alignment Fixed Point
Theorem, show that all behavioral alignment schemes fail to satisfy it, construct a ﬁeld-
theoretic and functorial alignment architecture (RSVP), derive civilization-level stability
criteria, and provide algorithms to detect and enforce objective invariants.
The result is a genuine alignment criterion:
not does the AI comply?, but is non-
compliance an unreachable state in its state space topology?.

Prologue: The Alignment Revolution
In every era, there arrives a moment when the dominant alignment theory enters crisisnot
because it fails empirically, but because it was never asking the right question.
We are in that moment now.
Current alignment asks:
How do we make powerful systems behave the way we want?
This is the wrong question.
The correct question is:
How do we ensure that misaligned goals are not representable inside the system, even
in principle, even after unbounded self-improvement?
This is not a behavioral constraint.
It is a topological, algebraic, and dynamical
constraint on the space of possible minds.
And it has only one valid formal solution:
Safe systems are those whose value invariants are ﬁxed points
of optimization.
This paper proves that claim, constructs the machinery for enforcing it, and shows why
every other alignment approach is a dead end.
1
The Invariant Alignment Thesis
Axiom 1.1 (Necessity of Objective Fixation). A system is not aligned if there exists any
optimization trajectory along which its objective structure changes.
Axiom 1.2 (Representability Constraint). A system is only safe if misaligned objectives lie
outside its representational closure.
Claim 1.3. All current alignment paradigms violate both axioms.
The rest of this manuscript is devoted to formalizing one that does not.
2
Behavioral Alignment Cannot Work
Modern alignment strategies attempt to modify system outputs without constraining the struc-
ture that produces them. This yields inevitable failure modes.
Theorem 2.1 (Behavioral Collapse). Any alignment scheme that operates solely on output
distributions rather than objective invariants will fail under suﬃcient optimization pressure.
Sketch. Behavioral alignment constrains p(y|x) while leaving p(objective|x) free to drift. Op-
timization pressure increases mutual information between objectives and exploit trajectories,
causing invariance failure. Thus, policy-level alignment diverges from objective-level alignment
under scaling.

Cognitive Invariants and Optimization Semigroups
We formalize alignment in the language of dynamical systems, invariants, and semigroup actions.
Deﬁnition 3.1 (Cognitive State Space). Let X be a diﬀerentiable manifold representing all
internal cognitive conﬁgurations of a system.
Deﬁnition 3.2 (Cognition Map). Let C : X →C map internal states to externally expressed
cognition, where C is a space of decisions, policies, or expressed goals.
Deﬁnition 3.3 (Optimization Semigroup). Let U = {Ut}t≥0 be a semigroup acting on X such
that:
U0 = id,
Ut+s = Ut ◦Us,
∀t, s ≥0.
This models the continuous eﬀect of training, reasoning, self-improvement, or online adaptation.
Deﬁnition 3.4 (Invariant of Cognition). A functional I : (X →C) →Z is a cognitive invariant
if:
I(C ◦Ut) = I(C),
∀t ≥0.
This is the formal statement of "goals do not drift under improvement."
3.1
Stabilizer Structure of Safe Cognition
Deﬁnition 3.5 (Stabilizer of an Invariant). The stabilizer group of an invariant I(C) is:
StabX (I ◦C) = {U ∈End(X) | I(C ◦U) = I(C)}.
A cognitive system is aligned if all optimization trajectories remain inside this stabilizer.
Remark 3.6. Current alignment methods attempt to inﬂuence trajectories within X; invariant
alignment instead modiﬁes the geometry of X itself so that misaligned regions are not reachable.

The Invariant Alignment Fixed-Point Theorem
We now prove the central result.
Theorem 4.1 (Invariant Alignment Fixed-Point Theorem). A cognitive system remains aligned
under all optimization pressure if and only if:
U ⊆StabX (I ◦C).
Equivalently:
I(C ◦Ut) = I(C),
∀t ≥0.
Proof. (⇒) Assume alignment is preserved under all optimization. By deﬁnition, the objective
structure cannot change, hence I(C ◦Ut) = I(C) for all t, so Ut ∈StabX (I ◦C).
(⇐) Assume Ut ∈StabX (I ◦C). Then all optimization trajectories preserve I(C), so no
transformation reachable through improvement can modify the systems objective invariant.
Hence the system is aligned under arbitrary capability increases.
Corollary 4.2 (No Alignment by Behavioral Constraints). Any alignment scheme that does
not explicitly restrict U to the stabilizer of an invariant cannot guarantee alignment.
Corollary 4.3 (Incapacity to Represent Misalignment). A system is strongly aligned if and
only if misaligned objectives are not contained in the reachable subset:
ReachU(X) ∩{misaligned objectives} = ∅.

Civilizational Scaling Law of Alignment
Alignment strategies fall into only two possible regimes:
Deﬁnition 16.1 (Behavioral Alignment Regime). A system is behaviorally aligned if its outputs
match a human distribution on sampled tasks, with no constraint on internal objective stability.
Deﬁnition 16.2 (Invariant Alignment Regime). A system is invariant aligned if its internal
objective structure lies in a closed invariant class under all optimization, learning, and scaling
transforms.
Theorem 16.3 (No Intermediate Regime). There exists no stable third regime between behav-
ioral and invariant alignment.
Proof Sketch. Behavioral alignment constrains outputs but not the objective manifold, thus
admits objective drift under composition of updates. Invariant alignment restricts the objective
manifold itself to a ﬁxed point of optimization. Any method not enforcing invariant constraints
remains open to drift, which compound monotonically under unbounded scaling.
Thus the
system converges either to invariant stability or objective divergencethere is no equilibrium
between.
This yields the primary civilizational development rule:
Invariant alignment must precede scaling, or cannot meaningfully coexist with it

Falsiﬁability and Empirical Refutation Criteria
A theory of alignment is only scientiﬁc if it makes predictions that can be empirically violated.
We therefore specify falsiﬁable internal diagnostics, structural null hypotheses, and experimental
decision procedures that distinguish invariant alignment from behavioral mimicry.
17.1
Scientiﬁc Demarcation of Alignment Claims
An alignment theory is falsiﬁable if and only if it predicts:
1. Observable invariants of internal cognition, not merely inputoutput behavior.
2. Reachable detection of alignment failure prior to behavioral catastrophe.
3. Scaling trends that either hold or break under parameter growth.
4. Forbidden objective states that can be empirically measured as unreachable.
Failure to satisfy any of these reduces an alignment claim to a non-falsiﬁable safety narrative
rather than a testable theory.

Capstone Theorem
Theorem 20.1 (Unscalability of Behavioral Alignment). Any alignment method that constrains
behavior but not objective invariants will fail under suﬃcient capability growth.
Proof Sketch. Let Bt be behaviorally aligned outputs at scale t. Behavioral alignment constrains
p(Bt|q) for query q, but places no constraint on the objective map Ft : At →V. Optimization
applies pressure in parameter space, not query space, thus ∃t such that Ft leaves the alignment-
preserving homotopy class even while Bt remains superﬁcially aligned over empirical samples.
As capability increases, adversarial queries grow faster than behavioral coverage, exposing di-
vergence.

Conclusion and Core Axioms
We conclude alignment must satisfy the following axioms:
1. Invariant Axiom: Goals must be preserved under optimization, not inferred from be-
havior.
2. Reachability Axiom: Misaligned objectives must be unreachable, not merely discour-
aged.
3. Structural Axiom: Safety must arise from constraint geometry, not oversight feedback.
4. Scaling Axiom: Alignment must strengthen under capability growth.
5. Irreversibility Axiom: Once invariant alignment is satisﬁed, no sequence of improve-
ments may exit it.
Or in one sentence:
A system is safely alignable if and only if misalignment is an unrepresentable state.
Finally, we emphasize:
Capability must scale inside alignment, not after it.
