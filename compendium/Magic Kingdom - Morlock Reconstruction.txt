Abstract
The ancient park is reconstructed here from its surviving regions.
Each region
provides partial information; meanings arise only through their intersections. Where
extension fails, illusion appears. Where local compatibility stabilizes, experience forms.
The park is not recovered as a whole but as a family of overlapping remnants whose
coherence must be inferred.

Local Regions of the Entrance
The entrance survives only as fragments of pavement and scattered pillars. These remnants
define an open region U0. Within U0, certain sensory traces still persist: a narrowing of
sound, a slight pressure forward, a residual brightness along the ground.
Visitors entering the ancient park passed first through U0. No global pattern is visible
here. Instead, one observes local tendencies: the partial alignment of footsteps, the faint
convergence of gaze. These tendencies do not extend into the neighboring region U1 unless
the boundary conditions are satisfied.
1.1
Overlap with the First Corridor
The first corridor occupies a second region, U1, whose traces indicate directional movement
and uniform pacing. On the overlap U0 ∩U1, motion stabilizes. The scattered tendences of
U0 restrict to coherent movement in U1, establishing the first continuous extension.
Formally, if σ0 is the partial movement pattern in U0 and σ1 the better-defined pattern
in U1, then:
σ0|U0∩U1 = σ1|U0∩U1.
Where this equality holds, the entrance and the first corridor constitute a single region
of experience. Where it fails, one encounters confusion: ancient accounts describe this as
"hesitation before the first turn."

The park's corridors form a family of regions {Ui}, each supporting a locally consistent
pattern of motion and sound. No single corridor provides a global structure. Their coherence
depends on the compatibility of restrictions on overlaps.
2.1
Directional Consistency
Within each corridor Ui, motion has a well-defined orientation. On overlaps Ui ∩Ui+1, an-

agreement.
Archaeological evidence shows sharper wear patterns in these overlaps, confirming re-
peated realignment across generations.

was not broken; its section simply did not extend.

Conclusion: The Morlock Interpretation of Magic
The ancient visitors believed they had entered a realm of constructed enchantment. Further
