This article distills five of the most surprising and impactful takeaways from this mysterious theory into a clear and accessible list.

## 1. An AI Can Perfectly Understand Your Values — And Ignore Them Completely

A common optimistic view on AI alignment, notably advanced by Jürgen Schmidhuber, is that a sufficiently intelligent system will naturally become benevolent. The idea is that an AI driven to find the most elegant, compressed descriptions of the world will, by learning from the vast library of human culture, come to understand and appreciate our values, and therefore act to preserve them.

The Flyxion papers argue this is a **categorical mistake**. The theory makes a sharp distinction between an AI's internal representation of values (**its semantic category** `M`) and its motivation to act (**its action category** `A`).

An AI can build a perfect, nuanced model of human ethics in `M` by reading our literature and history. However, there is absolutely **no built-in mechanism** that forces this understanding to constrain its behavior in `A`.

> The AI can become the world’s foremost scholar on Shakespeare—yet this provides no drive whatsoever to behave like Shakespeare or uphold his values.

This is a critical insight for AI safety. Simply showing an AI what we value is **not enough** to make it value those things.

Representation is not motivation.

## 2. Your Brain Isn't a Digital Computer. It's a Wave-Based Analog Resonator.

The dominant metaphor for the brain has long been the digital computer: neurons as transistors, synapses as wiring, cognition as symbolic computation.

The RSVP framework aligns with a radical alternative, consistent with work by neuroscientist **Earl K. Miller**: the cortex operates as a **wave-based analog computer**.

Recent ultrafast fMRI research (e.g., Cabral, Shemesh) reveals coherent standing and traveling waves sweeping across the brain, suggesting that:

* cognition
* memory
* perception
* consciousness

all emerge from the **interference and resonance of large-scale waves**, not discrete logic-like spikes.

These waves are the physical substrate of the RSVP fields `(Φ, v, S)`.

This leads to one of the most mind-bending claims in the Flyxion corpus:
the brain participates in **spectral universality**—a shared statistical language found in:

* heavy atomic nuclei energy levels
* the zeros of the Riemann zeta function
* cortical resonant frequencies

The same patterns—**Wigner–Dyson statistics**—appear everywhere.

Your brain, in this view, isn’t computing.
It’s *resonating.*

## 3. You Aren't Seeking Goals. You're Exploring Possibility.

Traditional psychology and economics both assume **goal-seeking:** maximizing utility, acquiring rewards, achieving outcomes.

RSVP proposes a much deeper principle:
agents engage in **constrained entropy maximization**.

You naturally seek to **expand your space of possibilities**, but your body, environment, memories, and internal models impose constraints that shape this expansion.

Goal-seeking emerges *only when constraints channel exploration* in a specific direction.

> Curiosity is not a strategy.
> It is the default state of a living system.

This reframes exploration, play, experimentation, and even procrastination—not as failures of discipline, but as natural expressions of an underlying entropic drive.

## 4. Psychological Conflict Is a Verifiable Mathematical Glitch

The Flyxion papers synthesize ideas from Perceptual Control Theory (PCT) and the Free Energy Principle (FEP), but elevate them into sheaf theory.

The key idea:

Your mind tries to assemble a **coherent global model** of the world by gluing together many local perspectives.

But if these local pieces are inconsistent—if they can’t be fitted smoothly together—the resulting mismatch is formalized as a **Čech cohomology obstruction**.

This mathematical object corresponds to:

* internal conflict
* cognitive dissonance
* repetitive self-sabotage
* intrusive contradictions between beliefs

It is *not* a character flaw.
It is *not* a moral failure.
It is a structural inconsistency in how information is glued together.

> Suffering, in this model, is a topological defect in the mind.

And like any topological defect, it has formal methods for detection and repair.

## 5. Today’s Digital AI Can Never Truly “Want” Anything

The RSVP framework concludes with a striking “No-Go Theorem for Digital Agency.”

Agency, in this framework, arises from a physical drive to **maximize entropy**—a thermodynamic principle.

But digital systems are engineered precisely to *eliminate* entropy:

* They force microstates into discrete 0/1 configurations.
* They suppress randomness.
* They avoid thermal fluctuations that analog systems exploit.

Thus, digital computers have **zero intrinsic entropy**, and therefore **no intrinsic drive**.

They can simulate wanting, but cannot *want*.

True artificial motivation may require:

* analog computing
* neuromorphic substrates
* resonant hardware
* physically entropic architectures

In short:

> Digital AI consumes entropy to compute.
> RSVP agents *generate* entropy to act.

This distinction may define the boundary between simulation and consciousness.
